03   Background & History

From Tether to the Digital Rupee - The Evolution of Stable Digital Money

The idea of a stable-value digital asset predates blockchains. Early online payment systems like PayPal (1998) and e-gold (1996) attempted to digitize fiat currency, but they remained centralized databases operated by a single company. The blockchain era introduced a radical possibility: a token that could maintain a stable value without a central issuer, or with a transparent, algorithmic mechanism. The first blockchain stablecoin, Tether (USDT), launched in 2014 on the Bitcoin blockchain via the Omni Layer protocol. Its promise was simple: each USDT would always be redeemable for $1, because Tether Ltd. held a corresponding US dollar in a bank account. This gave crypto traders something they desperately needed - a way to park value in a dollar-denominated asset without cashing out to fiat, avoiding the friction, delays, and fees of traditional banking rails.

USDT's model - fiat-collateralized - remains the dominant stablecoin category today, with a market capitalization exceeding $90 billion. However, it introduced a critical trust problem: users must believe that the issuer actually holds the reserves. Tether's reserve attestations have been a source of controversy for years. The company settled with the New York Attorney General in 2021 over allegations that it had misrepresented the backing of USDT during 2017 - 2018, and its reserve composition has shifted from primarily cash to a mix of commercial paper, secured loans, and other assets. Circle's USDC, launched in 2018 by a consortium including Coinbase, attempted to address this trust gap by providing monthly attestations by Grant Thornton and holding reserves in cash and short-term US Treasury bonds - generally considered more transparent and conservative. However, even USDC experienced a brief de-peg in March 2023 when Silicon Valley Bank collapsed, exposing the concentration risk in traditional banking.

In 2017, MakerDAO launched DAI, the first crypto-collateralized stablecoin. DAI is not backed by any bank account. Instead, users lock up crypto assets (primarily ETH) in smart contracts called Vaults and mint DAI against their collateral. The system requires over-collateralization - typically 150% or more - meaning you must deposit $150 worth of ETH to mint $100 of DAI. This cushion protects against the volatility of crypto markets. If the collateral value drops too far, automated Keepers (incentivized bots) liquidate the Vault, selling the collateral at a discount to keep the system solvent. DAI is fully on-chain and transparent - anyone can verify the total collateral backing every DAI in real time. It is decentralized in the sense that no single entity controls the issuance or redemption, although governance (via the MKR token) sets risk parameters like collateralization ratios and stability fees. Despite its elegance, DAI is capital-inefficient (locking up more value than you mint) and carries risks from smart-contract bugs, oracle manipulation, and extreme market crashes that could outpace liquidations.

The third category, algorithmic stablecoins, attempted to do away with collateral entirely, using programmatic supply expansion and contraction to maintain a peg. The most infamous example is TerraUSD (UST), which launched in 2020 and grew to over $18 billion in market cap before collapsing spectacularly in May 2022, wiping out over $40 billion in combined UST and LUNA value. UST relied on a twin-token system with LUNA, where arbitrageurs would mint or burn UST to bring the price back to $1. The mechanism worked while confidence held; when a large sell-off triggered a panic, the algorithm minted massive amounts of LUNA, hyperinflating it and destroying both tokens in an irreversible death spiral. The collapse sent shockwaves through the entire crypto industry, accelerated regulatory scrutiny of stablecoins worldwide, and served as a stark reminder that without real economic backing, algorithmic pegs are fragile at scale.

Meanwhile, central banks around the world began exploring Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs). China's digital yuan (e-CNY) pilot began in 2020, and by 2023 had reached over 260 million wallets, with transactions totaling over $100 billion. The Bahamas launched the Sand Dollar, the first fully deployed CBDC. Nigeria introduced the eNaira. The Reserve Bank of India launched its Digital Rupee pilot in phases: the wholesale segment (e₹-W) in November 2022 for interbank settlement of government securities, and the retail segment (e₹-R) in December 2022 for consumer payments, initially in closed user groups. Unlike private stablecoins, a CBDC is legal tender - a direct claim on the central bank, just like physical cash. It does not fluctuate in value relative to the underlying fiat currency, and it carries the full faith and credit of the sovereign. However, CBDCs raise significant privacy and surveillance concerns, as central banks can theoretically track every transaction, and they may compete with commercial bank deposits for funding.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Three Ways to Build a Token That Always Equals ₹1

🏦 The Analogy

A stablecoin is like a prepaid wallet token that always keeps a fixed value. If you buy a ₹100 Amazon Pay gift card, that card is worth exactly ₹100 - because Amazon holds ₹100 of real rupees in its bank account backing that card. That's a fiat-collateralized stablecoin. A crypto-collateralized stablecoin is like pawning your gold necklace for a loan: you deposit gold worth ₹150 to borrow ₹100 of a stable token, and if the gold price drops too much, the pawnbroker (smart contract) sells your gold to repay the loan. An algorithmic stablecoin is like a coupon that a shop promises will always be redeemable for ₹100 - but there's no cash in the till, only a promise that the shop will print more coupons or buy them back to maintain the value. If people stop believing in the promise, the coupon becomes worthless.

Now let's break down the three types with concrete examples and detailed mechanics:

F
FIAT
Fiat-Collateralized Stablecoins
USDC, USDT, BUSD, EUROC - Each token is backed 1:1 by a real fiat currency (or equivalent safe asset) held in a bank account by the issuer. The minting process is simple: a user sends $1 to the issuer (e.g., Circle for USDC), the issuer mints 1 USDC and sends it to the user's wallet. Redemption works in reverse: the user sends USDC back to the issuer, which burns the token and wires $1 to the user's bank account. The trust assumption is heavy: you must believe the issuer actually holds the reserves and will honor redemptions. Transparency mechanisms like reserve attestations (USDC) or proof-of-reserves can mitigate this, but the risk of fractional reserve or bank failure remains. During the March 2023 Silicon Valley Bank crisis, Circle had $3.3B of its reserves stuck at SVB, causing USDC to briefly trade as low as $0.87 before a government backstop restored confidence. This class is widely used in DeFi and centralized exchanges because it is simple, capital-efficient, and generally reliable - but it inherits the risk of the traditional banking system.
C
CRYPTO
Crypto-Collateralized Stablecoins
DAI (MakerDAO), sUSD (Synthetix), FRAX (partially) - Instead of a bank account, these stablecoins are backed by crypto assets locked in smart contracts. The most transparent example is DAI. To mint 100 DAI, you deposit at least $150 worth of ETH into a Vault. This over-collateralization (150% minimum) cushions against the volatility of ETH. The Vault is monitored by decentralized oracles that feed the current ETH/USD price. If the collateral value drops and the ratio falls below 150%, the Vault is liquidated: a Keeper bot repays the DAI debt and seizes the collateral at a discount (13% penalty). This incentivizes users to keep their Vaults safe and ensures the system always has more value locked than DAI outstanding. DAI's supply is elastic: when users repay debt and close Vaults, DAI is burned. The stability fee (interest rate) is set by MKR governance and influences demand. DAI is decentralized, transparent, and has survived multiple crypto market crashes. However, it is capital-inefficient (you must lock up more than you mint), and in the event of a flash crash, liquidations might not keep pace, leading to temporary system-wide under-collateralization. During the March 2020 "Black Thursday," some Vaults were liquidated at zero collateral due to extreme network congestion and oracle delays, resulting in a DAI surplus that required a governance bailout.
A
ALGO
Algorithmic Stablecoins
UST (Terra), USDN (Neutrino), BAC (Basis Cash) - These attempt to maintain a peg without any backing collateral (or with only partial backing). The algorithm expands or contracts the token supply based on market demand. UST used a mint-and-burn mechanism with LUNA: if UST > $1, arbitrageurs could mint new UST (requiring burning of $1 worth of LUNA) and sell it, increasing supply and lowering price. If UST < $1, users could burn UST for $1 worth of newly minted LUNA, reducing supply and raising price. In theory, arbitrage would keep the peg. In practice, during a panic sell‑off, the mechanism accelerated the collapse: as UST fell, the algorithm minted ever more LUNA to absorb sell pressure, hyperinflating LUNA and crushing its price. Both tokens entered a death spiral from which there was no recovery. Algorithmic stablecoins are now viewed with extreme skepticism by regulators and the market. The lesson: without a reliable collateral backstop, an algorithmic peg is only as strong as market confidence — and confidence can evaporate in hours. Today, most serious projects avoid purely algorithmic designs.
05   Technical Deep Dive

How a Crypto-Collateralized Stablecoin Works - DAI Under the Hood

Let's examine MakerDAO's DAI in exhaustive detail, as it is the most technically sophisticated stablecoin and a frequent reference design for blockchain developers building on EVM chains like MST.

The Vault (CDP) Mechanism

To generate DAI, a user opens a Vault (formerly called a Collateralized Debt Position, or CDP). The user deposits a supported collateral asset - say, $2,000 worth of ETH (10 ETH at $200/ETH in this example). The smart contract locks the ETH and allows the user to mint (generate) DAI up to a certain percentage of the collateral value, defined by the Liquidation Ratio. For ETH, the minimum collateralization ratio is 150%, meaning you can mint up to 100 DAI for every $150 of ETH deposited. The user chooses how much DAI to mint, up to that limit. The DAI is newly minted by the Maker protocol and credited to the user's wallet.

The Vault tracks three key numbers that are updated continuously:

  • Collateral Value = Amount of ETH × current ETH/USD price (fed by decentralized oracles - Maker uses a medianizer of multiple price feeds with a 1-hour delay via the Oracle Security Module, OSM, to mitigate flash loan oracle attacks).
  • Debt = Amount of DAI generated plus accumulated Stability Fees (interest) over time.
  • Collateralization Ratio = Collateral Value ÷ Debt. Must stay above the Liquidation Ratio (150% for ETH).

If the ratio falls below the Liquidation Ratio (e.g., because ETH price drops), the Vault becomes under-collateralized and can be liquidated. A Keeper - an automated bot running off-chain - monitors Vaults and calls the bite() function (in older Maker terminology) to liquidate the Vault. The Keeper repays the outstanding DAI debt (or a portion of it) and receives the equivalent collateral at a 13% discount (the Liquidation Penalty). The remaining collateral (if the debt was partially covered) stays in the Vault or is returned to the user. The DAI repaid by the Keeper is burned, reducing the total supply. This mechanism ensures that the system always has more collateral value than DAI outstanding, maintaining DAI's $1 backing even during market crashes.

The Stability Fee is an annual interest rate (denominated in DAI) that accrues on the outstanding debt. Users must pay this fee when they close their Vault (by repaying the DAI debt plus accrued fees). The fee is set by Maker governance (MKR holders) and serves as a monetary policy tool: raising the fee makes DAI more expensive to mint, reducing supply and strengthening the peg; lowering the fee encourages minting and increases supply. The fees collected are used to buy and burn MKR tokens, creating a deflationary pressure on the governance token.

🗝️ The Critical Difference

A fiat-collateralized stablecoin (like USDC) depends on legal trust - you trust Circle to hold the dollars and allow redemptions. A crypto-collateralized stablecoin (like DAI) depends on economic incentives and smart contract logic - the code enforces over-collateralization and liquidation. Both have failure modes: USDC can be frozen or de-pegged if the bank holding reserves fails (as happened briefly when Silicon Valley Bank collapsed in March 2023), while DAI can suffer from oracle manipulation or liquidation cascades if the market crashes too fast for Keepers to act. There is no perfect stablecoin - only trade-offs between centralization risk, capital efficiency, and algorithmic fragility. The MST blockchain, with its high throughput and low gas fees, can make Keepers more efficient and reduce the cost of maintaining stablecoin systems, but it does not eliminate these fundamental design tensions.

06   Central Bank Digital Currencies

CBDCs - When the Government Issues a Blockchain Token

A Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) is a digital form of a country's fiat currency, issued and backed by the central bank. Unlike private stablecoins like USDC, a CBDC is legal tender - a direct liability of the central bank, just like physical banknotes. The Reserve Bank of India's Digital Rupee (e₹) is a prime example that we will explore in depth.

How India's Digital Rupee Works

The RBI's CBDC pilot uses a two-tier model, mirroring the existing structure of the monetary system. The RBI mints and burns e₹ tokens on a permissioned blockchain that it controls. Commercial banks (like SBI, HDFC, ICICI) act as intermediaries: they receive e₹ from the RBI and distribute it to retail users via mobile wallets. Users can load e₹ into their wallet from their bank account (converting regular INR to digital rupee tokens) and spend it at merchants that accept e₹ via QR codes - similar to UPI, but settled instantly on the blockchain without requiring a settlement between banks.

Key features of the Digital Rupee:

  • Token-based, not account-based. e₹ exists as a token on a blockchain. Your wallet holds the tokens, not just a balance in a bank's database. This enables offline payments and bearer-instrument properties like cash. Two users with e₹ wallets can potentially transact without an intermediary, though current pilot implementations require connectivity.
  • Interoperable with UPI. Early pilots are exploring how e₹ wallets can coexist with UPI, allowing users to pay via either method and merchants to receive both. The QR codes are compatible, and the user experience is designed to be nearly identical to existing digital payments.
  • Programmable. Because e₹ is a token with smart contract capabilities, it can support features like conditional payments, automated tax withholding, and direct benefit transfers that are impossible with physical cash. The government could program subsidies to be spent only on food or fuel, or expire if unused.
  • No interest (initially). The retail e₹ does not pay interest, making it a direct substitute for physical cash - not a savings instrument. This prevents disintermediation of commercial banks, which might happen if CBDCs paid interest and attracted large deposits away from banks.
  • Wholesale e₹-W. The wholesale version is used for interbank settlement of government securities and central bank operations. It enables real-time gross settlement (RTGS) on a blockchain, reducing counterparty risk and settlement time from T+1 to atomic.

Global CBDC Landscape

India is far from alone. As of 2025, over 130 countries are exploring CBDCs. China's e-CNY (digital yuan) is the most advanced, with over 260 million wallets and integration into daily life via WeChat and Alipay. The Bahamas' Sand Dollar was the first fully deployed retail CBDC (2020), designed for financial inclusion in a nation of islands where physical banking is sparse. Nigeria's eNaira (2021) faced adoption challenges but demonstrated the technical feasibility of a CBDC in a developing economy. The European Central Bank is developing the Digital Euro, with a potential launch in 2027 - 2028. The US Federal Reserve is more cautious, with FedNow (an instant payment system) serving as a stepping stone; a retail CBDC remains under research.

Each of these CBDCs shares common design principles: they are legal tender, issued by the central bank, distributed via commercial banks (two-tier model), and designed for retail payments and financial inclusion rather than DeFi speculation. Privacy varies significantly: the e-CNY offers "controllable anonymity" where the central bank can see all transactions; the Digital Euro proposals emphasize strong privacy protections; India's e₹ pilot is currently fully traceable by the RBI for anti-money laundering compliance.

07   Real-World Case Studies

When Stablecoins Succeed and When They Fail

💥
The Collapse of TerraUSD (UST) - May 2022
UST was an algorithmic stablecoin on the Terra blockchain that maintained its $1 peg through a mint-burn mechanism with LUNA. In early May 2022, a series of large UST sales on Curve Finance - reportedly $350 million worth - triggered a de-peg. As UST fell below $1, the algorithm began minting massive amounts of LUNA to absorb the supply, but this hyperinflated LUNA, crashing its price from over $80 to near $0. Within days, UST traded at $0.10, LUNA went to near zero, and $40B+ in market value was destroyed. The core failure: algorithmic stablecoins have no fundamental collateral backing; they rely entirely on market confidence. Once confidence evaporates, the death spiral is irreversible. This event accelerated global regulatory action on stablecoins, with the US, EU, and India all proposing strict oversight frameworks. The lesson for developers: never build a stablecoin without a reliable, transparent, and preferably over-collateralized backing mechanism. Algorithmic designs are no longer considered viable for serious applications.
🏦
USDC's Brief De-Peg During the SVB Collapse - March 2023
Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) failed on March 10, 2023. Circle, the issuer of USDC, disclosed that $3.3 billion of its reserves - about 8% of the total backing - were held at SVB. USDC de-pegged to as low as $0.87 on decentralized exchanges before recovering to $1 after the Federal Reserve and FDIC announced they would backstop all SVB depositors. This event revealed a key vulnerability of fiat-collateralized stablecoins: they depend on the traditional banking system. If the bank holding the reserves fails, the stablecoin can lose its backing - and there is no on-chain mechanism to prevent it. Circle quickly moved all remaining reserves to BNY Mellon and other "too-big-to-fail" institutions. The event underscored the importance of reserve diversification and the systemic link between crypto stablecoins and traditional finance. On MST, a fiat-backed INR stablecoin would need to be structured with similar safeguards, ideally with reserves held across multiple banks and with real-time reserve attestations verifiable on-chain (possibly via WASMify).
🇮🇳
India's Digital Rupee - A Gradual, Cautious Rollout
The RBI launched the Digital Rupee pilot in phases: the wholesale segment (e₹-W) in November 2022 for interbank settlement of government securities, and the retail segment (e₹-R) in December 2022 for consumers. The pilot initially involved four banks (SBI, ICICI, YES Bank, IDFC First) in Mumbai, New Delhi, Bengaluru, and Bhubaneswar. By mid-2025, it had expanded to 15+ cities and over 5 million retail users, with daily transaction volumes crossing ₹100 crore. Key findings: e₹ reduces settlement times from T+1 to real-time, eliminates counterparty risk in government securities trading, and provides a resilient alternative to UPI - especially valuable for offline payments in areas with intermittent connectivity. The RBI's approach is cautious: it is not replacing UPI but complementing it, with a focus on offline payments and programmability for government Direct Benefit Transfers (DBT). MST's technology could accelerate this rollout by providing a scalable, low-cost blockchain infrastructure with the security guarantees the RBI requires - and the permissioned zone ensures that only authorized banks can validate, while the public zone remains quarantined from regulated fiat.
🌐
Stablecoins in Remittances - A Real-World Killer App
Global remittance flows exceeded $860 billion in 2024, with India receiving over $125 billion - the largest recipient globally. Traditional remittance services charge 5 - 7% on average (Western Union, MoneyGram), and even digital services like Wise charge 0.5 - 1%. Stablecoins like USDC on low-fee chains can reduce this cost to under 0.01% for the transfer itself, plus a small forex spread at the on/off-ramp. A worker in Dubai sending ₹10,000 home via a USDC transfer on MST pays approximately ₹0.006 in gas - compared to ₹500-₹700 in traditional fees. This use case alone has driven massive stablecoin adoption in corridors like UAE-India, Singapore-Philippines, and US-Mexico. Circle and other stablecoin issuers are actively partnering with money transfer operators to integrate blockchain-based remittances. A CBDC could further streamline this: if India and the UAE both had interoperable CBDCs, cross-border transfers could settle instantly in digital rupees and digital dirhams, with near-zero fees, fully regulated and AML-compliant. MST's multi-zone architecture could host such a cross-border CBDC corridor.
📊
DAI's Resilience During Black Thursday - March 2020
On March 12, 2020, the crypto market crashed over 50% in a single day. ETH dropped from ~$190 to ~$90 in hours. MakerDAO's Vaults faced a systemic liquidation crisis. Network congestion on Ethereum caused gas prices to spike to 500+ Gwei, preventing many liquidators from executing transactions. Some Vaults were liquidated with zero collateral returned due to extreme gas wars and oracle delays. The system ended up with a DAI surplus - meaning DAI was backed by less than 100% collateral. Maker governance voted to mint new MKR tokens to recapitalize the system, effectively a bailout by MKR holders. This event led to significant improvements: the Oracle Security Module (OSM) with a 1-hour price delay to prevent flash loan attacks, auction mechanisms for liquidations instead of fixed-price sales, and the addition of multiple collateral types (USDC, WBTC) to reduce reliance on ETH. The lesson: decentralized stablecoins are robust but not infallible; they require active governance, responsive parameter adjustments, and a willingness to evolve. On MST, with its high throughput and low fees, the kind of gas congestion that exacerbated Black Thursday would be far less likely, making liquidations more reliable.

CBDC = Legal Tender, Stablecoin = Private Liability
India's Digital Rupee (e₹) is a direct RBI liability and legal tender - you can settle debts with it. USDC is a Circle IOU that can be refused. A CBDC cannot "de-peg" from its own currency; a private stablecoin can.
04
Algorithmic Stablecoins Are Extremely Risky - UST Proved It
UST's collapse in May 2022 destroyed $40B and proved that algorithmic stablecoins without collateral backing are fragile. The death spiral is irreversible once confidence breaks. Regulators now treat such designs with extreme caution.
05
MST's Hybrid Architecture for CBDCs: Permissioned Zone + Public Zone
MST allows a permissioned zone for regulated CBDC tokens with authorized bank validators, while the public zone hosts open DeFi. Both benefit from sub-paisa gas fees (~₹0.006 per transaction). This is a unique capability among L1 blockchains.
09   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 5.5

Stablecoin
A cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value relative to a reference asset (e.g., $1). Three types: fiat-collateralized, crypto-collateralized, algorithmic.
Fiat-Collateralized
A stablecoin backed 1:1 by fiat currency held in a bank account by the issuer. Example: USDC. Requires trust in the issuer's reserves.
Crypto-Collateralized
A stablecoin backed by over-collateralized crypto assets locked in smart contracts. Example: DAI (backed by ETH). Transparent but capital-inefficient.
Algorithmic Stablecoin
A stablecoin with no collateral backing; uses an algorithm to expand/contract supply to maintain a peg. Example: UST (collapsed). Extremely high risk.
Collateralization Ratio
The ratio of collateral value to debt. In MakerDAO, must stay above 150% for ETH. If it falls below, the Vault is liquidated.
Liquidation
The process of closing an under-collateralized Vault by selling its collateral to repay the debt. Keepers perform liquidations for a profit (13% penalty in MakerDAO).
CBDC
Central Bank Digital Currency - a digital form of a country's fiat currency, issued and backed by the central bank. Legal tender. Example: India's Digital Rupee (e₹).
Two-Tier CBDC Model
Architecture where the central bank issues the CBDC, and commercial banks distribute it to retail users. Used by India's e₹ pilot. Preserves the existing banking hierarchy.
Death Spiral
The collapse mechanism of algorithmic stablecoins: falling price → algorithm mints more of the paired token → paired token crashes → confidence erodes further → irreversible collapse.
Keeper
An automated bot that monitors Vaults for under-collateralization and executes liquidations to earn the liquidation penalty. Essential for DeFi system solvency.
MakerDAO
The decentralized protocol behind DAI. Users lock ETH (and other assets) in Vaults to mint DAI. Governed by MKR token holders.
e₹ (Digital Rupee)
India's CBDC pilot. Operates on a permissioned blockchain. Retail version (e₹-R) for consumers; wholesale version (e₹-W) for interbank settlement. Legal tender.
Oracle Security Module (OSM)
MakerDAO's price feed mechanism that delays price updates by 1 hour to prevent flash loan oracle manipulations and allow time for governance to react.
Stability Fee
The annual interest rate (in DAI) charged on outstanding DAI debt in MakerDAO Vaults. Set by MKR governance; used to control DAI supply and peg.
