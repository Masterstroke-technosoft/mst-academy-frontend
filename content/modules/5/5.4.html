02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Car Without Petrol - Why Tokens Strand Users

📖 Story

In 2022, a freelance graphic designer in Pune received 800 USDT for a project. She tried to send 200 USDT to her mother in Kolhapur. MetaMask displayed an error: “Insufficient ETH for gas.” She had $800 in tokens, but exactly 0 ETH. The transaction was impossible.


She had to buy ETH from an exchange, wait 30 minutes, and pay ₹300 in gas fees just to move her own stablecoin. The experience was bewildering. She owned the asset, but the blockchain demanded a different currency - its native coin - to process the transfer. This is not a wallet bug; it is an architectural law of every EVM blockchain: gas fees are paid exclusively in the native coin, never in tokens. Just as a car cannot run without fuel regardless of how many passengers are inside, a token cannot move without the native coin.

This sub-module explains why this happens, how gas is calculated at the opcode level, and why MST's native coin MSTC makes these fees nearly invisible - but never zero. We'll explore the economic incentives, the security model, and the real-world consequences of the coin-token-gas triad.

03   Background & History

The Evolution of Blockchain Transaction Fees

When Bitcoin launched in January 2009, there were no tokens. Bitcoin's ledger tracked only one asset: BTC. The fee model was straightforward - attach a small amount of BTC to a transaction, and a miner would include it in the next block. Fees were optional but practically necessary for timely inclusion. Miners prioritized transactions with higher fees, creating a simple fee market. The fee compensated miners for the electricity and hardware used to secure the network. Transaction size in bytes mattered more than computational complexity because Bitcoin's Script language was intentionally limited - no loops, no complex state changes, no Turing completeness.

In 2015, Ethereum introduced a radically different model. Vitalik Buterin envisioned a world computer - a Turing-complete blockchain where anyone could deploy arbitrary code. But Turing-completeness brought a critical problem: if a smart contract could run forever, an attacker could freeze the entire network with an infinite loop. The solution was gas.

Every operation on the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) - adding two numbers, storing a variable, calling another contract - was assigned a gas cost defined in the Ethereum Yellow Paper. Users set a gas limit (maximum units they'll pay for) and a gas price (how much native coin per unit). If a transaction runs out of gas, it reverts - but the validators keep the fee, because they already did the work. This economic incentive makes infinite loops financially impossible: the attacker's ETH balance drains long before the network halts.

Meanwhile, the ERC-20 token standard emerged in November 2015, authored by Fabian Vogelsteller. ERC-20 defined a common interface for fungible tokens on Ethereum: totalSupply(), balanceOf(address), transfer(address,uint256), approve(address,uint256), transferFrom(address,address,uint256), allowance(address,address), plus the Transfer and Approval events. This standard unleashed an explosion of tokens. By 2017, during the ICO boom, thousands of ERC-20 tokens were created. Users suddenly held diverse assets - USDT, BAT, OMG, ZRX - but every transfer of these tokens required ETH for gas. The coin-token distinction became a mass-market pain point, as millions of new users discovered they needed a separate asset just to move their holdings.

Ethereum's gas model evolved further with EIP-1559 in August 2021, which restructured fees into a base fee (burned, reducing ETH supply) and a priority fee (tip to validators). This improved fee estimation but did not reduce average costs. During periods of high demand - NFT mints, DeFi liquidations - gas prices still spike to 500+ Gwei. A simple ERC-20 token transfer that consumes 65,000 gas can cost over ₹8,000 at peak congestion.

MST Blockchain was designed from the ground up to address this exact pain point. With a throughput of 4,000+ transactions per second and the Trika POSA consensus mechanism, MST maintains stable gas prices around 10 - 12 Gwei. The native coin MSTC powers the network at a fraction of a paisa per token transfer, enabling micropayments, gaming, and everyday transactions that are economically impossible on high-fee chains.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

Coin, Token, Gas - The Highway, The Car & The Toll

🚦 The Analogy

Imagine the blockchain is a highway. The native coin (MSTC, ETH) is the only currency accepted at the toll booth. A token (USDT, an NFT, a governance token) is the passenger inside the car - the valuable cargo you want to move. The gas fee is the toll you pay to drive on the highway. You cannot pay the toll with the passenger's gold watch (the token); you must use the designated currency. If you show up at the toll plaza with a car full of gold but no rupees, you are not getting through. That's exactly why you need ETH to move USDT on Ethereum, and MSTC to move USDT on MST.

Now let's formalize the definitions with precise technical boundaries that will be tested:

C
COIN
What is a native coin?
A coin is the native asset of a blockchain, defined at the protocol level - not inside a smart contract. It is created through block rewards or staking incentives. Its balance is stored directly in the chain's world state trie as a simple account field. It is used to pay gas fees, reward validators, and secure the network through staking. Examples: BTC on Bitcoin, ETH on Ethereum, MSTC on MST. A coin exists independently of any application - it is the lifeblood of the chain itself.
T
TOKEN
What is a token?
A token is a digital asset created by a smart contract deployed on an existing blockchain. It does not have its own consensus mechanism; it inherits the host chain's security. Its balance is stored inside the contract's mapping(address => uint256). Tokens follow standards like ERC-20 (fungible), ERC-721 (non-fungible), or ERC-1155 (multi-asset) to ensure interoperability across wallets and dApps. Examples: USDT on MST, an MST-minted NFT, a DAO governance token. A token transfer is a smart contract function call, not a protocol-level operation.
G
GAS
What is gas?
Gas is the unit that measures computational effort on the EVM. Every opcode has a fixed gas cost. Total fee = gas used × gas price (denominated in Gwei, where 1 Gwei = 10⁻⁹ of the native coin). Gas prevents infinite loops, compensates validators, and prioritizes transactions. A native coin transfer costs 21,000 gas. An ERC-20 token transfer costs ~65,000 gas - regardless of the token amount. Whether you send 1 USDT or 1,000,000 USDT, the gas consumed is identical, because the computational steps (storage reads/writes) are exactly the same.
05   Technical Deep Dive

Coin vs Token - An EVM-Level Dissection

The difference between a native coin and a token is not superficial - it is embedded in the architecture of the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) and every EVM-compatible chain, including MST. Understanding this at the opcode level is essential for every blockchain developer.

How Native Coin Transfers Work

When you send 1 MSTC from Alice to Bob, the transaction does not invoke any smart contract. Instead, the EVM processes a built-in value transfer. The account state - stored in the world state trie as a simple balance field - is updated: Alice's balance decreases by 1 MSTC + gas fee; Bob's balance increases by 1 MSTC. The only opcodes involved are the base transaction cost (21,000 gas) and the two balance updates (essentially SSTORE operations on the account storage, but the base cost already covers simple value transfer overhead). No contract bytecode is executed. No external CALL is made. The transaction is simple, direct, and efficient.

How Token Transfers Work

Now consider sending 100 USDT - an ERC-20 token - from Alice to Bob. USDT is a smart contract deployed at a specific address on the blockchain. The token balance is not a native account field; it is stored inside the contract's persistent storage as mapping(address => uint256) public balanceOf;. To transfer tokens, Alice sends a transaction to the USDT contract address with calldata encoding the transfer(address to, uint256 amount) function call.

The EVM then executes the contract's transfer() function, which performs these operations step by step:

  1. SLOAD - Read Alice's current balance from contract storage. This is a storage read operation. If this storage slot was already accessed in the same transaction (warm access), it costs 100 gas. If it's the first access (cold access), it costs 2,100 gas because the node must verify a Merkle proof.
  2. GT, SUB, ISZERO, PUSH, JUMPI - The require(balance >= amount) check is compiled into a series of cheap arithmetic and control flow opcodes. These cost 3 gas each for arithmetic, and 10 gas for jumps. Total: approximately 30 - 50 gas.
  3. SSTORE - Write Alice's new balance (old balance minus amount). If Alice's balance slot was previously zero and is becoming non-zero, this costs 20,000 gas. If the slot was already non-zero and is being updated to another non-zero value, this costs 5,000 gas. If the balance is being set to zero (full transfer), it costs 5,000 gas plus a 15,000 gas refund at the end of the transaction.
  4. SSTORE - Write Bob's new balance (old balance plus amount). Same cost structure as above. If Bob previously had zero tokens, this is a 20,000 gas operation.
  5. LOG3 - Emit the Transfer(address indexed from, address indexed to, uint256 value) event. Events are logged in the transaction receipt and are crucial for off-chain indexing (wallets, explorers, dApps). The base cost is 375 gas. Each indexed parameter (topic) costs an additional 375 gas. The non-indexed data (the amount) costs 8 gas per byte. For a standard Transfer event with three indexed parameters (from, to are indexed; value is not), the total is approximately 375 + 375×2 + 8×32 = 1,381 gas. However, depending on compiler optimizations and the exact number of topics, it typically falls between 500 - 1,500 gas.

These additional opcodes push the total gas consumption to approximately 65,000 gas - roughly three times the cost of a native coin transfer. The dominant cost is the SSTORE writes, which persist data on-chain forever and are therefore priced accordingly to reflect their permanent storage burden on every full node.

🗝️ The Immutable Rule

Gas fees are ALWAYS paid in the blockchain's native coin. Never in USDT. Never in an NFT. Never in any ERC-20, ERC-721, or ERC-1155 token. This is enforced at the protocol level. Even if a dApp offers "gasless" transactions through meta-transactions or a paymaster, a relayer is still paying the native coin behind the scenes. The economic layer does not disappear - it is merely abstracted. The validators must be compensated for their work, and that compensation is always in the coin that secures the network.

Gas fees are per transaction, not per token. Whether you send 0.01 USDT or 1,000,000 USDT, the gas consumed is nearly identical because the computational steps - reading two storage slots, writing two storage slots, emitting one event - do not depend on the numeric value of the amount. Sending a million USDT does not require a million times more gas; it requires exactly the same gas as sending one USDT. The fee scales with network computation, not with value transferred. This is why gas fees are a fixed overhead - and why low-fee chains like MST unlock micro-transactions that are impossible on Ethereum.

Understanding gas pricing requires three layers: (1) the gas cost per operation as defined in the Yellow Paper, (2) the gas limit and gas price set by the user, and (3) the fiat conversion of the native coin. Let's examine each layer in detail.

Layer 2: Gas Limit & Gas Price - The User-Controlled Levers

Every transaction includes two critical parameters:

  • Gas Limit (startGas): The maximum gas units the sender is willing to consume. If the transaction needs more gas than the limit, it fails and all gas up to the limit is still consumed - the validators did the work. For a native coin send, 21,000 is sufficient. For an ERC-20 transfer, a safe limit is 80,000 - 100,000 to account for variations (cold storage slots, complex token logic like deflationary tokens that burn or reflect).
  • Gas Price: The amount of native coin the sender pays per gas unit, measured in Gwei (1 Gwei = 10⁻⁹ of the native coin, so 1 ETH = 1,000,000,000 Gwei). Validators prioritize transactions with higher gas prices because they earn more for including them. On congested networks like Ethereum, gas prices can spike to 500+ Gwei as users outbid each other for block space. On MST, high throughput means block space is rarely contested, so gas prices remain stable at ~10 - 12 Gwei.

Layer 3: Fiat Conversion - The Real Cost to Users

The total fee in native coin = Gas Used × Gas Price. To convert to INR:

Fee (INR) = (Gas Used × Gas Price in Gwei × 10⁻⁹) × (Coin Price in INR)

⚠️ Common Misconceptions About Gas

Myth 1: "Gas fees are a percentage of the amount transferred." False. Gas cost depends on computational steps, not value. Sending ₹10 costs the same gas as sending ₹10,00,000.
Myth 2: "If I run out of gas, my money is lost." Partially false. The transaction reverts, so no value is transferred, but you still pay the gas fee for the computation that was performed. Always set a sufficient gas limit.
Myth 3: "MST transactions are free." False. They are extremely cheap (~₹0.006 per token transfer), but validators are still compensated in MSTC for their work. The economic incentive layer remains intact - it's just scaled to sub-paisa levels.
Myth 4: "A token transfer costs less gas if I send a smaller amount." False. The gas is per transaction, not per token. The computational steps (storage reads, writes, event emission) are identical regardless of the numeric value of the amount.

08   Real-World Case Studies

When Gas Makes or Breaks Applications

💸
The $4 Million Gas Bill - Otherdeed NFT Mint (May 2022)
During Yuga Labs' Otherdeed land sale, buyers collectively paid over $4 million in ETH gas fees in a single day just to mint virtual land parcels. The minted NFTs were ERC-721 tokens, but every mint required ETH for gas. Many users had budgeted ETH for the mint price (~$6,000 worth of APE tokens) but forgot to reserve ETH for gas. Their transactions failed - and the gas fees were still consumed. Some individuals lost $3,000+ in failed transactions. On MST, a similar high-demand NFT mint would cost a few paise per transaction. Failed mints would be economically trivial, and users wouldn't need to hold a second asset just to participate. This is why gaming and NFT projects are migrating to low-fee L1s like MST.
🎮
Axie Infinity - When Gas Killed a Game Economy
Axie Infinity, the pioneering play-to-earn game, launched on Ethereum in 2018. Breeding two Axies (NFTs) required multiple smart contract calls - approving the marketplace, initiating the breed, claiming the egg. These operations consumed 200,000 - 500,000 gas per breed, costing $50-$150 at typical gas prices - often exceeding the market value of the resulting Axie. Players were spending more on gas than they earned. In 2021, Sky Mavis migrated the entire game to a custom sidechain (Ronin) with its own native coin (RON) and negligible fees. The lesson: a game economy cannot survive on a high-fee chain. MST's gaming-focused design (with protocols for fair randomness and provable shuffles) provides a native, low-fee environment - no sidechain migration required.
🏦
India's Digital Rupee (e₹) - A CBDC Token on a Permissioned Lane
The Reserve Bank of India's Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) pilot issues the Digital Rupee as a token on a permissioned blockchain. Banks run smart contracts to mint and burn e₹, and the token circulates among retail users and merchants. However, the underlying blockchain still has a native coin that pays validators for processing transactions and maintaining the network. MST's hybrid architecture - with public and permissioned zones - is ideally suited for this model: the e₹ token lives in a permissioned zone with regulatory compliance, while MSTC silently pays the validator fees in the background. Retail users never see MSTC; they simply spend e₹ as easily as UPI. The coin-token-gas architecture is preserved underneath, but the user experience is seamless.
🌐
Micro-Lending & Remittances - The Impact of Sub-Paisa Fees
Consider a migrant worker in Mumbai sending ₹500 home to his family in Bihar every week via a USDC remittance dApp. On Ethereum, a ₹760 gas fee makes this impossible - the fee exceeds the amount being sent. On BNB Chain, a ₹10 fee is still 2% of the transfer - comparable to traditional remittance services like Western Union. On Polygon, ₹0.26 is excellent but can spike during congestion. On MST, a ₹0.006 fee is 0.0012% of the transfer - effectively invisible. This enables true micro-lending (loans of ₹200), per-stream royalty payments to artists (₹0.50 per play), automated daily savings of small amounts (₹10), and machine-to-machine payments for IoT devices. Low gas fees are not a luxury; they are a prerequisite for financial inclusion and the next wave of Web3 adoption.
📊
DeFi Liquidation Cascades - When Gas Costs Cause Systemic Risk
In decentralized lending protocols like Aave and Compound, positions are liquidated when collateral value falls below a threshold. Liquidators compete to repay the debt and claim the collateral at a discount. On Ethereum, during market crashes, gas prices spike to 500+ Gwei as liquidators race to submit transactions. In several instances, liquidations failed because the gas cost exceeded the liquidation profit - leaving underwater positions unclosed and causing bad debt for the protocol. On a low-fee chain like MST, gas costs are never a barrier to timely liquidations. The protocol remains solvent even in extreme market conditions. This demonstrates that gas economics are not just a UX issue - they directly impact the security and solvency of DeFi protocols.

Which factor primarily explains why the same token transfer costs ₹760 on Ethereum but ₹0.006 on MST?
  • A Ethereum uses more gas units for the same operation.
  • B The gas price (in Gwei) and the native coin's fiat value are vastly different between the two chains. ✅
  • C MST validators work for free, eliminating the need for gas.
  • D Ethereum deliberately sets higher gas limits for token transfers.
✅ Correct: B

The gas units consumed are identical (~65,000) because both chains use the same EVM opcode pricing. The fiat cost difference comes from gas price (45 Gwei on congested Ethereum vs 12 Gwei on MST) and the coin's INR value (₹2,60,000/ETH vs ₹8/MSTC).

Section B - True / False with Mandatory Justification
2 Questions · 2 marks each · 4 marks total
Q6
True/False + JustificationMedium2 marks
"If a dApp on MST uses a relayer to pay gas fees on behalf of users, the transactions become genuinely free - no native coin is consumed at all."
FALSE
📝 Sample Justification - Write something like this to score 2/2:
This statement is FALSE. Even when a relayer pays gas on behalf of the user, the relayer is still spending MSTC - the native coin - to compensate validators for processing the transaction. The validators perform real computational work (SSTORE, SLOAD, LOG3) and must be paid in the native coin to maintain the economic security of the network. The gas fee is not eliminated; it is shifted from the end user to the relayer. The cost is real; only the UX is abstracted. If transactions were genuinely free, an attacker could flood the network with infinite spam at zero cost - exactly the problem gas was invented to prevent. Gas abstraction is a UX improvement, not an elimination of gas economics.
📊 Marks Breakdown

1 mark: Correctly states FALSE.
+1 mark: Justification explains that validators still receive MSTC for their work, the economic layer persists, and that truly free transactions would break the network's denial-of-service protection. Must mention that gas is still consumed by the relayer.

Q7
True/False + JustificationMedium2 marks
"Sending 1,000,000 USDT in a single transaction costs significantly more gas than sending 1 USDT, because the network charges a fee proportional to the value transferred."
FALSE
📝 Sample Justification - Write something like this to score 2/2:
This statement is FALSE. Gas fees are per transaction, not per token and are determined solely by the computational steps required - not by the value transferred. The ERC-20 transfer() function performs the same operations regardless of the amount: it reads the sender's balance (SLOAD), subtracts the amount using simple arithmetic (SUB, 3 gas), updates storage (SSTORE), and emits an event (LOG3). Whether the amount is 1 USDT or 1,000,000 USDT, the number of storage reads and writes is identical, so the gas consumed (~65,000 gas) is the same. The fee scales with network computation, not with economic value. A 1,000,000 USDT transfer costs the same ₹0.006 on MST as a 1 USDT transfer.
📊 Marks Breakdown

1 mark: Correctly states FALSE.
+1 mark: Justification explains that gas depends on opcode execution (SLOAD, SSTORE count), which is constant regardless of the token amount. Must explicitly state that gas is per transaction, not proportional to value, and give a concrete example of identical cost for different amounts.

Key Terms - Sub-Module 5.4

Native Coin
The primary asset of a blockchain, defined at the protocol level. Used for gas fees, staking, and validator rewards. On MST: MSTC. Stored in the world state trie, not in a smart contract.
Token
A digital asset created by a smart contract on an existing blockchain. Follows standards like ERC-20, ERC-721, ERC-1155. Cannot pay gas fees directly. Its balance is stored in the contract's internal mapping.
Gas
Unit measuring computational effort on the EVM. Total fee = gas used × gas price. Prevents infinite loops, compensates validators, and prioritizes transactions. Fee is per transaction, not per token amount.
Gas Limit
Maximum gas a user authorizes for a transaction. Standard: 21,000 (coin send), 65,000 - 100,000 (ERC-20 transfer). If exceeded, the transaction fails but all gas up to the limit is still consumed.
Gwei
1 Gwei = 10⁻⁹ of the native coin. Gas prices are quoted in Gwei. Example: 20 Gwei = 0.000000020 ETH. Named after Claude Shannon, the father of information theory.
SSTORE
EVM opcode for writing to persistent storage. Costs 20,000 gas (new slot) or 5,000 gas (update). The most expensive common opcode because it permanently expands the blockchain state.
SLOAD
EVM opcode for reading from storage. Costs 100 gas (warm, already accessed) or 2,100 gas (cold, first access). Used in every token balance check during transfers.
EIP-1559
Ethereum Improvement Proposal implemented in August 2021. Restructured gas fees into a base fee (burned) and a priority fee (tip). Improved estimation but did not reduce average costs significantly.
Gas Abstraction
UX pattern where a relayer or paymaster pays gas on behalf of the user. Gas is still consumed in the native coin; only the payer changes. The economic layer remains intact.
MSTC
The native coin of MST Blockchain. Pays for all computation, secures Trika POSA consensus through staking, and serves as the network's economic foundation. Used to pay gas for all transactions.
World State Trie
The Merkle Patricia Trie data structure storing all account balances and contract storage in an EVM blockchain. Native coin balances are stored here directly, not inside contracts.
Relayer
A service that submits transactions on behalf of users, paying gas in the native coin. Enables gasless UX while preserving on-chain economics and network security.
Meta-Transaction
A transaction signed by the user but submitted and paid for by a relayer. The user's signature authorizes the action; the relayer pays the gas. Enables dApps to subsidize user gas costs.
Gas Station Network (GSN)
A decentralized network of relayers that allows dApps to pay gas on behalf of their users. Similar to meta-transactions but with a standardized protocol for relayer compensation.
