02   Real-World Introduction Story

The DAO That Was Captured by a Single Whale

📖 Story

In 2022, a small DeFi protocol's DAO had a simple governance rule: 1 token = 1 vote. It felt democratic. Anyone could buy tokens and vote. A proposal came up to divert treasury funds to a "community initiative" that would supposedly boost the protocol's growth. A whale who had recently accumulated 55% of the tokens on the open market voted in favor. The proposal passed. The treasury was drained into a contract controlled by the whale's team. The funds were never seen again. The DAO was devastated.


This was a textbook governance attack - also called a plutocracy exploit. Simple token voting had allowed wealth to buy control. The community later learned about quadratic voting, where voting power is the square root of tokens held, making it exponentially expensive to dominate. They migrated to a conviction voting model, where votes accumulate over time, preventing flash attacks. And they implemented delegation, where experts could be elected to represent passive holders. This sub-module teaches you all these models so your DAO never suffers the same fate.

03   Background & History

From Athenian Democracy to Quadratic Voting - The Quest for Fair Governance

The question of how to make collective decisions fairly has haunted humanity for millennia. Ancient Athens experimented with direct democracy - every citizen voted on every issue. It was fair but impossibly slow at scale. Representative democracy solved scale by electing officials, but introduced principal-agent problems: representatives often served their own interests, not the people's.

Blockchain DAOs inherited this challenge in a new form. The default became token-weighted voting: hold more tokens, cast more votes. It was simple to implement (a smart contract literally reads your balance), but it immediately created a system where the rich ruled. As DAOs grew, the flaws became visible. A 2022 study by Snapshot Labs found that in the average DAO, the top 1% of token holders controlled over 70% of the voting power. Most proposals passed with fewer than 10% of tokens voting. Governance had become a spectator sport.

In response, researchers and developers began adapting political science models to the blockchain. Quadratic voting, proposed by economist Glen Weyl, made each additional vote exponentially more expensive in tokens. Liquid democracy allowed delegation to trusted experts. Conviction voting, pioneered by the 1Hive project, made voting power accumulate over time, favoring long-term commitment over flash purchases. Reputation-based voting tied voting power to contributions, not capital. Each model tackled a different weakness of the simple 1-token-1-vote baseline.

On MST, with its sub-paisa gas fees, all these sophisticated models become practical. On Ethereum, quadratic voting might cost ₹500 per vote - only the rich participate. On MST, it costs ₹0.006 - true democratic participation is finally economically viable.

Key insight: There is no perfect governance model. Each trades off fairness, efficiency, and security differently. The skill is choosing the right model - or combination of models - for your DAO's specific purpose and community.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

Voting Power = The Recipe for Your DAO's Cake

🎂 The Analogy

Imagine your DAO is baking a cake, and the recipe is decided by vote. In simple token voting, whoever bought the most ingredients (tokens) decides the recipe - the sugar tycoon always wins. In quadratic voting, each additional bag of sugar you buy gives you less voting power - so no one person can dominate. In delegation, if you don't know much about baking, you can assign your vote to a master chef. In conviction voting, you can't just show up with a truckload of sugar and win - you have to keep adding it day after day, showing you really care about the cake. Each model changes who gets to decide the recipe.

Let's explore the six major models in depth:

05   Model 1 - Simple Token-Weighted Voting

1 Token = 1 Vote - The Default, and Its Dangers

This is the simplest and most common DAO governance model. Your voting power is directly proportional to the number of governance tokens you hold (or have delegated to you). If Alice holds 1,000 tokens and Bob holds 100 tokens, Alice has 10× Bob's voting power.

How It Works

The smart contract reads your token balance at a specific block number (the "snapshot") and assigns you that many votes. You can vote For, Against, or Abstain on any active proposal. If the number of For votes exceeds a threshold (e.g., >50% of total votes cast, or >quorum), the proposal passes.

Strengths

  • Simple to implement: OpenZeppelin's GovernorCountingSimple does this out of the box.
  • Capital-aligned: Those with the most financial stake have the most say - they have "skin in the game."
  • Liquid: Tokens can be bought and sold on the open market, making governance participation fluid.

Weaknesses

  • Plutocracy: Wealthy token holders can dominate, buying control on the open market. A single whale can pass proposals that benefit them at the expense of the community.
  • Voter apathy: Small holders feel their vote doesn't matter. Most tokens never vote. A 2023 study found average DAO voter turnout was just 8%.
  • Governance attacks: An attacker can borrow tokens (via flash loan) just before a snapshot, vote, and immediately sell - manipulating governance without long-term commitment.

06   Model 2 - Quadratic Voting

Making It Expensive to Dominate

Quadratic voting (QV) is a radical departure: instead of voting power being linear with tokens, it's the square root. If Alice holds 100 tokens, she gets √100 = 10 votes. If she holds 10,000 tokens, she gets √10,000 = 100 votes. To double her voting power, she needs 4× the tokens. To triple it, she needs 9×. This makes it progressively more expensive to accumulate outsized influence, while still giving larger stakeholders more say than smaller ones.

Strengths

  • Reduces whale dominance: A whale with 55% of tokens gets √55 ≈ 7.4 votes out of √100 = 10 total - far less than 55%.
  • Encourages broad participation: Small holders' votes carry more weight relative to their token holdings, incentivizing them to vote.
  • Resists governance attacks: Buying tokens for a flash attack is far less effective when each additional token gives diminishing returns.

Weaknesses

  • Sybil attacks: A whale could split tokens across many wallets, each getting √N votes, circumventing the quadratic cost. This requires identity verification to prevent.
  • Complexity: Harder to implement and explain to users. Not natively supported by OpenZeppelin; requires custom contracts.
  • Capital disincentive: Reduces the benefit of holding many tokens, which may discourage large investors from supporting the DAO.

07   Model 3 - Delegation / Liquid Democracy

Vote Yourself, or Send a Representative

Liquid democracy combines the best of direct and representative democracy. Token holders can vote directly on any proposal, or they can delegate their voting power to a trusted representative - an expert, a community leader, or a friend. Delegation can be revoked at any time. This solves a key problem: most token holders lack the time or expertise to evaluate every proposal, but they still want their voice to count.

How It Works

The governance token contract (e.g., OpenZeppelin's ERC20Votes) includes a delegate(address) function. When Alice delegates to Bob, Bob's voting power increases by Alice's token balance. Alice can vote directly on any proposal, which overrides the delegation for that specific vote (in some implementations). Delegation chains can form: Alice → Bob → Carol, where Carol ends up with the combined power.

Strengths

  • Expertise: Complex proposals can be evaluated by experts who have time and knowledge, not just capital.
  • Inclusivity: Small holders can pool their votes via delegation, collectively matching the influence of a whale.
  • Flexibility: You can vote on issues you care about and delegate the rest. Delegation can be changed at any time.

Weaknesses

  • Delegate capture: If a few delegates accumulate most of the power, the DAO effectively becomes an oligarchy.
  • Lazy delegation: Users may delegate to popular figures without understanding their positions.
  • Whale delegates: A whale who is also a delegate can concentrate enormous power - their own tokens plus delegated tokens.

08   Model 4 - Conviction Voting

Prove You Care by Locking Your Tokens Over Time

Conviction voting, pioneered by the 1Hive project for Aragon DAOs, is a radical model where voting power is not based on your token balance, but on how long you've locked your tokens in favor of a proposal. When a proposal is created, token holders can stake their tokens on it. Their voting power starts at zero and grows over time according to a curve. The longer they keep their tokens staked on the proposal, the more conviction they accumulate. If enough conviction accumulates to pass a threshold, the proposal can be executed.

The Conviction Curve

Conviction(t) = Tokens Staked × (1 - e^(-λ·t))

Where t is the time staked, and λ (lambda) is a rate parameter. The conviction approaches the staked amount asymptotically - it never exceeds it, but grows quickly at first and then levels off.

Key Features

  • No fixed voting period: Proposals stay open indefinitely. Anyone can stake at any time.
  • Tokens can be withdrawn: If a user changes their mind, they can unstake their tokens from a proposal, losing their accumulated conviction.
  • Execution threshold: A proposal passes when total conviction exceeds a threshold (e.g., 10% of total token supply).

Strengths

  • Resists flash attacks: An attacker cannot buy tokens and immediately pass a proposal - they'd need to stake for weeks or months.
  • Signals commitment: Only those who truly care about a proposal will lock tokens for an extended period.
  • Continuous: No "voting deadline" pressure. The community can deliberate indefinitely.

Weaknesses

  • Slow: Important decisions may take weeks to reach the conviction threshold.
  • Capital opportunity cost: Staking tokens on a proposal means they can't be used elsewhere (e.g., in DeFi). This may discourage participation unless the DAO provides yield.
  • Whales still have advantage: A whale staking many tokens will reach the threshold faster than many small holders combined.

09   Model 5 - Reputation-Based Voting

Power from Contribution, Not Capital

Reputation-based voting breaks the link between tokens and voting power entirely. Instead, voting power is earned through contributions to the DAO - completing tasks, receiving peer endorsements, or holding non-transferable reputation tokens (sometimes called "soulbound tokens" or SBTs). This model is ideal for DAOs where the goal is to reward active participants, not passive investors.

How It Works

  • Non-transferable reputation tokens: Minted for specific achievements (e.g., "Completed 5 code reviews", "Served as moderator for 6 months"). Cannot be bought or sold.
  • Peer endorsement: Community members vouch for each other, similar to how academic citations work. Your reputation score increases when trusted members endorse you.
  • Contribution tracking: Platforms like Coordinape and SourceCred calculate reputation scores from GitHub commits, forum posts, and meeting attendance.

Strengths

  • Meritocratic: Those who actually do the work have the most say, not those who simply bought tokens.
  • Sybil-resistant: Non-transferable tokens can't be accumulated by a single attacker across multiple wallets.
  • Community-aligned: Voting power reflects genuine commitment and expertise, aligning governance with long-term success.

Weaknesses

  • Subjective: Who decides what counts as a valuable contribution? The reputation system itself becomes a governance challenge.
  • Inertia: Early contributors accumulate reputation and may become entrenched, making it hard for newcomers to gain influence.
  • Complexity: Harder to implement than simple token voting. Requires off-chain tracking or sophisticated on-chain mechanisms.
10   Model 6 - Holographic Consensus

Let the Crowd Filter Proposals Before the DAO Votes

Holographic consensus, pioneered by DAOstack, addresses a practical problem: as a DAO grows, the number of proposals can become overwhelming. Most token holders can't evaluate 50 proposals a week. Holographic consensus introduces a prediction market layer: a small group of "predictors" stake tokens on which proposals they believe will pass. The proposals that accumulate enough stake become "boosted" and get attention from the full DAO. This acts as a scalable attention filter.

How It Works

  1. A proposal is submitted to the DAO.
  2. Anyone can stake tokens to "boost" the proposal - effectively betting that it will pass.
  3. If enough tokens are staked (reaching a threshold), the proposal becomes "boosted" and goes to a full DAO vote.
  4. If the proposal passes, the boosters are rewarded. If it fails, they lose their stake. This incentivizes careful selection.

Strengths

  • Scalable: The full DAO only votes on proposals that have passed the booster filter, reducing voter fatigue.
  • Economic incentives align: Boosters put skin in the game, so they only boost proposals they genuinely believe in.
  • Attention economy: Helps surface high-quality proposals in a sea of noise.

Weaknesses

  • Booster capture: A wealthy group could boost only their own proposals, gatekeeping the DAO.
  • Complexity: Adds another layer to governance, which can confuse participants.
  • Still experimental: Few DAOs use this model in production compared to simpler alternatives.

12   Combining Models

The Best DAOs Mix and Match

In practice, the most successful DAOs combine multiple models. For example:

  • Uniswap DAO: Simple token voting + delegation. Any UNI holder can vote or delegate. The combined power of small holders delegating to informed representatives has successfully passed major proposals.
  • 1Hive / Gardens: Conviction voting for treasury spending + quadratic voting for membership decisions. This ensures that routine spending (grants) has a continuous, deliberate process, while membership changes require broader consensus.
  • Gitcoin DAO: Quadratic funding for grant allocation (a variant of quadratic voting applied to funding, not just voting) + simple token voting for protocol parameters.
  • MST-based DAOs (future): Could combine conviction voting for treasury (slow, deliberate) with quadratic voting for emergency proposals (fast, whale-resistant), all running on sub-paisa fees.
MST Advantage: On Ethereum, combining multiple governance models (e.g., quadratic + conviction) would be prohibitively expensive due to complex on-chain calculations. On MST, you can run quadratic voting, conviction curves, and delegation all on-chain for paise per vote. This enables rich, multi-layered governance that's actually used.

🗳️ Example 1: Calculate Quadratic Voting Power

Scenario: A DAO has 10,000 total tokens. Alice holds 1,000 tokens. Bob holds 100 tokens. What is their quadratic voting power?

Alice: √1,000 ≈ 31.6 votes

Bob: √100 = 10 votes

Ratio: Alice has 3.16× Bob's power in QV, versus 10× in simple voting. QV compresses the gap by a factor of √10 ≈ 3.16.

⏳ Example 2: Conviction Voting - How Long Until a Proposal Passes?

Scenario: A DAO uses conviction voting with threshold = 500 conviction units. Alice stakes 100 tokens. The conviction function is Conviction(t) = Tokens × (1 - e^(-0.1·t)). How many days until her conviction reaches 500?

We need 100 × (1 - e^(-0.1·t)) = 500 → 1 - e^(-0.1·t) = 5 → impossible - conviction can never exceed the staked amount. Alice's conviction asymptotically approaches 100. To reach 500, she'd need to stake at least 500 tokens, or attract more stakers. This illustrates that conviction voting requires significant collective commitment - a single user can't pass a proposal alone unless they stake above the threshold.

🔗 Example 3: Delegation Chain Power Accumulation

Scenario: Alice delegates 500 tokens to Bob. Bob delegates 300 tokens to Carol. Carol holds 200 tokens. What is Carol's total voting power on a simple token-voting DAO?

Carol's total = 200 (own) + 300 (Bob's, which includes Alice's via Bob) = 500 votes. However, the chain is: Carol receives Bob's 300 (which already includes Alice's 500? No - Bob only has 300 of his own plus the delegated 500 from Alice = 800. Wait, re-read: Bob delegates 300 to Carol. Bob's total = 300 (own) + 500 (Alice) = 800. He delegates only 300 to Carol. So Carol gets 200 + 300 = 500. This shows how delegation amounts are explicit, not automatic cascading of all delegated power.

14   Common Pitfalls in Governance Design

Don't Let Your DAO Become a Cautionary Tale

⚠️
Choosing a model based on trend, not need
Quadratic voting is trendy, but if your DAO has 50 members who all know each other, simple voting with delegation works perfectly. Pick the simplest model that solves your actual problems.
⚠️
Ignoring the quorum problem
If quorum is set too high, no proposals ever pass. If set too low, a single active voter can control the DAO. Quorum should be set based on historical participation rates, and adjusted as the DAO grows.
⚠️
No emergency mechanism
Every DAO needs a way to act quickly in a crisis. A multisig of trusted community members for emergency actions, with a time-limited scope and community override, is a common and prudent pattern.
