02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Proposal That Almost Drained the Treasury - Until the Timelock Saved It

📖 Story

In 2022, a medium-sized DeFi DAO received a proposal: "Upgrade the staking contract to v2." The proposer had written the new contract, and the community was excited. The vote passed with 85% approval. But a sharp-eyed community member, while reviewing the code during the mandatory 48-hour timelock, discovered a hidden function that would allow the proposer to drain the entire staking pool. The community panicked. The DAO's multisig signers quickly canceled the proposal before it could execute. The treasury was safe. The timelock - that seemingly bureaucratic delay - had saved the DAO.


This story illustrates why a DAO proposal isn't just "vote and done." It's a carefully designed pipeline of checks, delays, and execution controls. In this sub-module, you'll learn every stage of that pipeline, from the moment an idea is posted on a forum to the instant a smart contract executes the community's will - all powered by OpenZeppelin's battle-tested contracts, and made affordable by MST's sub-paisa fees.

03   Background & History

From Paper Ballots to Smart Contract Execution - The Evolution of DAO Governance

In traditional organizations, a proposal follows a familiar path: someone writes a memo, it circulates for comments, a committee votes, and if approved, an executive implements it. The process relies on trust in the executives. In a DAO, the smart contract IS the executive. This required building a complete governance machine on-chain.

Early DAOs (2016-2018) used custom, often buggy, voting contracts. Each DAO built its own, leading to fragmentation and security incidents. The breakthrough came when OpenZeppelin released its Governor framework in 2021, inspired by Compound's Governor Alpha and Bravo. It provided a modular, audited set of contracts that handled the entire proposal lifecycle: creating proposals, casting votes, counting results, queuing in a timelock, and executing. DAOs could inherit these contracts, configure parameters, and focus on their community rather than reinventing governance.

Key milestones in the evolution of on-chain proposal execution:

  • 2016 - The DAO: First major DAO. Proposals were simple: invest ETH in projects. No timelock, no quorum - anyone could propose, and token holders voted directly.
  • 2020 - Compound Governor Alpha: Introduced the modern lifecycle: proposal threshold, voting delay, voting period, and execution. Became the blueprint.
  • 2021 - OpenZeppelin Governor: Modular, extensible version of Compound's model. Added support for timelocks, quorum fractions, and multiple voting styles.
  • Today - MST-native DAOs: Full EVM compatibility means OpenZeppelin Governor works out of the box on MST. Sub-paisa gas lets DAOs run fully on-chain governance without pricing out small holders.
Key insight: A DAO proposal is a state machine. Each stage has specific rules, actors, and outcomes. Understanding this machine is the foundation of building and auditing governance systems.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Seven Steps of a Proposal - Like a Bill Becoming a Law

🏛️ The Analogy

A DAO proposal is like a bill becoming a law. First, someone writes a draft (the proposal). It's introduced to the parliament (submitted on-chain). There's a mandatory reading period (voting delay). Then parliament debates and votes (voting period). If it passes, the bill goes to the president for review (timelock). If the president doesn't veto, it becomes law (execution). In a DAO, the "president" is a timelock smart contract - a mandatory waiting period that gives everyone time to exit if they disagree. After the wait, anyone can trigger execution, and the code runs automatically.

Let's explore the seven stages in detail, with code snippets from OpenZeppelin's Governor contract.

①
IDEA
Temperature Check & Off-Chain Discussion
Before anything goes on-chain, a proposal starts as an idea. The community discusses it on a forum (like Discourse) or a voting platform (like Snapshot). This "temperature check" gauges sentiment. If the idea has broad support, the proposer formalizes it into an on-chain proposal. This prevents spam and ensures that only serious proposals consume gas and attention.
②
SUBMIT
Proposal Submission (propose)
The proposer calls governor.propose() with the proposal details: target addresses, calldata, and a description. The contract checks if the proposer holds enough tokens (the proposal threshold). If yes, the proposal enters the Pending state. A unique proposal ID is generated. The proposal is now on-chain and visible to all.
③
DELAY
Voting Delay (Pending → Active)
After submission, there's a waiting period (e.g., 1 day) before voting begins. This gives token holders time to review the proposal, discuss it, and decide their position. The contract enforces this delay; no one can vote until it expires. This is the votingDelay() parameter.
④
VOTE
Voting Period (Active → Succeeded/Defeated)
Token holders cast their votes: For, Against, or Abstain. Voting power is determined by the governance token balance at a past block (the snapshot). The voting period lasts for a fixed duration (e.g., 3 days). At the end, the contract counts votes. If the proposal meets quorum and has more For than Against, it succeeds; otherwise, it's defeated. This is the votingPeriod() and quorum() logic.
⑤
QUEUE
Queuing in the Timelock (Succeeded → Queued)
If the proposal succeeds, it doesn't execute immediately. It must be queued in the TimelockController. Anyone can call governor.queue() to initiate this. The timelock records the execution details and starts its countdown (e.g., 48 hours). This gives the community time to exit before the proposal takes effect - a critical safety mechanism.
⑥
EXECUTE
Execution (Queued → Executed)
After the timelock delay expires, anyone can call governor.execute(). This triggers the TimelockController to execute the proposal's actions - calling the specified contracts with the specified calldata. The execution is atomic and unstoppable. The proposal state changes to Executed, and the transaction's effects are permanent on the blockchain.
05   Technical Deep Dive - OpenZeppelin Governor

The Engine That Runs the Proposal Machine

OpenZeppelin's Governor contract is the industry standard for on-chain DAO governance. It's a modular, inheritable framework that provides the full proposal lifecycle. Let's see a complete configuration for an MST-based DAO.

// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity ^0.8.20;

import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/Governor.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorCountingSimple.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorVotes.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorVotesQuorumFraction.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/governance/extensions/GovernorTimelockControl.sol";

contract MST_DAO is Governor, GovernorCountingSimple, GovernorVotes, GovernorVotesQuorumFraction, GovernorTimelockControl {
    constructor(IVotes _token, TimelockController _timelock)
        Governor("MST_DAO")
        GovernorVotes(_token)
        GovernorVotesQuorumFraction(4)  // 4% quorum
        GovernorTimelockControl(_timelock)
    {}

    // The delay before voting on a proposal starts
    function votingDelay() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 1 days; }
    // The duration of the voting period
    function votingPeriod() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 3 days; }
    // Minimum tokens required to create a proposal
    function proposalThreshold() public pure override returns (uint256) { return 10_000e18; }
    // quorum is defined by the QuorumFraction extension
    function quorum(uint256 blockNumber) public view override(Governor, GovernorVotesQuorumFraction) returns (uint256) {
        return super.quorum(blockNumber);
    }
    // The following are overrides required by Solidity
    function state(uint256 proposalId) public view override(Governor, GovernorTimelockControl) returns (ProposalState) {
        return super.state(proposalId);
    }
    function _execute(uint256 proposalId, address[] memory targets, uint256[] memory values, bytes[] memory calldatas, bytes32 descriptionHash) internal override(Governor, GovernorTimelockControl) {
        super._execute(proposalId, targets, values, calldatas, descriptionHash);
    }
    function _cancel(address[] memory targets, uint256[] memory values, bytes[] memory calldatas, bytes32 descriptionHash) internal override(Governor, GovernorTimelockControl) returns (uint256) {
        return super._cancel(targets, values, calldatas, descriptionHash);
    }
    function _executor() internal view override(Governor, GovernorTimelockControl) returns (address) {
        return super._executor();
    }
    function supportsInterface(bytes4 interfaceId) public view override(Governor, GovernorTimelockControl) returns (bool) {
        return super.supportsInterface(interfaceId);
    }
}

Let's break down the key parameters:

  • votingDelay: Time between proposal submission and the start of voting. Set to 1 day to allow token holders to review the proposal.
  • votingPeriod: Duration that voting is active. 3 days is typical - long enough for broad participation, short enough to maintain momentum.
  • proposalThreshold: Minimum number of tokens a proposer must hold (or be delegated) to submit a proposal. Here, 10,000 tokens. This prevents spam.
  • quorum: Minimum participation required for a proposal to pass. The GovernorVotesQuorumFraction sets quorum as a percentage of total token supply (here 4%). If fewer than 4% of tokens vote, the proposal fails even if the majority is in favor.
  • TimelockControl: Ensures that proposals are queued in a timelock before execution. The timelock delay is configured separately in the TimelockController contract.

Proposal States - The Full Enum

The Governor contract defines eight possible states for a proposal. Understanding this enum is essential for frontend developers building DAO dashboards.

enum ProposalState {
    Pending,   // Submitted, waiting for voting delay to expire
    Active,    // Voting is ongoing
    Canceled,  // Proposal was canceled (by proposer or guardian)
    Defeated,  // Voting ended, but didn't meet quorum or lost
    Succeeded, // Voting passed, waiting to be queued
    Queued,    // Queued in timelock, waiting for execution delay
    Expired,   // Timelock delay passed but never executed (rare)
    Executed   // Proposal actions have been performed
}
06   The TimelockController - Your Safety Net

Why Every DAO Needs a Mandatory Waiting Period

The TimelockController is a separate smart contract that acts as a mandatory waiting room for proposals. After a proposal passes, it must be queued in the timelock and wait for a set period (e.g., 48 hours) before it can be executed. During this time:

  • Token holders who disagree can exit the DAO (sell their tokens) before the change takes effect. This is the ultimate check on majority power - if a proposal is truly harmful, the market will react and token price will drop before execution.
  • The proposal can be canceled by a designated guardian (often a multisig) if a vulnerability is discovered.
  • The community has a final opportunity to review the code that will be executed.

Deploying and Configuring a TimelockController

// Deploy TimelockController with a 2‑day minimum delay
uint256 minDelay = 2 days;
address[] memory proposers = new address[](1);
address[] memory executors = new address[](1);
proposers[0] = address(governorContract); // only governor can propose (queue)
executors[0] = address(0); // anyone can execute

TimelockController timelock = new TimelockController(
    minDelay,
    proposers,
    executors,
    address(0) // admin (set to zero to disable admin functions)
);

The timelock's minDelay is the minimum time a proposal must wait before execution. The proposers (usually only the governor contract) can schedule (queue) proposals. The executors (often set to the zero address, meaning anyone) can execute them after the delay.

07   Interactive Examples - Click to Explore
📝 Example 1: Submitting a Proposal to Fund a Developer

Scenario: A DAO wants to pay 5,000 USDC to a developer for building a new frontend. The proposal has passed off-chain discussion.

On-chain proposal creation (using ethers.js on MST):

const governor = new ethers.Contract(governorAddress, GovernorABI, signer);
const targets = [usdcAddress];
const values = [0];
const calldatas = [
  new ethers.Interface(["function transfer(address,uint256)"]).encodeFunctionData("transfer", [developerAddress, ethers.parseUnits("5000", 6)])
];
const description = "Fund frontend development (Proposal #42)";
const tx = await governor.propose(targets, values, calldatas, description);
await tx.wait();
console.log("Proposal submitted, tx:", tx.hash);

The proposal now enters the Pending state. The voting delay countdown begins.

🗳️ Example 2: Voting on a Proposal

After the voting delay, the proposal becomes Active. Token holders can cast their votes.

// Vote FOR proposal with ID 42
const voteTx = await governor.castVote(42, 1); // 0=Against, 1=For, 2=Abstain
await voteTx.wait();

Voting power is determined by the governance token balance at the snapshot block. If Alice holds 1% of tokens, her vote counts as 1% of the total.

⏳ Example 3: Queuing and Executing

After the voting period ends, the proposal succeeds. Anyone can queue it in the timelock.

// Queue the proposal
const queueTx = await governor.queue(targets, values, calldatas, ethers.keccak256(ethers.toUtf8Bytes(description)));
await queueTx.wait();
// Wait for the timelock delay (e.g., 2 days)
// Then execute
const executeTx = await governor.execute(targets, values, calldatas, ethers.keccak256(ethers.toUtf8Bytes(description)));
await executeTx.wait();
// The USDC transfer occurs automatically
08   MST-Specific Advantages

Why MST Makes the Proposal Lifecycle Affordable for Everyone

1
Sub-paisa proposal creation
On Ethereum, submitting a proposal can cost ₹2,000-₹5,000 in gas, effectively barring small holders from participating. On MST, the same transaction costs ~₹0.01. This means proposals can be submitted by anyone who meets the token threshold, not just whales.
2
Affordable voting for every holder
Casting a vote on MST costs ~₹0.006. A holder with 100 tokens can vote on every proposal for less than a rupee a year. On Ethereum, the same participation would cost thousands of rupees, leading to massive voter apathy. Low fees = high turnout = more legitimate governance.
3
Fast finality for execution
MST's ~3-second block time means that when a proposal is executed, the effects are confirmed almost immediately. There's no risk of chain re-organization, which can happen on slower chains.
4
Full EVM compatibility
OpenZeppelin Governor, TimelockController, and ERC20Votes contracts work identically on MST. No code changes needed. Deploy the same audited contracts with the same tools (Hardhat, OpenZeppelin).
09   Common Pitfalls

Don't Let These Mistakes Sabotage Your DAO

⚠️
Setting quorum too high or too low
If quorum is 20% of total supply and only 10% of tokens are actively held (the rest are lost or in cold storage), no proposal will ever pass. Set quorum based on circulating supply, not total supply. Many DAOs use a quorum of 2-4% of total supply, which reflects actual participation.
⚠️
Forgetting to grant the timelock executor role
If the governor contract is not granted the PROPOSER_ROLE on the timelock, proposals can pass but never be queued. Always grant the governor address as a proposer when deploying the timelock. Test the full lifecycle on a testnet before mainnet.
⚠️
Ignoring the snapshot block
Voting power is determined at a past block (the snapshot). If tokens are transferred after the snapshot, the new holder does NOT gain voting power for ongoing proposals. This prevents flash loan attacks. Always understand when the snapshot is taken.
10   Real-World Case Studies

Proposals That Shaped the DeFi Landscape

🏦
Uniswap DAO - The Fee Switch Proposal
Uniswap's DAO has debated the "fee switch" for years - a proposal that would turn on protocol fees, diverting a portion of trading fees from LPs to UNI token holders. Multiple proposals have been submitted, debated, and voted on. The process follows the exact lifecycle described above: temperature check on the forum, formal on-chain proposal, voting period, and (if passed) queuing. The DAO has not yet passed the fee switch, demonstrating that governance can be slow and deliberate - exactly as designed.
⚖️
MakerDAO - Emergency Shutdown Proposal
During the March 2020 market crash, MakerDAO faced a systemic deficit. An emergency proposal was submitted to mint new MKR tokens and auction them to cover the debt. The proposal went through the full lifecycle, including a shortened voting period (due to emergency powers) and a timelock. The proposal passed and was executed, recapitalizing the system. This demonstrated that the Governor + Timelock framework can handle both routine and emergency governance.
11   Hands-On: Deploy Your Own Governor on MST

Step-by-Step Deployment Guide

Follow these steps to deploy a fully functional Governor contract with a TimelockController on MST Testnet.

🧱 Step 1: Deploy a Governance Token (ERC20Votes)

First, deploy an ERC-20 token that supports voting. Use OpenZeppelin's ERC20Votes extension.

// contracts/MyGovernanceToken.sol
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/token/ERC20/extensions/ERC20Votes.sol";
contract MyGovToken is ERC20Votes {
    constructor() ERC20("MyGovToken", "MGT") {
        _mint(msg.sender, 1_000_000 * 10**decimals());
    }
}
⏳ Step 2: Deploy the TimelockController

Deploy the timelock with a 2-day minimum delay. The governor address will be added later as a proposer.

const Timelock = await ethers.getContractFactory("TimelockController");
const timelock = await Timelock.deploy(2 * 24 * 3600, [], [], ethers.ZeroAddress);
await timelock.deployed();
🏛️ Step 3: Deploy the Governor contract

Deploy the Governor, passing the token and timelock addresses. Then grant roles.

const Governor = await ethers.getContractFactory("MST_DAO");
const governor = await Governor.deploy(token.address, timelock.address);
await governor.deployed();

// Grant the governor as a proposer and executor on the timelock
const PROPOSER_ROLE = ethers.keccak256(ethers.toUtf8Bytes("PROPOSER_ROLE"));
const EXECUTOR_ROLE = ethers.keccak256(ethers.toUtf8Bytes("EXECUTOR_ROLE"));
await timelock.grantRole(PROPOSER_ROLE, governor.address);
await timelock.grantRole(EXECUTOR_ROLE, governor.address);
🧪 Step 4: Test the full proposal lifecycle

Submit a test proposal, advance time (using Hardhat's time helpers), vote, queue, and execute. Verify that the target contract is called correctly. Gas cost on MST Testnet: less than ₹0.05 total for the entire lifecycle.

13   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 15.3

Proposal Lifecycle
The sequence of states a DAO proposal goes through: Pending, Active, Succeeded/Defeated, Queued, Executed.
Governor Contract
OpenZeppelin's modular smart contract that manages the entire proposal lifecycle, voting, and execution.
TimelockController
A smart contract that enforces a mandatory delay between a proposal passing and its execution, providing a safety window.
Voting Delay
The time between proposal submission and the start of voting, allowing token holders to review the proposal.
Voting Period
The duration during which token holders can cast their votes (For, Against, Abstain).
Quorum
The minimum number of votes (or percentage of total supply) required for a proposal to be valid.
Proposal Threshold
The minimum number of tokens a user must hold (or have delegated) to submit a proposal.
Snapshot
The past block number at which token balances are recorded to determine voting power, preventing flash loan attacks.
