02   Real-World Introduction Story

The DAO That Lost ₹10 Crore Because of a Single Private Key

📖 Story

In 2022, a promising DeFi DAO had accumulated ₹10 crore in its treasury - a mix of ETH, USDC, and governance tokens. The treasury was controlled by a single externally-owned account (EOA) held by the founder. One morning, the founder woke up to find that his private key had been compromised through a phishing attack. The attacker drained the entire treasury in a single transaction. The DAO had no multisig, no timelock, no governance-controlled treasury. It was a single point of failure. The DAO collapsed within weeks.


Contrast this with a well-managed DAO: its treasury is a Gnosis Safe multisig requiring 3 of 5 signers - all respected community members with hardware wallets. Spending proposals go through the DAO's Governor contract, are queued in a timelock, and are executed automatically via the Safe's module system. No single person can move a rupee without consensus. On MST, every treasury action - paying a contributor, swapping tokens for stablecoins, funding a grant - costs a fraction of a paisa, enabling active, democratic management. This sub-module teaches you to build the second kind of treasury.

03   Background & History

From Single Keys to Smart Contract Safes - The Evolution of DAO Treasuries

In the early days of crypto (2011-2016), project treasuries were simple: a single Bitcoin or Ethereum address controlled by one person. This worked for small amounts, but as ICOs raised millions, the risk of a single compromised key became unacceptable. The first major innovation was the multisignature (multisig) wallet - a smart contract that requires M-of-N signatures to execute any transaction. The most popular implementation, Gnosis Safe, launched in 2018 and quickly became the standard for DAO treasuries.

Parallel to multisig development, DAOs began integrating treasuries with on-chain governance. Instead of multisig signers manually executing every approved proposal, a Governor contract could queue and execute transactions directly through the multisig, making the governance process fully automated. Projects like Moloch DAO (2019) pioneered this pattern, where a proposal could be submitted, voted on, and if passed, the treasury would automatically execute the transfer.

Today, the most sophisticated DAOs use a layered treasury: a core multisig for high-value assets, operational wallets for smaller, frequent payments (managed by a subset of signers or a DAO-elected team), and yield strategies to put idle assets to work. On MST, the sub-paisa transaction costs mean that even small, frequent treasury operations - like paying a monthly stipend to a contributor - can be executed on-chain without wasting funds on gas.

Key insight: A DAO treasury is not just a wallet. It's a financial engine that must be secured, governed, diversified, and deployed. The architecture you choose determines whether your DAO thrives or becomes a cautionary tale.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The DAO's Bank Account - But No Single Person Has the Password

🔐 The Analogy

Imagine a bank vault with three locks, and the keys are held by three different people. To open the vault, at least two of them must turn their keys simultaneously. That's a 2-of-3 multisig. The vault also has a rule: it can only be opened if a majority of the town's citizens (token holders) voted to allow it. That's a governance-controlled treasury. And the vault is smart enough to invest some of its gold in interest-bearing bonds while keeping enough cash on hand for daily expenses - that's treasury management.

Let's explore the core concepts:

M
MULTISIG
What is a multisig wallet?
A multisig (multi-signature) is a smart contract that requires multiple private keys to authorize a transaction. Common configurations: 2-of-3, 3-of-5, 4-of-7. This means that even if one key is stolen, the attacker cannot move funds. Gnosis Safe is the most trusted multisig on EVM chains, securing over $100 billion in assets.
S
SAFE
How does Gnosis Safe work as a DAO treasury?
A Gnosis Safe can be deployed on any EVM chain, including MST. It's a smart contract wallet with features like: owner management (add/remove signers via another multisig vote), modules (allow custom logic like spending limits, allowance for specific addresses), and transaction builder (batch multiple actions into one transaction). For DAOs, the Safe can be configured to only execute transactions that have been approved by the Governor contract - creating a fully on-chain governance-to-execution pipeline.
D
DIVERSIFY
How should a DAO diversify its treasury?
The "endowment model" is widely used: hold a mix of stablecoins (USDC, DAI) for operational expenses, native protocol tokens for long-term alignment, and blue-chip crypto assets (ETH, MSTC) for growth. Yield-bearing assets (staked tokens, lending deposits) can generate passive income. The key principle: don't be 100% in any single asset. A DAO whose treasury is 90% in its own governance token is one market crash away from insolvency.
05   Technical Deep Dive - Multisig Wallets & Gnosis Safe

Build a 3-of-5 Multisig Treasury on MST

Gnosis Safe is the gold standard for multisig treasuries. Let's walk through setting up a Safe on MST and integrating it with governance.

Deploying a Gnosis Safe

Gnosis Safe is available through its web interface (app.safe.global) or via smart contract deployment. On MST, you can deploy the Safe contracts using the official factory. The core contracts are:

  • Safe Proxy Factory: Creates new Safe instances deterministically.
  • Safe Singleton: The master copy (implementation) contract.
  • MultiSend: Allows batching multiple transactions into one.
  • Default Fallback Handler: Handles incoming ETH and token transfers.

A typical deployment script for MST (using Hardhat):

const { ethers } = require("hardhat");

async function deploySafe() {
  // Deploy the Safe singleton and factory
  const Safe = await ethers.getContractFactory("Safe");
  const safeSingleton = await Safe.deploy();
  await safeSingleton.deployed();

  const Factory = await ethers.getContractFactory("SafeProxyFactory");
  const factory = await Factory.deploy();
  await factory.deployed();

  // Create a new Safe with 5 owners, threshold 3
  const owners = [addr1, addr2, addr3, addr4, addr5];
  const threshold = 3;
  const tx = await factory.createProxyWithNonce(
    safeSingleton.address,
    "0x", // initializer (empty for default)
    saltNonce
  );
  const receipt = await tx.wait();
  // The new Safe address is emitted in the event
  console.log("Safe deployed at:", safeAddress);
}

Adding Modules for Spending Limits

Gnosis Safe supports modules - smart contracts that extend its functionality. A common module is the SpendingLimit module, which allows a designated address to spend up to a certain amount per day without requiring full multisig approval. This is useful for operational expenses.

// Enable the SpendingLimit module for an operations wallet
const spendingLimitModule = await deploySpendingLimit(
  safeAddress,
  operationsWallet,
  ethers.parseEther("100"), // max 100 MSTC per day
  RESET_TIME_ONE_DAY
);
// Enable the module in the Safe
await safe.enableModule(spendingLimitModule.address);

Connecting Safe to DAO Governance

To enable fully on-chain treasury execution, the Safe should be the executor of the DAO's Governor contract. When a proposal passes, the Governor queues it in the Timelock, and upon execution, the Timelock calls the Safe's execTransaction function.

Alternatively, the Safe itself can be one of the targets of a proposal, meaning the proposal instructs the Safe to perform a transaction. For example, a proposal could call safe.execTransaction(to, value, data, operation, ...) with signatures collected off-chain via the Safe's UI, or with on-chain approval from the Safe's owners (who could be the Governor contract itself or individual signers).

06   Treasury Diversification & Risk Management

Don't Put All Your Eggs in One Basket - Especially If the Basket Is Your Own Token

One of the most common and dangerous treasury mistakes is over-concentration in the DAO's own governance token. If the token price crashes, the treasury becomes worthless, and the DAO can no longer pay contributors or fund operations. Professional DAOs follow diversification principles:

The Endowment Model

Inspired by university endowments (like Harvard's), this model divides the treasury into:

  • Operating Reserve (20-30%): Stablecoins (USDC, DAI) for paying contributors, grants, and operational costs for the next 12-24 months. This ensures the DAO can survive a multi-year bear market.
  • Core Holdings (30-50%): Blue-chip crypto assets like MSTC, ETH, or BTC. These provide long-term growth potential and are relatively liquid.
  • Protocol-Owned Liquidity (10-20%): The DAO's own governance token paired with a stablecoin or native token, deposited in a DEX to provide liquidity and earn fees. This also defends against price manipulation.
  • Yield-Bearing Assets (10-20%): Staked tokens, lending deposits (on Aave or Compound), or LP tokens in yield farms. These generate passive income for the DAO without selling core assets.
  • Illiquid / Strategic (5-10%): NFT collections, early-stage investments, or real-world assets. These are long-term bets.

Diversification Mechanics on MST

On MST, actively managing a treasury is economically feasible because each rebalancing transaction costs ~₹0.006. A DAO can:

  • Swap tokens on a DEX: If the DAO receives protocol fees in MSTC, it can periodically swap a portion to USDC to build the operating reserve. Gas cost: ~₹0.006 per swap.
  • Deposit into lending protocols: Idle USDC can be deposited into Aave (on MST) to earn yield. Gas cost: ~₹0.006 per deposit/withdrawal.
  • Stake MSTC: Earn staking rewards while retaining exposure to MSTC. Liquid staking (if available) allows the staked MSTC to be used in DeFi.
  • Provide liquidity: Deposit protocol-owned tokens into a DEX pool to earn trading fees and deepen liquidity. Gas cost: ~₹0.012 for add/remove liquidity.
07   Treasury Execution Patterns

How Money Leaves the Treasury - Three Common Flows

In a DAO, every treasury disbursement must be authorized. Here are the three most common patterns, from least to most trustless.

Pattern 1: Multisig Signers Execute Manually

The proposal passes (on-chain or off-chain via Snapshot). Multisig signers review the result, then manually sign and execute the transaction from the Safe. This is simple but relies on the signers being responsive and honest. It introduces a trust assumption: the signers must faithfully execute the community's will.

Pattern 2: Governor + Timelock → Safe (Automated)

The proposal passes on-chain through the Governor contract. It is queued in the Timelock. After the delay, anyone calls execute(), and the Timelock sends the transaction to the Safe. The Safe must have the Timelock as an authorized module or owner. This removes the need for signer intervention - the code executes automatically.

Pattern 3: DAO-Controlled Treasury Smart Contract

Instead of a separate Safe, the treasury is a custom smart contract that integrates directly with the Governor. Proposals call functions like treasury.transfer(token, to, amount). This is the most trustless model, but it requires a custom, audited treasury contract.

08   Interactive Examples - Click to Explore
💸 Example 1: Pay a Contributor from the Treasury

Scenario: A DAO has approved a proposal to pay a developer 1,000 USDC for completing a frontend feature. The treasury is a 3-of-5 Safe. Two of the five signers are traveling; the remaining three must execute the payment.

Process: One signer creates the transaction in the Safe UI, specifying the USDC contract address, the transfer function, and the developer's address and amount. The transaction is submitted for signing. The other two available signers approve it via the Safe UI (or via a script using their keys). Once 3 signatures are collected, the transaction is broadcast to the MST network. Gas cost: ~₹0.006. The developer receives 1,000 USDC.

🔄 Example 2: Rebalance Treasury - Sell MSTC for USDC

Scenario: The DAO's treasury has accumulated 100,000 MSTC from protocol fees, but its operating reserve in USDC is low. The DAO wants to sell 10,000 MSTC for USDC to ensure it can pay contributors for the next year.

On-chain execution via proposal: A proposal is created targeting the DEX router contract with calldata for swapExactTokensForTokens(10000 MSTC, minUSDC, [MSTC, USDC], treasury, deadline). The proposal passes governance, is queued, and executes. The Safe calls the router, which swaps MSTC for USDC and deposits the USDC back into the Safe. Total gas: ~₹0.018 (approval + swap). On Ethereum, this rebalance could cost ₹2,000+. On MST, it's a no-brainer.

📊 Example 3: Earn Yield on Idle Stablecoins

Scenario: The treasury has 50,000 USDC sitting idle. The DAO wants to deposit it into Aave (on MST) to earn ~5% APY.

Process: A proposal targets the Aave lending pool contract on MST with calldata for deposit(USDC, 50000, treasury, 0). After governance approval, the Safe deposits the USDC and receives aUSDC (interest-bearing tokens). The aUSDC stays in the Safe. When the DAO needs the funds, a reverse proposal withdraws them. The gas cost for deposit is ~₹0.006; for withdrawal, ~₹0.006. The yield earned can pay for the gas thousands of times over.

09   MST-Specific Advantages for Treasury Management

Why MST Makes Active Treasury Management Affordable

1
Sub-paisa transaction costs
Every treasury action - paying contributors, swapping tokens, harvesting yield - costs ~₹0.006 on MST. On Ethereum, the same actions cost ₹500-₹2,000 each. This means DAOs on MST can actively manage their treasuries daily without burning funds on gas. A DAO with 100 treasury transactions per year would spend ₹0.60 on MST vs. ₹50,000+ on Ethereum.
2
Full EVM compatibility with Gnosis Safe
Gnosis Safe contracts deploy and function identically on MST. The Safe UI can be pointed at an MST RPC. All modules (SpendingLimit, Allowance) work out of the box.
3
Fast finality for time-sensitive treasury operations
MST's ~3-second block time means that when a multisig transaction is executed, the funds move almost instantly. There's no 15-minute wait for confirmations, which is critical during market volatility when a DAO needs to quickly move assets.
4
Hybrid zones for compliance
For DAOs that need to interact with regulated entities, MST's permissioned zones allow a part of the treasury (e.g., the operating reserve) to be managed with KYC compliance, while the core holdings remain on the public zone.
10   Common Pitfalls in Treasury Management

Don't Let Your DAO Become the Next Cautionary Tale

⚠️
Single-signer treasury (or 1-of-N with N=1)
A single private key controlling the treasury is a recipe for disaster. Always use at least a 2-of-3 multisig, with keys held by different people on different devices, ideally hardware wallets.
⚠️
100% allocation in native governance token
If the DAO's token crashes 90%, the treasury becomes worthless and the DAO cannot pay contributors. Diversify into stablecoins and blue-chip assets.
⚠️
No operational reserve for a bear market
DAOs should hold at least 12-24 months of operating expenses in stablecoins. This ensures the DAO can survive a multi-year crypto winter without selling core assets at the bottom.
⚠️
Ignoring smart contract risk in yield strategies
Depositing treasury funds into unaudited yield farms can lead to loss of principal. Stick to battle-tested protocols (Aave, Compound, major DEXs) and consider insurance (like Nexus Mutual) for large deposits.
11   Real-World Case Studies

How Top DAOs Manage Billions

🏦
Uniswap DAO - The $2.5 Billion Treasury
Uniswap's treasury, held in a Gnosis Safe and governed by UNI token holders, is one of the largest DAO treasuries. It consists primarily of UNI tokens (protocol-owned), plus ETH and stablecoins from the fee switch (not yet activated). The DAO has funded developer grants, liquidity mining programs, and a political advocacy arm (DeFi Education Fund). The treasury's execution is a mix of manual multisig (for early grants) and on-chain proposals (for larger, recurring programs).
💰
MakerDAO - Diversified with Real-World Assets
MakerDAO's treasury includes not just crypto assets but also real-world assets (RWAs) like U.S. Treasury bonds, managed through legal structures. The DAO's governance votes on risk parameters and allocation. This demonstrates that DAOs can manage traditional financial instruments, bridging DeFi and traditional finance. MST's RWA Orbitals could enable similar diversification for DAOs in India.
⚖️
Gitcoin DAO - Quadratic Funding from the Treasury
Gitcoin DAO uses a quadratic funding model: the community donates to projects, and the DAO matches those donations from its treasury, with the match amount determined by the number of unique donors (not the amount). This ensures that projects with broad community support get the most funding. The treasury holds GTC tokens and ETH, and disbursements are executed via Snapshot + Safe.

14   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 15.4

Multisig (Multi-Signature)
A smart contract wallet requiring M-of-N signatures to execute a transaction. Eliminates single points of failure.
Gnosis Safe
The most widely used multisig wallet on EVM chains, supporting modules, spending limits, and integration with DAO governance.

A treasury diversification strategy that allocates assets across operating reserves, core holdings, yield-bearing assets, and strategic investments.
