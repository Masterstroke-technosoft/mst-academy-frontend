The Story - ₹1,200 vs ₹8: The Entire Argument for Decentralisation

📍 Setting: Kerala - A Worker's Monthly Ritual

Rajan works in a Gulf country. He earns ₹60,000 per month equivalent. Every month he sends money home to his family in Thrissur, Kerala. He has two options.

Option 1 - Western Union: Walk to the nearest Western Union outlet. Fill in a paper form. Show passport. Wait 2 - 3 days. Pay ₹1,200 in fees (2% + fixed charges). His family receives ₹58,800.

Option 2 - Crypto via blockchain: Open phone. Scan his family's wallet QR code. Send. Wait 3 seconds. Pay ₹8 in transaction fees. His family receives ₹59,992.

The difference: ₹1,192 per month. ₹14,304 per year. Over 20 years of working abroad: ₹2.86 lakh - gone to middlemen.

India receives $120 billion in annual remittances - the largest in the world. At an average fee of 6%, that's $7.2 billion per year that leaves Indian workers' pockets and goes to Western Union, MoneyGram, and bank transfer fees. Decentralisation's biggest gift to India is not technology - it is $7.2 billion returned to working families.

Security Notes

9.1 - The Custodial Trap: Why "Empowerment" Requires Responsibility

Decentralisation's user empowerment benefit comes with an equal responsibility: if you lose your private key, nobody can help you recover your funds. This is the trade-off for true ownership. In 2021, Stefan Thomas, a programmer, lost access to 7,002 Bitcoin (worth ~₹3,500 crore at peak) because he forgot the password to his encrypted hard drive containing his private keys. Centralised systems have account recovery - decentralised ones do not, by design.

⚠️ MST's Solution: SARAL Protocol

MST's SARAL Protocol addresses this specific problem. It uses secure enclaves (hardware-protected key storage) and social recovery mechanisms - where trusted contacts can collectively help you recover access if you lose your device. This preserves non-custodial ownership (no company holds your key) while providing a safety net for key loss. True empowerment without the catastrophic downside of irreversible loss.

9.2 - Transparency's Double Edge: Privacy vs Accountability

Full transparency is powerful for accountability but creates financial privacy risks. If your wallet address is linked to your identity (as it might be through exchange KYC), every transaction you make is publicly traceable. Governments, businesses, and criminals can all see your complete financial history. This is why MST Blockchain's ZK proof layer is critical - enabling selective disclosure where you prove specific facts (I have enough balance for this transaction) without revealing your complete financial history.

Glossary / Key Terms

Resilience

System's ability to continue functioning when components fail. Decentralised systems achieve architectural resilience - any node can fail without affecting the network.

Transparency

All transactions recorded on a public immutable ledger viewable by anyone, anywhere, anytime - without permission. Enables real-time fraud detection by any participant.

Remittance

Money sent by workers abroad to their home country. India receives $120B annually - world's largest. Average fee ~6.2% currently; blockchain reduces this to <0.2%.

Correspondent Bank

Intermediary bank in international transfers. Connects sending and receiving banks that don't have a direct relationship. Charges fees at each hop. Blockchain eliminates the need for correspondents.

SWIFT

Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication - the messaging network banks use for international transfers. Slow (3 - 5 days), expensive, and centralised. Blockchain is its decentralised replacement.

User Empowerment

Users holding cryptographic ownership of assets and data via private keys. No company can freeze, seize, or restrict access without the private key - which only the user holds.

Censorship Resistance

Architectural property where no single entity can block, remove, or restrict access. Achieved through global validator distribution across multiple jurisdictions and IPFS content addressing.

Non-Custodial Wallet

Wallet where only the user holds the private key. No exchange, bank, or company has custody. Complete ownership - and complete responsibility if the key is lost.

Gas Fee

Fee paid to blockchain validators for processing a transaction. In MST, extremely low (<₹10 typically). This is the only "middleman" fee - and it goes to the decentralised validator network, not a corporation.

Pseudonymous

Identity represented by wallet address, not real name. Transactions publicly visible but not directly attributed to a named person. De-anonymisation possible through chain analysis and KYC data.

Exchange Rate Spread

Hidden fee embedded in currency conversion - the difference between market exchange rate and the rate offered by a money transfer service. Often the largest hidden cost in remittances.

Five Nines

99.999% uptime - industry gold standard for high-availability systems, meaning 5.26 minutes of downtime per year. MST Blockchain targets ~100% availability through distributed architecture.

Explanation: The Yes Bank moratorium illustrates the fundamental difference between a bank account and a blockchain wallet.
