03   Background & History

From Order Books to Mathematical Robots - The AMM Revolution

Traditional exchanges - like the Bombay Stock Exchange or Coinbase - use an order book model. Buyers and sellers place orders at specific prices, and the exchange matches them. This works well for highly liquid assets, but it requires market makers (professional firms) to continuously quote prices. In crypto, especially for new tokens, order books often suffer from low liquidity and wide spreads.

In 2016, an Ethereum researcher named Vitalik Buterin proposed the idea of an on-chain automated market maker. The concept was simple: instead of matching buyers and sellers, let a smart contract hold a pool of two tokens and use a mathematical formula to determine the price. The first implementation was Bancor in 2017, but it was Uniswap, launched by Hayden Adams in November 2018, that perfected the model and brought AMMs to the masses.

Uniswap v1 used the constant product formula: x * y = k, where x and y are the reserves of the two tokens. When a trader swaps token A for token B, they add to one reserve and remove from the other, but the product k remains constant (ignoring fees). This simple rule created a fully automated, always-available market with no order book, no spreads set by humans, and no central authority.

Uniswap v2 (2020) added ERC-20/ERC-20 pairs (v1 only supported ETH as one side) and introduced the concept of LP tokens representing a provider's share of the pool. Uniswap v3 (2021) introduced concentrated liquidity, allowing LPs to provide liquidity within specific price ranges, dramatically improving capital efficiency. Today, Uniswap processes billions in daily volume, and its AMM design has been copied by hundreds of protocols across dozens of chains - including MST.

MST advantage: Because MST is fully EVM-compatible, Uniswap v2 and v3 contracts can be deployed on MST with minimal changes. And because gas fees are ~100,000× lower than Ethereum's, active LP management - adjusting positions, claiming fees, rebalancing - becomes economically practical. On Ethereum, each adjustment can cost ₹500+; on MST, ₹0.006.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Robot Market Maker - Always Ready to Trade

🤖 The Analogy

Imagine a vending machine that never runs out of snacks, and the price changes automatically based on how many snacks are left. If people buy a lot of chips, the machine raises the price. If nobody buys, the price drops. The machine doesn't care who's buying or selling - it just follows a simple rule. An AMM is exactly that vending machine, but for tokens. It holds a pool of two tokens and uses a formula to decide how many of each you get. No human sets the price; the math does.

Every AMM has three core components. Let's understand them in plain language:

P
POOL
What is a liquidity pool?
A liquidity pool is a smart contract that holds two tokens (e.g., MSTC and USDC). Anyone can deposit equal values of both tokens to become a liquidity provider (LP). LPs earn a share of the trading fees. The pool never runs out of liquidity because the formula adjusts the price as tokens are traded - it just gets more and more expensive to drain it completely.
F
FORMULA
What is the constant product formula?
The core of Uniswap-style AMMs is x * y = k. Here, x is the amount of token A in the pool, y is the amount of token B, and k is a constant (before fees). When a trader swaps, they change x and y, but the product stays the same. This formula automatically raises the price as demand increases and lowers it as supply increases - exactly like supply and demand in traditional markets, but enforced by code.
L
LOSS
What is impermanent loss?
Impermanent loss (IL) is the difference between the value of your tokens if you'd simply held them versus providing them as liquidity. It happens when the price of the tokens changes relative to each other. The loss is "impermanent" because if the price returns to the original, the loss disappears. But if you withdraw while the price is far from your entry, the loss becomes permanent. Trading fees can offset IL, but LPs must understand this risk.
05   Technical Deep Dive - The Constant Product

x * y = k - The Formula That Runs Decentralized Trading

Let's explore the math behind AMMs with concrete numbers. No advanced degree required - just multiplication and division.

How a Swap Works

Suppose an MSTC/USDC pool starts with 100 MSTC and 10,000 USDC. The constant product k = 100 × 10,000 = 1,000,000. The current price of MSTC is 10,000 USDC / 100 MSTC = 100 USDC per MSTC.

Now a trader wants to buy 1 MSTC. How much USDC must they pay? After the swap, the new MSTC reserve will be 99 (since they take 1 out), so the new USDC reserve must satisfy:

x' * y' = k → (100 - 1) * y' = 1,000,000 → 99 * y' = 1,000,000 → y' = 10,101.01 USDC

The trader must add 101.01 USDC to the pool (from 10,000 to 10,101.01). So the price they pay for 1 MSTC is 101.01 USDC - slightly more than the spot price of 100 USDC. This is called slippage or price impact. The larger the trade relative to the pool, the worse the price.

The Price Curve

The constant product formula creates a hyperbola. Near the middle, the curve is relatively flat - small trades have low price impact. Toward the edges (near zero of one token), the curve becomes extremely steep - draining the last of a token is astronomically expensive. This means the pool can never be completely drained; it just gets prohibitively costly.

06   Impermanent Loss - The Silent Risk

Why Liquidity Providers Can Lose Value

Impermanent loss occurs because AMMs automatically rebalance your portfolio as the price changes. When one token rises in value, the pool sells it (to arbitrageurs) to maintain the constant product, reducing your exposure to the winner and increasing exposure to the loser. Here's the math.

Impermanent Loss Formula

If you deposit tokens when the price ratio is P, and later the price changes by a factor of r (so the new price is r × P), the impermanent loss (as a percentage of your initial deposit value) is:

IL = 2√r / (1 + r) - 1

This formula assumes no fees are earned. Let's test it:

  • Price stays the same (r = 1): IL = 2√1 / (1+1) - 1 = 2/2 - 1 = 0. No loss.
  • Price doubles (r = 2): IL = 2√2 / 3 - 1 ≈ 2.828/3 - 1 ≈ 0.9427 - 1 = -5.73%. You lose ~5.7% relative to holding.
  • Price quadruples (r = 4): IL = 2√4 / 5 - 1 = 4/5 - 1 = -20%.
  • Price drops by half (r = 0.5): IL = 2√0.5 / 1.5 - 1 ≈ 1.414/1.5 - 1 ≈ 0.9427 - 1 = -5.73% (symmetric).

Notice: impermanent loss is symmetric - a 2x increase and a 2x decrease both cause the same percentage loss. The loss increases as the price moves further from your entry. Importantly, trading fees can offset this loss. If the pool earns 0.3% fees on every trade, high-volume pools can compensate for significant IL.

Calculate Your Own IL - Hands-On

🧮 Example: MSTC/USDC Pool - Price Doubles

Initial Deposit: You provide 10 MSTC and 1,000 USDC to the pool. The price of MSTC is 100 USDC. Total value = 2,000 USDC.

Price doubles: External market price of MSTC rises to 200 USDC. Arbitrageurs trade against the pool to align the pool price with the market. After arbitrage, the pool reserves adjust according to the constant product:

Initial k = 10 × 1,000 = 10,000. New MSTC price = 200 USDC → USDC/MSTC ratio must be 200. So (y / x) = 200, and y = 200x. Plug into x*y=10,000: x*(200x)=200x²=10,000 → x²=50 → x≈7.07 MSTC, y≈1,414.21 USDC.

Your share: If you own 100% of the pool (for simplicity), your tokens are now 7.07 MSTC + 1,414.21 USDC = 7.07×200 + 1,414.21 = 2,828.21 USDC. If you had just held: 10 MSTC × 200 + 1,000 USDC = 3,000 USDC. Loss = 3,000 - 2,828.21 = 171.79 USDC, which is 5.73% of 3,000. Matches the IL formula for r=2 (5.73%).

08   Uniswap v2 vs v3

Concentrated Liquidity - More Bang for Your Buck

Uniswap v2 spreads liquidity evenly across all price ranges from 0 to infinity. That's safe, but capital-inefficient - most of the liquidity sits unused because prices rarely visit extremes. Uniswap v3 introduced concentrated liquidity: LPs can choose a price range (e.g., 1,800 - 2,200 USDC per ETH) and provide liquidity only within that range. This means capital is concentrated where it's needed most, earning far higher fees per dollar deposited.

📋 SCENARIO
Vikram deposits 5 ETH and 10,000 USDC into an ETH/USDC liquidity pool when 1 ETH = 2,000 USDC. After some time, the price of ETH rises to 3,000 USDC. Vikram withdraws his liquidity. Assume no trading fees were earned and Vikram owns 100% of the pool for simplicity.
Given:
• Initial ETH: 5, Initial USDC: 10,000
• Initial price: 2,000 USDC/ETH
• New price: 3,000 USDC/ETH
• Constant product k = 5 × 10,000 = 50,000
• Vikram owns the entire pool (100%).

Task: (a) Calculate the new pool reserves after arbitrage to match the new price. (b) Determine the total value of Vikram's withdrawn tokens at the new price. (c) Determine the value he would have had if he simply held the tokens. (d) Compute the impermanent loss in USDC and as a percentage.
✅ Correct Calculation

(a) New reserves: Price = y/x = 3,000, so y = 3,000x. Plug into x*y = 50,000: x * (3,000x) = 3,000x² = 50,000 → x² = 50,000/3,000 = 16.666… → x ≈ 4.0825 ETH. Then y = 3,000 × 4.0825 ≈ 12,247.45 USDC.

(b) Value if withdrawn: 4.0825 ETH × 3,000 + 12,247.45 USDC = 12,247.5 + 12,247.45 = 24,494.95 USDC.

(c) Value if held: 5 ETH × 3,000 + 10,000 USDC = 15,000 + 10,000 = 25,000 USDC.

(d) Impermanent loss: 25,000 - 24,494.95 = 505.05 USDC. As a percentage: (505.05 / 25,000) × 100 ≈ 2.02%. (Note: the formula gives IL = 2√(1.5)/(2.5) - 1 = 2×1.2247/2.5 - 1 = 0.9798 - 1 = -0.0202, i.e., 2.02% loss.)

12   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 11.2

AMM
Automated Market Maker - a decentralized exchange that uses a mathematical formula to set prices instead of an order book.
Constant Product
The formula x*y=k used by Uniswap. Ensures that the product of token reserves remains constant, determining swap prices.
Liquidity Pool
A smart contract that holds two tokens and allows users to trade between them, with liquidity provided by LPs who earn fees.
Impermanent Loss
The loss LPs experience relative to holding tokens, caused by price changes between the paired assets. Can be offset by trading fees.
Concentrated Liquidity
Uniswap v3 feature allowing LPs to provide liquidity only within a chosen price range, boosting capital efficiency.
Slippage
The difference between the expected price of a trade and the actual execution price, caused by the trade moving the AMM curve.
