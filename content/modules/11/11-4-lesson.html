03   Background & History

From Locked Coins to Algorithmic Farms - The Evolution of Yield

The concept of earning a return on idle capital is as old as banking itself. In traditional finance, you deposit money in a savings account and earn interest - the bank lends your money and shares a tiny fraction of the profit. In crypto, staking and yield farming achieve the same goal, but they cut out the middleman. The returns come directly from network inflation, trading fees, or protocol incentives, all managed by transparent smart contracts.

Staking originated with Proof-of-Stake (PoS) consensus mechanisms. In 2012, Peercoin introduced the first PoS model, where coin holders could "stake" their coins to help validate transactions and earn block rewards. The idea was simple: align economic incentives with network security. If you have skin in the game, you're less likely to attack the network. Ethereum's transition to PoS in 2022 (The Merge) made staking a mainstream concept, with millions of ETH locked in the Beacon Chain.

In parallel, yield farming emerged from the DeFi summer of 2020. Compound's launch of the COMP governance token introduced "liquidity mining" - users who lent or borrowed on Compound received COMP tokens as a reward. This turned lending from a low-yield activity into a speculative gold rush. Farmers would deposit assets to earn COMP, sell the COMP for more assets, and repeat. The term "yield farming" was born. Soon, protocols like Yearn Finance automated the process, creating vaults that automatically moved funds between the highest-yielding opportunities.

Today, staking and yield farming are the backbone of DeFi's incentive design. They bootstrap liquidity, distribute governance power, and give users a reason to hold tokens long-term. On MST, with its native Trika POSA consensus, MSTC staking directly secures the network - stakers earn rewards while helping choose validators and process transactions.

Key insight: Staking is the "savings account" of crypto - low risk, steady returns. Yield farming is the "active investment portfolio" - higher returns, but requires more strategy and carries more risk. Both are dramatically more profitable on low-fee chains like MST.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

Staking = Earning Rent. Yield Farming = Being a Landlord.

🏠 The Analogy

Imagine you own an apartment. Staking is like renting it out to a single, reliable tenant who pays a fixed rent every month. You hand over the keys, and the tenant uses the apartment (the tokens are locked). In return, you get a steady income. Yield farming is like running a hotel - you actively move your capital between different "rooms" (protocols) depending on which one has the highest occupancy rate and pays the best nightly rate. You might rent a room to a guest (provide liquidity to a DEX), then use the earnings to buy more rooms. You're constantly optimizing, monitoring, and reallocating. The hotel requires more work, but the potential income is much higher.

Let's break down the core concepts:

S
STAKING
What is staking?
Staking means locking your tokens in a smart contract or with a validator to support network operations (in PoS) or to signal commitment (in governance). In return, you earn staking rewards - typically a fixed or variable APY paid in the same token. Your tokens are usually locked for a period, and you may face penalties (slashing) if you act maliciously (in PoS) or withdraw early. Staking is considered lower risk because the returns are relatively predictable.
Y
YIELD
What is yield farming?
Yield farming is the active practice of moving crypto assets between different DeFi protocols to maximize returns. This might involve: providing liquidity to a DEX to earn trading fees, depositing into a lending protocol to earn interest, staking LP tokens in a reward farm to earn additional governance tokens, and then reinvesting those tokens. Yield farmers constantly monitor APYs, impermanent loss, and token prices to optimize their strategy. It's higher risk - impermanent loss, smart contract risk, and reward token price volatility all factor in.
V
veTOKEN
What is veTokenomics?
Voting-escrow tokenomics (veTokenomics) is a mechanism pioneered by Curve Finance. Users lock their governance tokens for a chosen period (e.g., 1 week to 4 years) and receive veTokens (voting-escrow tokens) in return. The longer the lock, the more veTokens you get. veTokens cannot be transferred, but they grant enhanced voting power and boosted staking rewards. This aligns incentives: the biggest rewards and the most governance influence go to those who commit long-term. It reduces mercenary capital and creates loyal communities.
05   Technical Deep Dive - Staking

How Proof-of-Stake Secures a Network (and Pays You)

In a Proof-of-Stake blockchain like MST (using Trika POSA), validators are chosen to produce blocks based on the amount of MSTC they have staked (or delegated to them). The more MSTC at stake, the higher the chance of being selected. Validators earn block rewards and transaction fees, which they share with their delegators (minus a commission).

Staking Rewards and APR Calculation

The staking APR depends on the total amount of MSTC staked across the network. If more tokens are staked, the reward per token decreases (since the same block rewards are shared among more stakers). If many stakers withdraw, the APR increases to attract them back. This creates a self-balancing mechanism.

Example: Suppose the MST network issues 10,000,000 MSTC in staking rewards per year. If 100,000,000 MSTC is staked, the APR = 10,000,000 / 100,000,000 = 10%. If the staked amount drops to 50,000,000, the APR rises to 20%, incentivizing more staking.

Liquid Staking

A drawback of traditional staking is that your tokens are locked and can't be used elsewhere. Liquid staking solves this: protocols like Lido (on Ethereum) and similar services on MST issue a derivative token (e.g., stMSTC) that represents your staked MSTC plus accruing rewards. You can then use stMSTC in DeFi - as collateral in lending, in liquidity pools, or in yield farms - earning staking rewards AND DeFi yields simultaneously. This composability is one of DeFi's most powerful features.

06   Technical Deep Dive - Yield Farming

The Art of Maximizing Returns Across DeFi

Yield farming is the practice of combining DeFi protocols to generate the highest possible APY. A typical strategy might involve three or four protocols layered together. Let's trace a concrete example.

Example: MSTC-USDC Farm on MST

  1. Provide liquidity: Deposit equal values of MSTC and USDC into the MSTC-USDC liquidity pool on a DEX. You receive LP tokens representing your share of the pool. You earn 0.3% of all swap fees.
  2. Stake LP tokens: Take those LP tokens and stake them in a "reward farm" contract. The farm pays additional rewards in its governance token (e.g., FARM). You now earn trading fees + FARM tokens.
  3. Harvest and compound: Every week, claim your FARM tokens. Sell half for USDC and half for MSTC. Add both to the liquidity pool (increasing your LP share), then stake the new LP tokens back into the farm. Your position grows exponentially.
  4. Monitor and exit: If the price of FARM drops too much, or if impermanent loss on the MSTC-USDC pool is cutting into profits, you may exit - unstake LP tokens, withdraw liquidity, and move capital elsewhere.

The effective APY can be calculated as: APY = Base Pool APR + Reward Token APR, but with price risk. If FARM token price drops, the reward APR in dollar terms decreases. Farmers must constantly assess whether the additional reward token emissions justify the risk of holding a volatile token.

07   veTokenomics

Locking Tokens for Power and Profit - The Curve Model

Curve Finance introduced veTokenomics to solve a problem: mercenary capital. In typical yield farming, whales would deposit large amounts, extract rewards, and leave - adding no long-term value to the protocol. veTokens change that. When you lock CRV (Curve's token) for a period, you receive veCRV. The longer you lock, the more veCRV you get. veCRV is non-transferable and decays linearly over time toward zero. But while you hold it, you get:

  • Boosted rewards: Your liquidity provision rewards can be boosted up to 2.5× based on your veCRV balance.
  • Governance power: veCRV holders vote on gauge weights - directing future CRV emissions to their preferred pools.
  • Fee sharing: Some protocols share a portion of trading fees with veToken holders.

This mechanism creates a powerful alignment: the users who govern the protocol are the same ones who have locked tokens for years, ensuring they make decisions that benefit the long-term health of the ecosystem. Other protocols like Balancer (veBAL) and Frax (veFXS) have adopted similar models. On MST, future DeFi protocols could implement veTokenomics, leveraging low gas costs to make even small locks practical - since creating or extending a lock costs a transaction.

08   Auto-Compounding & MST Advantages

Why MST's Sub-Paisa Fees Turn Small Farms into Gold Mines

Compounding - reinvesting your earnings to generate more earnings - is the most powerful force in finance. But in DeFi, every reinvestment costs gas. On Ethereum, a single harvest and compound cycle can cost ₹500-₹2,000. For a position earning ₹100 per week, compounding weekly is impossible - you'd lose money on every cycle. This forces small farmers to compound only monthly or quarterly, severely reducing their effective APY.

On MST, with gas costs of ~₹0.006 per transaction, you can compound every hour if you want, and it would cost less than a rupee a month. This allows:

  • Small positions to be profitable: A ₹5,000 farm can be actively managed without gas eroding returns.
  • Auto-compounding vaults: Protocols like Yearn can deploy on MST, offering auto-compounding strategies that run every few minutes - boosting APY to near its mathematical maximum.
  • Daily harvesting: Farmers can harvest and reinvest daily, capturing every bit of compound interest without worrying about cost.
MST's advantage in one number: Compounding daily vs monthly on a 50% APR farm yields an effective APY of ~64.8% vs ~50% - a 30% increase in returns. On Ethereum, the gas cost makes daily compounding a fantasy. On MST, it's reality.

10   Real-World Case Studies

How Yield Farming Built (and Sometimes Broke) Protocols

🏦
Compound - The Birth of Liquidity Mining
In June 2020, Compound launched its COMP token and began distributing it to lenders and borrowers. Overnight, the TVL of Compound exploded from $100M to over $1B as farmers rushed in. The strategy was simple: deposit stablecoins, earn interest + COMP, borrow against the deposit, earn COMP on the borrow (yes, borrowers earned COMP too), repeat. This "yield farming" loop drove the first DeFi summer and proved that token incentives could bootstrap massive liquidity. However, it also created mercenary capital that left when incentives dried up - a problem veTokenomics later solved.
🔄
Curve - The veTokenomics Pioneer
Curve Finance launched its CRV token in August 2020, introducing the vote-escrow model. Users lock CRV for up to 4 years to receive veCRV, boosting their liquidity rewards up to 2.5× and gaining governance power over gauge weights. This created the "Curve Wars" - protocols bribing veCRV holders to direct emissions to their pools. veTokenomics aligned incentives so well that Curve became the dominant stablecoin exchange, with protocols building entire strategies around its emissions.
🤖
Yearn Finance - The Auto-Compounding Vault
Yearn Finance, launched by Andre Cronje in 2020, created "vaults" - smart contracts that automatically move user funds between the highest-yielding strategies. A user deposits USDC into a Yearn vault, and the vault automatically lends it on Aave, provides liquidity on Curve, or farms governance tokens - all optimized by automated strategies. Yearn's success demonstrated that the future of yield farming is automation. On MST, such vaults could run even more efficiently, with strategies rebalancing every block instead of daily.
11   Common Pitfalls

Don't Lose Your Crop - Avoid These Yield Farming Traps

⚠️
Chasing APY without understanding the source
A 1000% APY usually means the reward token is highly inflationary and will crash in price. Calculate returns in stablecoin terms, not in the reward token's current price. If a farm pays 1000% in a token that drops 90% in a month, your real return is 10% - and you might have impermanent loss on top.
⚠️
Forgetting about impermanent loss
When you provide liquidity to a volatile pair, impermanent loss can wipe out your trading fees and reward token gains. Always check the IL calculator for your pair's expected volatility. If the tokens are highly correlated (e.g., two stablecoins), IL is minimal. If they're volatile (e.g., MSTC and a new governance token), IL can be severe.
⚠️
Smart contract risk - the hidden rug pull
Yield farming involves interacting with multiple protocols, each with its own smart contracts. A bug in any one of them could lead to loss of funds. Stick to audited, battle-tested protocols with high TVL. New farms offering insane APYs are often scams or have unaudited code. Remember: if it looks too good to be true, it probably is.

13   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 11.4

Staking
Locking tokens in a protocol to earn rewards, either for network security (PoS) or to signal long-term commitment (governance).
Yield Farming
The active practice of moving crypto assets between protocols to maximize returns, often involving lending, LPing, and reward token harvesting.
Liquid Staking
A derivative token (e.g., stMSTC) that represents staked tokens, allowing them to be used in DeFi while still earning staking rewards.
veTokenomics
Voting-escrow tokenomics - a mechanism where locking governance tokens grants boosted rewards and voting power, aligning long-term incentives.
APY
Annual Percentage Yield - the effective annual return including compounding. Higher than APR for the same nominal rate if compounded.
LP Tokens
Tokens received for providing liquidity to a DEX pool, representing the provider's share of the pool and entitling them to fees.
