02   Real-World Introduction Story

Four Builders, Four Chains, One Frustration

📖 Story

Ravi works at a small money transfer shop in Kochi. He sends remittances for families whose relatives work in the Gulf. In 2021 he heard about Bitcoin - "send money anywhere, no bank needed." He convinced his uncle in Dubai to buy ₹50,000 worth of Bitcoin and send it to his wallet in India. The transaction took 47 minutes to get one confirmation. By then, the price of Bitcoin had dropped 4%. And the network fee? ₹1,200. That is more than the 2% commission the local bank charges. Ravi's uncle never used Bitcoin again.


Ananya is a DeFi enthusiast in Mumbai. She spends her evenings yield farming and providing liquidity on Uniswap. In May 2022, during the Otherside NFT mint, Ethereum gas fees spiked to over ₹8,000 for a single transaction. Ananya had ₹15,000 stuck in a liquidity pool and could not withdraw it without paying more in gas than the funds were worth. Her money was technically hers - but economically trapped. She watched helplessly as a smart contract bug was announced and the pool was drained before she could act.


Karan runs a small game studio in Pune. His team built a play to earn game on Solana because the chain promised fast, cheap transactions for in game purchases. On 14 September 2021, Solana went down for 17 hours. Players could not log in, items could not be traded, and the in game economy froze. Karan's Discord exploded with angry users demanding refunds. The network that promised Visa level speed delivered a complete blackout - with no backup and no estimated recovery time.


Deepak is a supply chain developer for a pharmaceutical company in Hyderabad. He chose Polygon to build a track and trace system because it was "Ethereum compatible but cheap." In March 2022, the Ronin bridge (used by Axie Infinity, built on a similar sidechain architecture) was hacked for $620 million. Deepak's boss called him into a meeting and asked: "Is our system safe?" Deepak had to explain that any sidechain inherits the security of its bridge - and bridges are the single biggest attack surface in crypto.


Four people. Four different chains. Four different failures. But a single underlying truth: each of these blockchains solved one problem while creating another. Bitcoin solved decentralised money but cannot scale. Ethereum solved programmability but priced out ordinary users. Solana solved speed but sacrificed reliability. Polygon solved fees but created bridge risk. None of them solved all three pillars of the blockchain trilemma. And until someone does, Web3 will remain a promise - not a reality.

03   Background & History

The Blockchain Trilemma - And Why Every Chain Stumbled

In 2017, Vitalik Buterin coined the term "blockchain trilemma." The idea is simple: a blockchain wants three things - security, scalability, and decentralisation. But improving one typically weakens another. It is like trying to make a single vehicle that is simultaneously the fastest Formula 1 car, the safest Volvo, and the most fuel efficient Maruti Suzuki. You cannot maximise all three at once. You have to pick.

₿
Bitcoin (2009) - Maximum Security, Minimum Scalability
Satoshi chose security and decentralisation above all else. Bitcoin's Proof of Work requires miners to burn staggering amounts of electricity - currently more than the entire country of Argentina. This makes the network incredibly secure: to reverse a Bitcoin transaction, you would need to control more than 50% of the world's mining power, which would cost tens of billions of dollars. But the cost of that security is speed. Bitcoin processes about 7 transactions per second. Visa processes 24,000. You cannot buy chai with Bitcoin unless you are willing to wait an hour and pay a fee that is higher than the chai itself.
Ξ
Ethereum (2015) - Programmability Unleashed, But at What Price?
Vitalik Buterin looked at Bitcoin and said: "What if the blockchain could run programs, not just send money?" Ethereum introduced smart contracts - self executing code on a global computer. This gave birth to DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, and the entire Web3 ecosystem. But the global computer has a bottleneck: every node in the network must execute every transaction. When demand spikes (an NFT mint, a DeFi frenzy), the network congests. Gas fees explode. In May 2022, a single Uniswap trade cost over ₹20,000 in gas. Ethereum became a chain for the rich.
◎
Solana (2020) - Speed Demon, Fragile Engine
Solana's pitch was breathtaking: 65,000 transactions per second, sub second finality, fees of a fraction of a paisa. It achieved this by using a different consensus mechanism - Proof of History - and by requiring validator nodes to use extremely powerful hardware. The trade off was decentralisation and reliability. Solana has suffered over 10 major outages since launch, including a 17 hour blackout in September 2021 and a 20 hour halt in February 2023. Each time, the entire network stopped. No transactions. No DeFi. No NFTs. Just silence.
⬡
Polygon (2020) - Cheap and Cheerful, but Who Is Guarding the Door?
Polygon offered a brilliant shortcut: instead of building a new Layer 1, it created a sidechain that runs alongside Ethereum. Transactions on Polygon are fast and cheap because they do not go through Ethereum's mainnet - they are processed on Polygon's own validators. But the security model is fundamentally different. Polygon relies on a bridge to move assets between Ethereum and Polygon. That bridge is a smart contract - and smart contracts can be hacked. Over $2 billion was stolen from cross chain bridges in 2022 alone. When you use Polygon, you are trusting that bridge code is flawless. History suggests otherwise.
🗝️ The Trilemma Table - Seeing the Trade Offs

Every blockchain is a set of compromises. Bitcoin chose security and decentralisation, sacrificing scalability. Ethereum chose programmability but inherited scalability problems. Solana chose speed but compromised on reliability and decentralisation. Polygon chose low fees but introduced bridge risk. There is no free lunch in blockchain design - at least, there was not until MST's approach to the trilemma.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Blockchain Trilemma - Explained Like You Are Choosing a Vehicle

The blockchain trilemma is not a technical paper. It is a real world design constraint. Think of it like choosing a vehicle for your daily commute.

🚗 The Vehicle Analogy

Bitcoin is a tank. Incredibly secure, almost impossible to destroy. But it moves at 7 kilometres per hour and consumes a tanker of fuel every kilometre. Perfect for storing national gold reserves. Terrible for buying groceries.

Ethereum is a luxury bus. It can carry many passengers and has all kinds of fancy features - TV screens, WiFi, reclining seats. But the ticket price changes every minute based on demand. During rush hour, a ticket can cost more than the bus itself.

Solana is a Formula 1 race car. Blindingly fast. Built for the track. But every few months, the engine blows up. The whole car stops. You wait on the side of the road until a tow truck arrives. Fantastic when it works. But can you trust it for a cross country trip?

Polygon is a shuttle bus that drives alongside the highway. It is fast and cheap because it avoids the main road's toll gates. But to get from the highway to the shuttle, you cross a narrow bridge. If that bridge collapses, everything on the shuttle is lost.

W
WHY
Why do these trade offs exist?
Because decentralisation is expensive. If you want thousands of independent computers around the world to all agree on a single version of the truth, that takes time and energy. Bitcoin and Ethereum prioritise this agreement above all else - hence the high cost and slow speed. Solana tried to speed it up by using fewer, more powerful computers - but that makes the network more fragile. Polygon bypassed the agreement entirely by running a separate chain - but that introduces bridge risk. Every design decision is a trade off.
W
WHAT
What is the specific shortcoming of each chain?
Bitcoin: 7 TPS, 10 minute block time, energy consumption of a medium country. Unusable for payments.
Ethereum: Gas fees that spike 100x during congestion, making simple transactions cost ₹5,000 to ₹10,000. Complex and unforgiving UX - one wrong click and you lose everything.
Solana: Multiple total network outages. Validator hardware costs over ₹50 lakhs, centralising the network among a few wealthy operators.
Polygon: Security depends entirely on bridge smart contracts. Over $2 billion stolen from bridges in 2022. Not a fully independent Layer 1.
H
HOW
How do these shortcomings affect real users?
Ravi cannot use Bitcoin for remittances because it is slower and more expensive than a bank. Ananya cannot use Ethereum DeFi during peak times because gas fees consume all her gains. Karan cannot trust Solana for his game because an outage kills the player experience. Deepak cannot use Polygon for enterprise supply chain because a bridge hack would destroy his company's credibility. Each chain fails at a different real world use case.
W
WHERE
Where do these chains still work reasonably well?
Bitcoin works as digital gold - a store of value for long term holding. Ethereum works for high value DeFi transactions where the gas fee is a small fraction of the amount moved. Solana works during periods of stability for high frequency trading apps. Polygon works for low stakes applications where users can tolerate bridge risk. But none of them are suitable for mass adoption, daily microtransactions, enterprise grade reliability, or onboarding the next billion users.
L
LIMITS
What are the fundamental limits that no upgrade can fix?
Bitcoin's 7 TPS is a design choice - not a temporary bug. Ethereum's Layer 2 rollups help, but introduce their own complexity and fragmentation. Solana's hardware requirements mean the network will always tend toward centralisation. Polygon's sidechain model will always depend on a bridge. These are architectural limitations, not temporary issues. They cannot be patched - they can only be replaced by a better architecture.
05   Technical Deep Dive

Chain by Chain - The Specific Technical Failures

Now we move from stories to specifications. As a blockchain developer, you need to know exactly what breaks, why it breaks, and how to spot the trade off in any new chain someone pitches to you.

₿
Bitcoin - The Scalability Ceiling

Bitcoin's block size is capped at 1 megabyte. Each block is produced approximately every 10 minutes. Simple arithmetic: a typical Bitcoin transaction is about 250 bytes. That gives a theoretical maximum of roughly 4,000 transactions per block, or 7 transactions per second. For a global payment system, that is hopelessly inadequate.

The real world is worse. During peak demand, fees skyrocket. In April 2021, the average Bitcoin transaction fee reached $62. For Ravi's ₹50,000 remittance, that would be ₹4,600 - nearly 10% of the amount sent. The very people who need decentralised money the most - the unbanked, the underbanked - are priced out.

Bitcoin's Lightning Network was supposed to solve this by moving small transactions off chain. But Lightning requires users to open and close channels on chain (paying fees twice), manage channel liquidity, and stay online to receive payments. It is not a seamless solution - it is a complex workaround that still confuses experienced developers.

Developer's lens: Bitcoin has no smart contract language beyond a limited scripting system. You cannot build a DeFi protocol, an NFT marketplace, or a supply chain dApp on Bitcoin. It is a single purpose chain - digital gold - and it was never designed to be anything else.

Ξ
Ethereum - The Gas Auction from Hell

Ethereum is a world computer where every operation costs "gas." A simple transfer of ETH costs 21,000 gas. A Uniswap swap costs 150,000 to 300,000 gas. A complex DeFi interaction can cost 500,000 gas or more. The gas price is set by an auction: users bid against each other to get their transactions included in the next block.

When the network is congested, this auction becomes brutal. In May 2022, gas prices hit 1,500 gwei. At that price, a Uniswap swap cost over ₹20,000. A failed transaction - where the contract reverts due to an error - still consumes the gas. You pay thousands of rupees for a transaction that does nothing.

Ethereum's transition to Proof of Stake in September 2022 reduced energy consumption by 99.9%, but it did not reduce gas fees. Gas fees are a function of block space demand, not consensus mechanism. The Merge was about security and sustainability - not scalability. Sharding, which was supposed to increase throughput, has been delayed repeatedly and replaced by a rollup centric roadmap that pushes complexity to Layer 2.

Developer's lens: Deploying a smart contract on Ethereum mainnet can cost ₹50,000 to ₹2,00,000 in gas alone. Every bug fix, every upgrade, every new feature requires a fresh deployment and another gas bill. This makes iterative development financially punishing.

◎
Solana - Speed Without a Safety Net

Solana's architecture is radically different. It uses Proof of History - a cryptographic clock that orders transactions before they are processed, allowing validators to execute them in parallel. This parallel execution is what gives Solana its claimed 65,000 TPS. But it comes with a cost.

Solana validators must run extremely powerful hardware: 12 core CPUs, 128 GB of RAM, high end SSDs. The cost of running a validator is estimated at ₹50 lakhs per year or more. This prices out small operators. The network ends up dominated by a few dozen well funded validators - exactly the centralisation that blockchain was supposed to prevent.

More critically, Solana has no mempool - no waiting area for pending transactions. When the network is overloaded, transactions are simply dropped. Bots and arbitrage traders flood the network with spam transactions during high demand periods, causing validators to fork. The network stops producing blocks. Engineers must manually coordinate a restart - a process that took 17 hours in September 2021 and 20 hours in February 2023.

Developer's lens: Building on Solana means betting that the network will not go down during your critical launch window. For a game studio like Karan's, an outage during a tournament or an NFT mint is catastrophic. For DeFi protocols, an outage means users cannot liquidate positions during a market crash - and they will sue.

⬡
Polygon - The Bridge Is the Weakest Link

Polygon is not a standalone Layer 1. It is a sidechain - a separate blockchain that runs alongside Ethereum and uses its own validators. Transactions on Polygon are cheap because they do not pay Ethereum gas. But when you move assets from Ethereum to Polygon (or back), you use a bridge - a smart contract that locks tokens on one chain and mints equivalent tokens on the other.

Bridges are complex. They involve multiple smart contracts, validator sets, and cryptographic proofs. A single bug in any of these components can be exploited. In 2022 alone, the Wormhole bridge lost $320 million, the Ronin bridge lost $620 million, and the Nomad bridge lost $190 million. That is over $1.1 billion stolen from bridges in one year.

Polygon's bridge has not been hacked - yet. But it is secured by a multisig of 5 of 8 signers. If an attacker compromises 5 keys, they can drain the bridge. This is not a hypothetical risk - multisig compromises have happened repeatedly in crypto history. Deepak's pharmaceutical company cannot accept "it has not been hacked yet" as a security guarantee.

Developer's lens: When you build on Polygon, you are not building on Ethereum. You are building on a chain that depends on Ethereum for its ultimate security, but processes transactions independently. The bridge is a single point of failure. If the bridge is exploited, all assets on Polygon become worthless.

Architectural insight: Every chain's strength is also its weakness. Bitcoin's simplicity is its security - and its limitation. Ethereum's programmability is its genius - and its congestion. Solana's speed is its selling point - and its fragility. Polygon's low fees are its advantage - and its bridge risk. A chain that solves all three aspects of the trilemma simultaneously has never existed. Until MST.

10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 6.2

Blockchain Trilemma
The design constraint that a blockchain cannot simultaneously maximise security, scalability, and decentralisation. Improving one typically weakens at least one of the others.
Proof of Work (PoW)
Bitcoin's consensus mechanism where miners compete to solve cryptographic puzzles. Extremely secure but energy intensive and slow (7 TPS).
Gas Fee
The unit of computational cost on Ethereum. Users bid gas prices to get transactions included; during congestion, fees can exceed ₹10,000 per transaction.
Proof of History (PoH)
Solana's cryptographic clock that timestamps transactions before processing, enabling parallel execution. Enables high speed but contributes to network fragility under spam conditions.
Sidechain
A separate blockchain that runs alongside a main chain (like Ethereum) and uses a bridge to transfer assets. Polygon is a sidechain. Sidechains are fast and cheap but introduce bridge risk.
Bridge
A smart contract that locks tokens on one chain and mints equivalent tokens on another. Bridges are complex and have been repeatedly exploited - over $2 billion stolen in 2022.
EVM Compatibility
The ability of a blockchain to run smart contracts written in Solidity, using the same tooling as Ethereum. Polygon is EVM compatible; Solana is not (uses Rust).
The Merge
Ethereum's transition from Proof of Work to Proof of Stake in September 2022. Reduced energy use by 99.9% but did not reduce gas fees - scalability remains a separate challenge.
Mempool
A waiting area for pending transactions before they are included in a block. Bitcoin and Ethereum have mempools; Solana does not - contributing to its outage vulnerability.
Validator Hardware Requirements
The computing power needed to run a node. Solana's high requirements (~₹50 lakhs/year) centralise the network among wealthy operators. Bitcoin and Ethereum have lower barriers.
