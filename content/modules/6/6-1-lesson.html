02   Real-World Introduction Story

Four People, One Trap - A Day in the Life of Web2

📖 Story

Meera runs a cloud kitchen in Pune. She sells butter chicken on a popular food delivery app. Last month her total orders were ₹3,50,000. After the app took its 28% commission, deducted GST on that commission, subtracted packaging costs, and forced her to co-fund a “Free Delivery” promotion, Meera was left with ₹1,60,000. Out of that she paid ₹90,000 for ingredients, ₹40,000 in rent and electricity, and ₹20,000 to her two helpers. Her own salary? Zero. The app made over ₹98,000 from her orders - without cooking a single meal.


Vikram drives an auto-rickshaw in Bengaluru, but he also drives for a ride hailing platform. Yesterday he completed 14 trips and earned ₹2,100 in fares. The platform took ₹525 (25%). Fuel cost ₹600. Vehicle EMI ₹350. Vikram took home ₹625 for 11 hours of work - that's ₹56 per hour, less than the minimum wage for unskilled labour in Karnataka. The platform made ₹525 from Vikram's labour - without owning the vehicle, buying the fuel, or bearing the accident risk.


Priya sells handmade jewellery on a popular e commerce marketplace. A necklace priced at ₹800 is sold. The marketplace charges 15% referral fee (₹120), a closing fee of ₹30, and shipping of ₹60. She also pays 18% GST on the fees. Priya's net realisation: ₹575. The raw materials cost ₹350. Her margin is ₹225 for 3 hours of intricate work - ₹75 per hour. The marketplace earned over ₹170 from this one sale simply by connecting Priya to a buyer. Priya cannot even email the buyer to tell them about her new collection - the marketplace owns that relationship.


Arjun is a tech educator on YouTube. His channel has 5 lakh subscribers. Last month a video explaining pointers in C got 3 lakh views. The ad revenue was ₹48,000. YouTube took its 45% cut - ₹21,600. Arjun received ₹26,400. Then YouTube changed its advertiser friendly guidelines retroactively - three older videos were demonetised without warning. Arjun lost ₹15,000 of his projected income overnight. He has no way to reach his subscribers outside YouTube - the platform holds his audience hostage.


These are not isolated sob stories. They are the inevitable mathematics of a centralised platform economy. In every case, the person doing the actual work - cooking, driving, creating, making - earns a fraction of the value they generate. The platform that owns the database, the matching algorithm, and the customer relationship captures the lion's share. And if any of these four people try to leave, they leave behind their entire business. The platform is not a partner. It is a rent seeking landlord - and the rent keeps going up.

We call this the Web2 trap. It is the single most important reason why blockchain exists - because blockchain is, at its core, a technology for removing the need to trust a central rent seeker. Understanding this trap in granular, human detail is the foundation for everything you will learn in the MST ecosystem.

03   Background & History

From Open Internet to Digital Feudalism - How We Got Here

The internet was born as a decentralised network. In the 1990s, anyone could set up a server, publish a website, and reach the world - no permission needed. The early web was a chaotic, vibrant, peer to peer playground. So what changed? The short answer: aggregation beats distribution when it is easier for users.

🏗️
Phase 1: The Aggregator Gold Rush (2005 - 2012)
Companies realised they could build a single website that gathered all the scattered suppliers in one place. Expedia aggregated hotels. Amazon aggregated sellers. YouTube aggregated videos. The pitch was irresistible: “We will bring you customers - just pay us a small cut.” In exchange for discovery, suppliers handed over their customer relationships. At first the cut was small - 5%, 10% - because platforms were fighting for market share. But the architecture was already centralised: the platform owned the database.
📱
Phase 2: The Smartphone Lock-In (2010 - 2016)
The iPhone and Android turned aggregators into apps. Now a platform was not just a website - it lived on your home screen, collected your location, your contacts, your usage patterns. App stores added another 30% tax on top. In India, the Jio revolution brought 500 million new users online, all of them accessing the internet primarily through apps owned by a handful of companies. The web became a walled garden.
📈
Phase 3: Network Effects Become Moats (2016 - 2020)
Once a food delivery app has most of the restaurants and most of the hungry customers, a new competitor cannot break in - even with a better product. Restaurants will not leave because that is where the orders are. Customers will not leave because that is where their favourite restaurants are. This is the network effect moat. The platform no longer needs to be the best; it just needs to be the biggest. And now that the moat is built, the platform can raise its fees. And they did.
💰
Phase 4: The Harvest (2020 - Now)
With competition crushed and providers locked in, platforms switched from growth to extraction. Commissions that were 10% five years ago are now 28%. Delivery fees, surge pricing, and mandatory promotions erode provider income further. Algorithms are tuned not for the best match but for maximum platform revenue. Creators, drivers, sellers, and restaurateurs are working harder than ever - and earning less. The platform lifecycle is complete: subsidise, dominate, lock, extract.
🗝️ The Developer's Realisation

As a developer, you might think: “I just build the features - the business model is someone else's problem.” But the architecture you choose is the business model. A centralised database with one admin who can read, write, and delete is a system where one party has absolute power. A blockchain is a system where power is distributed by code. Every line of Solidity you write is a choice: do you build another digital landlord, or do you build a digital commons?

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Web2 Trap - Explained Like You Are Catching Up with a Friend

Let us strip away the jargon. The Web2 trap is simply this: someone else owns the room where you do business, and they charge you rent for every single transaction - forever. The more successful you become inside that room, the more rent you pay. And you can never leave, because all your customers are inside that room and you do not have their phone numbers.

🏢 The Architect & the Building Owner Analogy

Imagine you are an architect. You design beautiful houses. A company called “BuildConnect” says: “Set up your office inside our building. We will bring clients to you. We only take 20% of every project fee.” Sounds fair. Years pass. Your reputation grows - you have designed 200 houses. But BuildConnect now takes 30%. They decide which clients see your profile. They also sell your floor plans to other architects without paying you. And if you try to move out, you walk away with nothing - no client list, no portfolio, no reviews. All of that belongs to BuildConnect. You built a career inside someone else's building. That is the Web2 trap.

W
WHY
Why does the Web2 trap happen?
Because Web2 platforms are built as centralised databases. One company controls the server, the code, and all the data. That company's goal is to maximise profit - and the most reliable way to do that is to increase the rent it charges to both sides of the market. Once users are locked in by network effects, the platform has no incentive to lower fees. The architecture guarantees exploitation.
W
WHAT
What exactly gets trapped?
Three things: (1) Providers - the people who actually create the value: chefs, drivers, artisans, creators. They cannot leave without losing their entire income. (2) Consumers - their data, preferences, and social connections are locked inside the platform; switching means starting from zero. (3) Data itself - the most valuable resource of the 21st century. The platform collects it for free and monetises it for billions, while the people who generated the data see none of that value.
H
HOW
How does the platform maintain control?
Through four levers: (1) Commission - a percentage fee on every transaction. (2) Data moat - hoarding all customer information so providers cannot build independent relationships. (3) Algorithmic control - deciding who gets seen and who gets buried, with zero transparency. (4) Deplatforming power - the ability to delete your account and destroy your business with a single click.
W
WHERE
Where do we see this trap in everyday life?
Everywhere. Food delivery (Swiggy/Zomato), ride hailing (Ola/Uber), e commerce (Amazon/Flipkart), home services (Urban Company), hotel booking (OYO), content creation (YouTube/Instagram), even online dating. Any app that sits between two groups and takes a cut is operating this model. The only question is how far along the extraction curve they are.
L
LIMITS
Can we fix this trap while staying within Web2?
Not structurally. Regulation can cap commissions (like the EU's Digital Markets Act), but it cannot change the fact that a single company owns the database. That company will always find new ways to extract value - through advertising, data sales, or priority placement. The only permanent solution is to change the architecture: from one company owning the platform to the community owning the protocol. That is the blockchain thesis in one sentence.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The Four Levers of Platform Rent Seeking - Anatomy of a Trap

The Web2 trap is not a random accident of capitalism. It is a precise, engineered system with four interlocking components. As a blockchain developer, you need to understand each lever not just economically, but architecturally - because your code will replace these levers with smart contracts.

1
Lever 1: Commission Extraction - The Percentage Tax

Every time a transaction flows through the platform, the platform takes a cut. In food delivery, it is 23 to 28%. In ride hailing, it is 20 to 25%. In app stores, it is 15 to 30%. In e commerce marketplaces, total fees can reach 45% when you add referral fees, closing fees, shipping charges, and advertising.

The crucial point: the percentage rises over time. When platforms were competing for market share, commissions were low (5 to 10%). Once they won, commissions jumped. This is not a one time tax - it is a permanent, growing rent on every transaction forever. And because the platform processes the payment, the provider has no choice: if they want to get paid, they pay the tax.

Developer's lens: In a Web2 app, the payment flow is Customer → Platform → Provider (minus cut). In a Web3 dApp on MST, the flow is Customer → Smart Contract → Provider, with a gas fee of fractions of a paisa. The platform's 28% rent is replaced by an open protocol's sub paisa fee. The difference in take rate is not 10% - it is 10,000x.

2
Lever 2: Data Moat - The Information Asymmetry Weapon

The platform captures every interaction: who clicked what, who ordered when, who abandoned the cart, who lives near whom. This data is a goldmine. The platform uses it to optimise its own revenue - showing you the restaurant that pays the highest commission, not necessarily the one you would like best. But the restaurant never sees this data.

Imagine Priya, the jewellery seller. She does not know that 40% of people who viewed her necklace also looked at earrings. If she knew that, she could create a combo offer. But Amazon knows - and Amazon sells the data to big brands or uses it to launch its own competing jewellery line. The data asymmetry means the platform always has more information than the provider - and information is power.

Developer's lens: In a centralised system, all data sits in one database controlled by one entity. That entity can read, analyse, and monetise it freely. In a blockchain system, data is on a public ledger. Privacy can be preserved with zero knowledge proofs, but the key point is: no single party gets a god view of everyone's behaviour. The data moat collapses.

3
Lever 3: Algorithmic Control - The Invisible Hand That Crushes You

Who decides which restaurant appears first when you search “biryani near me”? The platform's algorithm. That algorithm is a black box - proprietary, unauditable, and tuned for one thing: platform profit per search. A restaurant can drop from rank 3 to rank 30 overnight based on a metric it does not control and cannot see.

YouTube's recommendation algorithm is legendary for this. A creator's video that was getting 100,000 views per day can suddenly stop being recommended - with no explanation. The creator's income halved, and they have zero recourse. The algorithm is the platform's ultimate weapon: it can make or break any provider silently, without accountability.

Developer's lens: In Web3, ranking logic can be encoded in open source smart contracts. The rules are transparent. If the community wants to change the ranking formula, they vote on it. No black box. No secret tuning. The algorithm becomes a public good, not a corporate weapon.

4
Lever 4: Deplatforming - The Nuclear Option

At any moment, for any reason - or no reason - the platform can delete your account. Your 5 star rating, your 2,000 positive reviews, your 5 lakh subscribers, your 10 years of transaction history - gone. You have no recourse, no appeal, no backup. You are a tenant, not an owner.

This is not theoretical. Creators get demonetised for “advertiser friendly” violations that did not exist when they uploaded the video. Drivers get deactivated after a single customer complaint, even if it is false. Small sellers get suspended for “policy violations” that are never clearly specified. The threat of deplatforming keeps everyone compliant - and terrified.

Developer's lens: On a blockchain, your reputation is on chain. Your transaction history, your reviews, your credentials - they live on a public ledger that no single entity can erase. A protocol can have community moderation rules, but it cannot unilaterally delete your existence. This is the difference between tenancy and ownership.

Architectural insight: All four levers are made possible by a single design decision - centralisation of the database and the business logic. The platform owns the only copy of the truth. Once you distribute the data and make the logic transparent and immutable, none of these levers can exist in their current form. That is not an incremental improvement - it is a category change.

08   Real-World Case Studies

The Web2 Trap in India - Same Pattern, Different Industries

🍛
Food Delivery - The Restaurant Squeeze
The National Restaurant Association of India (NRAI) has been locked in a public battle with Swiggy and Zomato since 2019. Their complaints: commissions have risen from ~10% to 23 - 28%, mandatory discounting forces restaurants to sell below cost, customer data is never shared, and cloud kitchens owned by the platforms themselves get preferential placement. In 2024, the NRAI filed a formal complaint with the Competition Commission of India (CCI) alleging anti competitive practices. Meanwhile, restaurants report that delivery orders now lose money - they treat it as a marketing expense, hoping to convert customers to dine in. The platform that was supposed to be a growth channel has become a necessary evil.
🚗
Ride Hailing - The Driver's Disappearing Income
A 2023 survey by the Indian Federation of App based Transport Workers (IFAT) found that driver incomes have dropped 30 to 40% since 2019, while platform commissions have risen. Drivers report working 12 to 14 hours a day to earn ₹15,000 to 20,000 a month - net of fuel, EMI, and maintenance. The platform sets surge pricing unilaterally (taking the upside), reduces incentives arbitrarily, and can deactivate drivers after a few customer complaints with no hearing. The driver bears all the risk - vehicle loan, accident, health - and the platform bears none. It is a classic principal agent problem where all power sits with the principal.
📦
E Commerce - The Seller's Margins Evaporate
Small sellers on Amazon.in and Flipkart face a cascade of fees: referral fee (2 to 20% depending on category), closing fee, shipping fee, storage fee (if using FBA), and advertising fee to get visibility. A seller of a ₹500 product can easily pay ₹150 to 200 in total platform charges. Worse, Amazon has been accused of using seller data to launch its own private label products that compete directly with the sellers whose data it mined - a practice that is the subject of a CCI investigation. The seller builds the product, manages inventory, and handles returns; the platform controls discovery, sets the rules, and captures the margin.
🎥
Content Creation - The Audience Is Never Yours
Indian YouTubers, Instagram influencers, and even journalists on X (Twitter) have built massive audiences - only to discover they do not own those audiences. YouTube's algorithm can reduce a channel's views by 80% with a single update. Monetisation policies change retroactively - a video that earned ₹50,000 last year can be demonetised this year under new guidelines. Instagram's feed algorithm buries posts from creators unless they pay for “boosting.” The creator invests years building a brand, but the platform owns the distribution. As one creator put it: “I have 2 million followers, but if Instagram bans me tomorrow, I have zero.”
⚠️ The Pattern Across All Industries

Food, transport, retail, media - four completely different sectors, one identical structure. A central company owns the discovery, the transaction, and the relationship. The people who actually create the value - cooking, driving, making, filming - are reduced to interchangeable, replaceable providers. This is not a bug in Web2. It is the natural equilibrium of a system where one party controls the infrastructure.

09   Assessment Preparation

Top Facts - Know These Cold

01
The Web2 Trap Definition
A centralised platform inserts itself between providers and consumers, extracts a rising commission, and locks both sides in through data hoarding, algorithmic control, and network effects. The value creators (providers) earn shrinking margins, while the platform captures the majority of the value without bearing the operational risk.
02
The Four Rent Seeking Levers
(1) Commission extraction - a rising percentage tax on every transaction. (2) Data moat - hoarding all customer data so providers cannot build independent businesses. (3) Algorithmic control - opaque ranking and visibility rules that optimise for platform profit. (4) Deplatforming power - the ability to erase a provider's entire business with a single click.
03
The Platform Lifecycle
Subsidise growth → achieve market dominance → lock in both sides → raise commissions → extract maximum value. This cycle is observed across every major Web2 platform, from Uber to YouTube.
04
Web2 vs Web3 - The Architectural Difference
Web2 runs on a centralised database owned by a single company. Web3 runs on a distributed ledger governed by a community. The former enables rent seeking; the latter makes it structurally impossible because no single party controls the data, the logic, or the payment rails.
05
Why Regulation Alone Cannot Fix It
Regulation can temporarily cap commissions, but the underlying centralised architecture remains. The platform will always find new ways to monetise its god view of the data. The only lasting fix is to change the architecture - from a rent seeking middleman to an open, community owned protocol. That is the entire purpose of blockchain.

12   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 6.1

Web2 Trap
The economic structure where a centralised platform inserts itself as a mandatory intermediary, locks in providers and consumers, and extracts an ever increasing rent (commission, data, visibility) without bearing the operational risk.
Platform Rent Seeking
The practice of capturing a large share of the value created by others simply by controlling the marketplace infrastructure - analogous to a landlord collecting rent without maintaining the building.
Data Moat
A competitive barrier created by hoarding all user interaction data, so that providers cannot independently reach or understand their own customers - locking them into the platform forever.
Algorithmic Control
The use of opaque, unauditable algorithms to determine visibility, pricing, and promotions. The platform can boost or bury any provider silently, with no accountability.
Deplatforming
The ability of a platform to delete a provider's account instantly, erasing years of reputation, customer relationships, and transaction history - the ultimate power in a centralised system.
Network Effect Moat
The lock in that occurs when a platform has most of the providers and most of the consumers - making it nearly impossible for either side to leave, because all the value is on the platform.
Take Rate
The percentage of each transaction that the platform keeps. In food delivery, 23 to 28%; in ride hailing, 20 to 25%; in app stores, 15 to 30%.
Self Sovereign Identity
A Web3 model where individuals own and control their digital identity and personal data, choosing when and with whom to share it - the opposite of a platform owning your data.
Token Governed Protocol
A system where the rules (fees, ranking, upgrades) are decided by token holders through transparent, on chain voting - rather than by a central CEO.
Gas Fee
A tiny fee paid to the blockchain network for processing a transaction. On MST, this is fractions of a paisa - compared to platform commissions that can be hundreds of rupees.
