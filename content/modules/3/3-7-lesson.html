02 · Real-World Introduction Story

The Day Ethereum Split in Two - The DAO Hack of 2016

📖 Story

June 17, 2016. A hacker exploits a vulnerability in "The DAO" - a decentralised autonomous organisation that had raised $150 million worth of ETH (the largest crowdfund in history at the time). The attacker begins draining funds through a recursive calling exploit, siphoning 3.6 million ETH (~$60 million) into a child contract. The Ethereum community watches in real time as weeks of crowdfunded capital disappears.


The crisis sparks an existential debate: should the Ethereum community alter the blockchain's history to return the stolen funds - or uphold the principle of "code is law" and let the theft stand?


Those who voted to intervene: "This is pragmatic. 14% of all ETH in existence was stolen. We can fix it."
Those who refused: "If we reverse this, blockchain is not immutable. We've proven that human consensus can override cryptographic guarantees. Everything is corrupt."


The community voted. The majority chose to create a hard fork - rewriting history so the stolen funds returned to their original owners. Ethereum was born anew. But a minority refused to join the new chain and continued on the original, unaltered blockchain - calling it Ethereum Classic (ETC). One network. Two communities. Two coins. Forever.


This is the story of a hard fork - and why upgrades to decentralised networks are never just technical decisions.

03 · What Is a Fork? - The Core Concept

When a Blockchain's Rules Change

A blockchain's rules - which transactions are valid, how blocks are structured, what the block size limit is - are called the protocol. When developers propose changes to the protocol, every node must decide whether to adopt the new rules. How many nodes upgrade, and whether the old rules are compatible with the new ones, determines what kind of fork occurs.

🔑 The Road Fork Analogy

Imagine a highway suddenly splits into two roads. If your car can drive on both roads - the old rules still get you somewhere - that's a soft fork: backward compatible. If the new road requires a completely different kind of vehicle - your old car simply can't drive there - that's a hard fork: a permanent divergence. Anyone still driving the old car is on a different network entirely.

🟢 Soft Fork - Backward Compatible
  • New rules are a subset of old rules - old nodes still accept new blocks
  • Only miners/validators need to upgrade to enforce new rules
  • Non-upgraded nodes remain on the same chain (they just don't enforce the new rule)
  • No chain split - one blockchain continues
  • Requires majority of mining/validator power to activate
  • Less disruptive - users don't need to do anything
🔴 Hard Fork - NOT Backward Compatible
  • New rules are incompatible with old rules - old nodes reject new blocks
  • Everyone must upgrade - or they stay on the old chain
  • If a significant minority refuses to upgrade: permanent chain split
  • Two separate coins, two separate communities, forever
  • May create replay attack risk - same TX valid on both chains
  • Examples: Ethereum vs ETC (2016), BTC vs BCH (2017)

05 · Major Forks in History

Every Significant Fork - What Happened and Why

Fork Name Type Chain When What Changed Outcome
SegWit (Segregated Witness) Soft Fork Bitcoin Aug 2017 Moved signature data outside the transaction body - effectively increasing block capacity without changing the 1 MB block size limit. Also fixed transaction malleability. Activated after long political battle. ~80% of Bitcoin transactions now use SegWit. Non-upgraded nodes still accept SegWit blocks. No chain split.
Bitcoin Cash (BCH) Hard Fork Bitcoin Aug 1, 2017 Increased block size from 1 MB to 8 MB (later 32 MB) to allow more transactions per block and lower fees - the "big blockers" solution to Bitcoin's scaling debate. Permanent split. BCH holders received 1 BCH for every BTC. BCH has ~1 - 2% of Bitcoin's market cap. Never achieved its "PayPal killer" ambitions.
Bitcoin SV (BSV) Hard Fork Bitcoin Cash Nov 2018 Craig Wright's faction split from BCH claiming to restore "Satoshi's original vision" - even larger blocks (128 MB) and additional opcodes for on-chain data storage. BCH split into BCH and BSV. Both had hash wars. BSV has minimal market share. Craig Wright's claim to be Satoshi has been legally challenged and largely rejected.
Ethereum DAO Fork Hard Fork Ethereum July 20, 2016 Returned 3.6M ETH stolen from The DAO by rewriting the blockchain state - effectively reversing a series of transactions that had already been mined and confirmed. Ethereum (ETH) continues on the forked chain. Ethereum Classic (ETC) continues on the original. First major demonstration that "immutable" blockchains can be altered by social consensus.
Ethereum "The Merge" Hard Fork Ethereum Sept 15, 2022 Switched Ethereum's consensus mechanism from PoW to PoS. The Beacon Chain (launched Dec 2020) merged with the original execution layer. Energy use dropped 99.95%. Smooth transition - no significant chain split (a tiny ETHW PoW fork briefly existed). Considered the most complex blockchain upgrade in history. Successfully executed.
Taproot Soft Fork Bitcoin Nov 2021 Added Schnorr signatures (replacing ECDSA) enabling signature aggregation, improved smart contract privacy (all complex scripts look like simple payments externally), and enabled Bitcoin Ordinals/Inscriptions as a side effect. Activated via Speedy Trial (miners signal readiness). Backward compatible - old nodes still work. Now enables richer Bitcoin scripting and privacy-preserving multisig.
06 · Deep Dive - Ethereum vs Ethereum Classic

The Philosophical Battle Behind the Chain Split

The ETH/ETC split is not merely a technical event - it is the most important philosophical debate in blockchain history, still unresolved today. Two irreconcilable worldviews collided:

ETH
The Pragmatist View - Ethereum (ETH)
"Blockchains are tools created by humans for human purposes. The DAO hack was a catastrophic technical failure that exploited a bug - not a legitimate expression of smart contract intent. The spirit of The DAO was to fund projects, not to enrich an attacker through a recursive call exploit. Immutability is a desirable property, but it is not an absolute moral law that supersedes the interests of the human community it serves. We can and should fix clear injustices when we have the ability to do so - especially when there is overwhelming community consensus."
ETC
The Absolutist View - Ethereum Classic (ETC)
"Code is law. Smart contracts execute exactly as written - including unforeseen edge cases. The attacker did not hack anything; they used the contract's own functions legally. If we reverse this transaction because 'we don't like the outcome,' we have proven that Ethereum is not immutable - that a sufficiently motivated social consensus can rewrite history. This makes every smart contract promise conditional on the community's approval. No one can trust any blockchain guarantee ever again. The only honest choice is to let the code execute as written, however painful."
🗝️ The Unresolved Tension

Both views are internally consistent. The ETH community chose pragmatism and now hosts 60%+ of all DeFi, NFTs, and smart contract activity. The ETC community chose ideological purity and maintained a much smaller but more consistent ecosystem. Neither side has been proven definitively right - the tension between "code is law" absolutism and "community governance pragmatism" remains the central philosophical fault line in blockchain today. MST resolves this by designing governance explicitly into the protocol through Trika POSA's on-chain governance layer.

07 · The Bitcoin Scaling War - BTC vs BCH

What the Block Size Debate Was Really About

Bitcoin's 1 MB block size limit - added by Satoshi in 2010 to prevent spam - became a fierce battleground from 2015 to 2017. As Bitcoin grew in popularity, blocks filled up. Fees spiked. Transactions took hours or days to confirm.

  "Keep blocks small. Large blocks require expensive hardware to store

  BIG BLOCKERS (Team BCH / Roger Ver, Jihan Wu):
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  "Increase block size to 8 MB (then 32 MB). Bitcoin was designed
   to be electronic cash — cheap, fast, global payments."
  "Satoshi himself said blocks could be increased when needed."
  "A chain nobody can afford to use is not Bitcoin."
  Supporters: Some large miners (Bitmain), Bitcoin.com.

  THE RESULT: August 1, 2017 — BCH hard fork
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Every BTC holder received 1 BCH (same private key unlocks both).
  Two chains diverged from block #478,558 onward.

  2024 Status:
  BTC: $1.2T+ market cap. Digital gold. Lightning Network maturing.
  BCH: ~$6B market cap. "Peer-to-peer electronic cash" mission unclear.
  BSV: Craig Wright's further fork. Marginal ecosystem.

  WHO WAS RIGHT? The market voted for BTC. But the philosophical
  debate about what Bitcoin "is" — store of value or payment system —
  was never definitively answered.
The BTC/BCH fork was not purely technical - it was a fundamental disagreement about what Bitcoin was supposed to be, which community gets to decide, and how decentralised governance should work.
08 · How MST Handles Protocol Upgrades

On-Chain Governance - Avoiding Contentious Forks by Design

MST's Trika POSA includes an on-chain governance module - the Governor contract - that provides a structured, transparent process for protocol upgrades. This is designed to avoid the chaotic, politically charged fork battles that split Bitcoin and Ethereum communities.

1
Proposal - Any Staker Can Propose
Any wallet holding above the minimum proposal threshold of MSTC can submit a governance proposal - a formal description of a protocol change, including code, rationale, and implementation timeline. Proposals are posted on-chain and visible to all. This replaces the informal BIP (Bitcoin Improvement Proposal) or EIP (Ethereum Improvement Proposal) process with a binding on-chain mechanism.
2
Deliberation - Community Debates On-Chain
A defined deliberation period (default: 7 days) allows the community to discuss, amend, and respond to the proposal. All activity is recorded on-chain. Cabinet Validators can signal support or opposition publicly - creating a transparent record of sentiment before the formal vote.
3
Vote - Stake-Weighted Community Decision
All MSTC stakers vote - weighted by their stake. A 2/3 supermajority is required to pass a proposal. This threshold ensures that a narrow majority cannot force changes on a large minority - mimicking the high bar required for constitutional amendments. Delegators' votes are cast by their validator unless they override.
4
Execution - Automatic, On-Chain
If the proposal passes, the Governor contract automatically executes the change - updating parameters, activating new features, or triggering validator upgrades - without requiring manual intervention by any developer or company. No hard fork needed for most upgrades. This is governance as a protocol feature, not a political process.
💡 Why This Matters

The Bitcoin and Ethereum communities spent years in acrimonious public debates about upgrades - with social media campaigns, miner signal games, and eventually chain splits. MST's on-chain governance transforms this political process into a transparent, mathematically enforced one. If 2/3 of staked MSTC votes yes - it passes. If it doesn't reach 2/3 - it fails. No ambiguity. No backroom deals. No contentious hard forks from upgrade disagreements.

05
Replay Attack After Hard Fork
After a hard fork, a transaction signed for Chain A may also be valid on Chain B (same private key, same format). This "replay attack" risk requires chains to implement replay protection - adding chain-specific data to transactions so they're only valid on one chain.

Q1
MCQEasy1 mark
Which of the following BEST describes a "soft fork" in a blockchain network?
  • A A permanent split creating two separate blockchains with incompatible rules
  • B A temporary network outage caused by too many transactions in the mempool
  • C A backward-compatible protocol upgrade where old nodes still accept blocks produced under the new rules ✅
  • D A process where validators vote to reduce the block reward for miners
✅ Correct Answer: C - Explanation

A soft fork tightens the protocol rules - the new rules are a strict subset of the old rules. Old nodes see new-rule blocks as valid (they comply with old rules too), so non-upgraded nodes remain on the same chain. Only miners/validators need to upgrade to enforce the new rules. No chain split occurs. Bitcoin's SegWit (2017) and Taproot (2021) are examples - old nodes still sync the chain, they just don't understand the new features.

Q2
MCQMedium1 mark
Ethereum Classic (ETC) exists because:
  • A It was launched as a separate blockchain from scratch by a different team
  • B Ethereum's network suffered a technical failure and split automatically
  • C A minority of the Ethereum community refused the 2016 hard fork that reversed the DAO hack, continuing on the original unaltered chain ✅
  • D Ethereum Classic uses a different smart contract language than Ethereum
✅ Correct Answer: C - Explanation

After the 2016 DAO hack, the Ethereum community voted to hard fork - rewriting state so the stolen 3.6M ETH was returned to original owners. The majority adopted the forked chain (ETH). A minority who believed in "code is law" refused to follow the fork and continued on the original, unaltered chain. This became Ethereum Classic (ETC). ETC and ETH share identical history up to block #1,920,000 - the fork point. ETH has vastly outgrown ETC in market cap and ecosystem.

Q3
MCQMedium1 mark
What is the primary risk of a "replay attack" after a contentious hard fork?
  • A Miners on the new chain can access wallets on the old chain using brute force
  • B A transaction broadcast on one chain may also be valid and executed on the other chain - spending coins on both simultaneously ✅
  • C The new chain slows down because it must replay all historical transactions from scratch
  • D Smart contracts must be redeployed because their addresses change after the fork
✅ Correct Answer: B - Explanation

After a hard fork, both chains share the same cryptographic key format and (initially) the same transaction structure. A signed transaction - "send 1 ETH from my address to Priya" - may be valid and executable on BOTH chains, since both recognise the same signature and format. An attacker (or even an accidental broadcast) could "replay" your ETH transaction on ETC, moving ETC you intended to keep. Protection: chains add a Chain ID field to transactions - a unique identifier per chain. ETH uses Chain ID 1; ETC uses Chain ID 61. Transactions signed for one chain are rejected by the other.

Section B - True / False with Justification
1 Question · 2 marks
Q4
True / False + JustificationMedium2 marks
"A hard fork always results in a permanent chain split - creating two separate blockchains and two different cryptocurrencies."
FALSE
📝 Sample Justification - write both marks' worth:
This statement is FALSE. A hard fork is a non-backward-compatible protocol upgrade - old nodes cannot follow the new chain. However, a chain split only occurs when a significant minority of nodes refuse to upgrade and continue mining/validating the old chain. If the entire community upgrades (or the non-upgraders have negligible mining/staking power), there is no permanent split - the old chain simply dies from lack of validators, and only one chain survives. A clear example is Ethereum's "The Merge" (September 2022) - a hard fork switching from PoW to PoS. The community overwhelmingly adopted the new chain. A tiny ETHW (EthereumPoW) fork briefly appeared but was negligible and quickly faded. The result was one dominant chain, not a permanent split. Therefore, hard forks are a necessary condition for a split, but not a sufficient one - community adoption determines whether two chains persist.

Key Terms - Sub-Module 3.7

Soft Fork
A backward-compatible protocol upgrade. Old nodes still accept new blocks. Only miners/validators need to upgrade. No chain split. Examples: SegWit, Taproot.
Hard Fork
A non-backward-compatible protocol upgrade. Old nodes reject new blocks. If significant minority refuses to upgrade: permanent chain split into two blockchains.
Ethereum Classic (ETC)
The continuation of the original Ethereum blockchain after the 2016 DAO hard fork. Upholds "code is law" - no transaction reversals. Shares history with ETH before block #1,920,000.
The DAO Hack (2016)
Attack draining 3.6M ETH from The DAO using a recursive call exploit. Prompted the Ethereum hard fork. The philosophical debate between ETH and ETC stems from this event.
Replay Attack
After a fork, a transaction valid on one chain may also execute on the other. Prevented by Chain ID - a per-chain unique identifier embedded in transactions. ETH = Chain ID 1, ETC = Chain ID 61.
SegWit
Segregated Witness - Bitcoin's 2017 soft fork moving signature data outside transaction bodies. Effectively increased capacity and fixed transaction malleability. Now used in ~80% of Bitcoin transactions.
Bitcoin Cash (BCH)
August 2017 hard fork of Bitcoin increasing block size from 1 MB to 8 MB. Led by "big blockers" wanting cheaper, faster on-chain payments. Now has ~1% of Bitcoin's market cap.
The Merge (2022)
Ethereum's September 2022 hard fork switching from PoW to PoS. The Beacon Chain merged with the execution layer. Energy use dropped 99.95%. No significant chain split.
Code is Law
The ETC philosophy: smart contracts execute exactly as written, regardless of outcome. No human consensus can override what code does. The counterposition to ETH's pragmatic governance.
On-Chain Governance
Protocol upgrade decisions made through binding blockchain votes (stake-weighted). MST's Governor contract enables this - avoiding the political fork battles of Bitcoin and Ethereum.
