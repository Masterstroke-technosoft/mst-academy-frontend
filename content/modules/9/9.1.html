02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Two Founders Who Built a Solution Nobody Wanted

📖 Story

In 2022, two passionate developers spent six months building a blockchain-based voting system for student elections. The smart contracts were elegant, the UI was slick, and the gas costs were optimized. They launched on a major EVM chain. Total users after three months: 12. The student council didn't care about immutability; the existing Google Form was good enough. The project failed - not because of technology, but because nobody needed it.


Contrast that with a team that spent a week researching the Indian gig economy. They interviewed delivery riders and discovered a trust gap: riders didn't trust the platform's rating system, and customers feared fake reviews. They built a decentralized reputation system where every rating is on-chain, tamper-proof, and portable between platforms. They launched a pilot with 50 riders. Within months, a major logistics company offered to integrate it. The difference: one team started with the technology; the other started with the problem.

This sub-module ensures you start with the problem. You'll learn to look at the world through a "blockchain lens" - not to force the technology onto every situation, but to recognize the rare, high-impact opportunities where it truly belongs.

03   Background & History

The Art of Ideation - How Great Web3 Products Are Born

Blockchain ideation has evolved rapidly. Early projects (2013 - 2016) focused almost exclusively on digital currency and simple tokens. The mindset was "how can we copy Bitcoin but slightly different?" By 2017, the ICO boom shifted ideation to "what token can we sell?" - often with little real-world utility. This era produced many whitepapers and few working products.

The DeFi summer of 2020 marked a turning point. Uniswap, Aave, and Compound proved that blockchain could solve genuine financial problems - permissionless access, transparency, and composability. Suddenly, ideation became user-centered. The question changed from "what can we build on Ethereum?" to "what problems do people have that only blockchain can solve?"

Today, with chains like MST offering high throughput, sub-paisa fees, and easy user onboarding, the ideation canvas has expanded further. Problems that were impractical on Ethereum - micro-payments for content, real-time gaming assets, low-cost supply chain tracking - are now feasible. The best capstone projects sit at this intersection: a real human need, plus a blockchain feature that traditional infrastructure cannot provide.

Golden rule of Web3 ideation: "Don't look for a problem for your blockchain. Look for a problem that only a blockchain can solve." If a centralized database can do it cheaper and faster, let it.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Ideation Funnel - From Fog to Focus

🔍 The Analogy

Ideation is like panning for gold. You start with a broad river - every industry, every pain point, every inefficiency. You scoop up promising sand (ideas) and run it through increasingly fine filters: "Is there real demand?", "Can a blockchain fix this?", "Can I build it in a few weeks?" Most ideas wash away. The nuggets that remain are your capstone candidates.

The ideation funnel has four stages. Click each to explore:

①
DIVERGE
Generate as many ideas as possible
Think about industries you know: education, healthcare, logistics, entertainment. List problems. Don't judge yet. At this stage, quantity beats quality. Examples: "freelancers don't get paid on time", "farmers can't prove organic certification", "concert tickets get scalped".
②
FILTER
Apply the blockchain litmus test
For each idea, ask five questions: 1) Is there a trust gap between parties? 2) Does the problem involve multiple entities that don't share a database? 3) Is there a need for permanent, auditable records? 4) Can smart contracts automate a manual process? 5) Would decentralization reduce costs or increase participation? Ideas scoring 3+ "yes" answers survive to the next stage.
③
VALIDATE
Talk to real users, check feasibility
Find 5 potential users (friends, online communities, professionals) and describe your idea in one sentence. Listen to their reactions. Do they say "I need this" or "that's interesting"? Check if a similar dApp already exists. Research the legal landscape (e.g., financial services require licenses).
④
SELECT
Choose your capstone and commit
Pick the idea with the best balance of impact, feasibility, and personal interest. You'll be working on this for weeks; passion matters. Write a one-page proposal. Share it with peers for feedback. Then lock in your choice and move to architecture design (9.2).

05   The Blockchain Litmus Test

Does Your Idea Actually Need a Blockchain?

This is the most important question you'll ask during ideation. Many promising-sounding ideas fail this test because a centralized database or simple web app would work just as well. Here's the detailed checklist, with examples:

Criteria✅ Blockchain-Worthy❌ Better Without Blockchain
Trust gapMultiple parties transact but don't trust each other (e.g., buyer and seller on a freelance platform)All users trust a central authority (e.g., internal company tool)
Multiple writersMany independent entities need to write to a shared record (e.g., hospitals updating a patient's medical history)Only one entity writes; others only read (e.g., a blog)
Immutability requirementRecords must be tamper-proof and auditable forever (e.g., land titles, academic credentials)Data can be edited or deleted by an admin (e.g., user preferences)
Disintermediation gainRemoving a middleman significantly reduces cost or increases speed (e.g., remittances without banks)The intermediary adds real value that can't be automated (e.g., doctors diagnosing patients)
Automation potentialA smart contract can automate a manual, error-prone process (e.g., insurance claims triggered by weather data)The process requires human judgment in every case (e.g., creative design)

Let's apply this test to two ideas:

✅
Decentralized Freelance Platform (Passes)
Trust gap: clients and freelancers don't trust each other. Multiple writers: both parties update order status. Immutability: dispute records must be auditable. Disintermediation: removes 20% platform fee. Automation: escrow releases automatically upon confirmation. Verdict: Strong blockchain fit.
❌
To-Do List App (Fails)
Only one writer (the user). No trust gap. No disintermediation. A centralized database is faster, cheaper, and simpler. Verdict: Not a blockchain use case.
🗝️ The Acid Test

If you can remove the blockchain from your idea and nothing breaks, you don't need a blockchain. The best capstone ideas are the ones where the blockchain isn't a feature - it's the foundation.

06   Interactive Ideation Workshop

Find Your Capstone Idea - Step by Step

Work through these exercises. By the end, you'll have a shortlist of validated ideas.

📝 Exercise 1: The Pain Point Harvest

Think about industries you've personally experienced: education, healthcare, freelancing, gaming, social media, e-commerce, logistics, banking. For each, ask: "What frustrated me as a user? What felt unfair? Where did I have to trust a company that I didn't trust?" Write down 10 pain points. Don't censor yourself.

Examples: "UPI payments sometimes fail and I have to trust the bank's dispute resolution", "My university degree can be faked easily", "Online game items I bought disappeared when the server shut down".

🧪 Exercise 2: Apply the Litmus Test

Take your 10 pain points and score each on the five criteria (1 = not at all, 5 = extremely). Sum the scores. Ideas scoring 18+ are strong candidates. Ideas scoring below 12 probably aren't blockchain problems.

🗣️ Exercise 3: The 5-Minute Validation

Take your top 3 ideas. For each, find one person in your network who works in that industry or has experienced that pain point. Describe your idea in 60 seconds. Ask: "Would you use this? What would make you not use it?" Write down their exact words. This is gold.

📊 Exercise 4: The MST Fit Check

For your top idea, ask: "Which MST feature makes this possible or better?" Is it the sub-paisa fees (micropayments, frequent transactions)? SARAL (onboarding non-crypto users)? WASMify (verifiable off-chain data)? Hybrid zones (permissioned + public)? Be specific. If your idea could run identically on Ethereum, you're not leveraging MST's advantage.

07   MST Use Case Map

Domains Where MST Excels - Spark Your Imagination

Here are concrete areas where MST's architecture creates opportunities that don't exist on other chains. Use these as inspiration for your capstone:

💸
Micro-Payments & Creator Economy
Platforms where users pay tiny amounts per view, per stream, or per article. On Ethereum, gas costs 100x the payment. On MST, a ₹1 tip costs ₹0.006 in gas - 99.4% of the payment reaches the creator. Ideas: per-article paywalls, pay-per-minute video streaming, per-play music royalties.
🎮
Gaming & Virtual Economies
In-game assets that are truly owned by players, with provably fair loot boxes (FortunaX, SatvaShuffle). Low fees enable frequent, small transactions - looting 10 items doesn't cost ₹500. Ideas: play-to-earn games, cross-game item marketplaces, provably fair casino.
🆔
Identity & Credentials
Self-sovereign identity where users control their data. MST's hybrid zones allow enterprises to store private data in permissioned lanes while public verification happens on the open chain. Ideas: on-chain academic credentials, reusable KYC, decentralized professional profiles.
📦
Supply Chain & Provenance
Track products from factory to consumer with immutable records. WASMify can verify IoT data (temperature, location) and submit ZK proofs on-chain. Low fees mean every scan, every handover can be recorded. Ideas: organic certification tracker, pharmaceutical supply chain, carbon credit tracking.
🏛️
DeFi & Tokenized Assets
Lending, borrowing, and yield farming with sub-paisa transaction costs. Tokenize real-world assets (real estate, invoices, commodities) and trade them fractionally. Ideas: micro-lending circles, invoice factoring marketplace, real estate fractional ownership.
🌐
Social & Content Platforms
Decentralized social networks where users own their data and content. SARAL enables non-crypto users to onboard with Google login. Ideas: censorship-resistant publishing, token-curated content, decentralized Q&A platform.
08   Real-World Case Studies

Ideation That Changed the World

🦄
Uniswap - The Simple Idea That Created a $5B Protocol
Hayden Adams was laid off from Siemens and decided to learn Ethereum. He was inspired by a Reddit post describing an automated market maker. The idea was simple: "What if anyone could trade tokens without an exchange, just by providing liquidity to a pool?" He built a proof of concept in a few weeks. Today, Uniswap is the largest DEX in the world. The idea succeeded because it solved a real problem (centralized exchange hacks, KYC barriers, listing fees) with an elegant on-chain solution. Your capstone doesn't need to be Uniswap - but it should solve a problem as clearly as Uniswap solved token trading.
🎓
IIT Madras - Blockchain Credentials on a Budget
In 2022, IIT Madras started issuing degree certificates as NFTs on a blockchain. The problem was real: fake degrees are a massive industry in India. The solution was simple: issue each degree as an ERC-721 token with metadata linking to the student's identity. Employers can verify a degree in seconds by checking the blockchain. The cost per certificate? A few rupees. This is exactly the kind of high-impact, low-cost project that shines on MST, where minting an NFT costs ₹0.02.
09   Common Pitfalls in Ideation

Don't Fall Into These Traps

⚠️
"Blockchain for everything" syndrome
Forcing blockchain into a problem that doesn't need it. If your idea can be a Google Sheet, make it a Google Sheet. Only use blockchain when trustlessness, immutability, or decentralized consensus are essential.
⚠️
Ignoring the user onboarding challenge
A dApp that requires MetaMask, ETH, and seed phrases will have near-zero adoption among normal users. Leverage SARAL or other wallet abstraction solutions to make onboarding as easy as signing into Google.
⚠️
Choosing a project that's too large
"Decentralized Amazon" is a multi-year, multi-team project. Your capstone should be a focused MVP you can complete in weeks. Start with one core feature. You can always expand later.

Use the following structure for your proposal. Each section should be 2-4 sentences.
Required sections:

(b) Solution Overview: What is your dApp? How does it work at a high level? Who are the users?

11   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 9.1

Ideation
The process of generating, filtering, and validating ideas for a project or product. In blockchain, ideation focuses on finding problems that benefit from decentralization.
Trust Gap
A situation where two or more parties need to transact but do not trust each other. Blockchain bridges trust gaps by replacing trust in a person or company with trust in code.
Blockchain Litmus Test
A set of questions to determine if a problem actually requires a blockchain. Evaluates trust gap, multiple writers, immutability needs, disintermediation potential, and automation potential.
MVP (Minimum Viable Product)
The simplest version of a product that can be built and tested with real users. For a capstone, the MVP should be completable in 2-3 weeks and demonstrate core functionality.
MST Fit
The degree to which a dApp leverages MST's unique features - sub-paisa fees, SARAL onboarding, WASMify, hybrid zones - to achieve something impossible or impractical on other chains.
Problem Statement
A concise description of the real-world pain point that a project addresses. Should include who is affected, how severe the problem is, and why existing solutions fall short.
