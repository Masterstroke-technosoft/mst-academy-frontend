02   Real-World Story

The Chain That Was Too Centralised to Survive

📖 Story

In 2022, a promising new blockchain launched with a bold claim: 100,000 transactions per second. The team had achieved this incredible speed by running the entire network on just 12 high-powered validator servers, all operated by the founding company and its investors. The chain was fast, cheap, and heavily marketed as an "Ethereum killer."


But within months, cracks appeared. When the founding company received a government subpoena, all 12 validators were ordered to freeze a specific wallet. The funds were frozen instantly. Users realised that this "decentralised" blockchain was, in reality, controlled by a single legal entity. Trust evaporated. Developers left. The token price collapsed. The chain is now virtually abandoned.


This is the scalability trilemma in action. The team solved scalability by sacrificing decentralisation. They got speed, but they lost the very thing that makes a blockchain valuable - trustlessness. The trilemma is not an abstract academic concept. It is the fundamental constraint that has shaped every blockchain's success or failure. In this sub-module, you will learn exactly how it works, and how to evaluate any chain - including MST - against its iron laws.

03   What Is the Scalability Trilemma?

The Impossible Triangle of Blockchain Design

The scalability trilemma was popularised by Ethereum co-founder Vitalik Buterin. It states that a blockchain can achieve at most two of the following three properties:

1. Security: The network can resist attacks from adversaries controlling a significant portion of resources. Transactions, once confirmed, are irreversible and tamper-proof. Security requires that no single entity can rewrite history or double-spend coins.

2. Decentralisation: The network is not controlled by any single entity or small group. Anyone can run a node, validate transactions, and participate in consensus. Decentralisation ensures censorship resistance and trustlessness.

3. Scalability: The network can process a high volume of transactions quickly and cheaply. Scalability means the chain can handle global demand - thousands or millions of transactions per second - without fees becoming prohibitive.

The trilemma claims that improving one property inevitably forces a sacrifice in at least one of the other two. A highly secure and decentralised chain (like Bitcoin) must limit its throughput because every transaction must be validated by thousands of nodes. A highly scalable and secure chain (like some enterprise blockchains) may achieve speed by limiting validator access, sacrificing decentralisation. A highly scalable and decentralised chain might do so by reducing security guarantees (e.g., using a less robust consensus mechanism).

04   Security - The Non-Negotiable Foundation

Why Security Must Come First

Security in a blockchain context means that the network can withstand attacks, that transactions are final, and that the ledger cannot be tampered with. A chain that is not secure is worthless - no matter how fast or cheap it is, if an attacker can reverse transactions, double-spend coins, or censor users, the system has failed.

Security is primarily determined by the consensus mechanism and the cost of attacking the network. In Proof-of-Work, the cost is the electricity and hardware required to control 51% of the hash rate. In Proof-of-Stake, the cost is the amount of staked capital that can be slashed if an attack is detected. A chain with a $1 billion market cap where attacking requires $500 million in staked tokens is far more secure than a chain where attacking costs only $50,000.

Why security limits scalability: Every transaction must be verified by a sufficient number of independent nodes to ensure security. The more nodes that must reach consensus, the slower the process. A chain with 100,000 validators (highly decentralised and secure) takes longer to reach consensus than a chain with 21 validators (less decentralised, but faster). This is the direct trade-off: more verifiers = more time = less throughput.

🔑 The Cost of Attack

Bitcoin's security is measured in hash power: the cost to rent enough ASIC miners to control 51% of the network for an hour is estimated at over $500,000. Ethereum's security is measured in staked ETH: a 51% attack would require purchasing and staking over $30 billion worth of ETH, which would then be slashed. MST's Parlia consensus combines stake-weighted validator election with PoA-style round-robin block production, creating a high cost of attack while maintaining throughput.

05   Decentralisation - The Soul of Blockchain

Why "Who Controls the Chain?" Is the Only Question That Matters

Decentralisation is the property that no single entity, government, or small group can control the blockchain. It is measured by the number of independent nodes, the diversity of their operators, and the barriers to participation. A truly decentralised chain allows anyone with consumer hardware to run a validating node and participate in consensus.

Decentralisation matters for three critical reasons:

1. Censorship resistance: If a government orders a bank to freeze your account, the bank must comply. If a government orders a centralised blockchain operator to freeze your address, they must comply. If a government tries to censor a transaction on a decentralised chain with 10,000 independent validators across 100 countries, they cannot - there is no single point of control.

2. Trustlessness: You don't need to trust any single entity to behave honestly. You trust the mathematics and the economic incentives that make it irrational for any participant to cheat.

3. Resilience: If a data centre goes down, a decentralised network continues to operate. If the founding company disappears, the chain lives on. Bitcoin has survived over a decade without Satoshi Nakamoto.

Why decentralisation limits scalability: Every additional node that must validate transactions adds latency. If you have 10,000 nodes distributed globally, they must all communicate, agree on block order, and synchronise state. This takes time. Reducing the number of validators improves speed but sacrifices decentralisation. This is the second leg of the trilemma.

06   Scalability - The Demand That Never Ends

Transactions Per Second, Block Size, and the Global Audience

Scalability is the blockchain's ability to handle a growing number of transactions. It is measured by transactions per second (TPS), block size, block time, and the associated transaction fees. A scalable blockchain can support global adoption - billions of people making payments, trading assets, and interacting with dApps without the network grinding to a halt.

The demand for scalability is driven by real-world usage. Bitcoin processes ~7 TPS. Ethereum processes ~30 TPS. Visa processes ~65,000 TPS. For a blockchain to become a global settlement layer, it must close this gap. The challenge is that increasing TPS typically requires either increasing block size (which makes it harder for small nodes to keep up, reducing decentralisation) or reducing the number of validators (which reduces security and decentralisation).

Why scalability limits security and decentralisation: Larger blocks take longer to propagate. If a block is so large that it takes 30 seconds to reach all nodes, validators in different parts of the world will see different versions of the chain, creating forks and reducing security. Smaller blocks propagate faster but carry fewer transactions, limiting TPS. The trilemma is not a lack of clever engineering - it is a fundamental physical constraint of distributed systems.

08   Beyond the Trilemma - Layer 2s, Sharding, and Modular Chains

How the Industry Is Trying to Break the Impossible Triangle

The trilemma is often described as an immutable law, but it is more accurately a constraint on monolithic Layer-1 blockchains. Several approaches attempt to circumvent it:

Layer 2 scaling: Move execution off the main chain. Rollups (Optimistic and ZK) execute transactions on a faster layer, then post compressed data back to the secure, decentralised L1. The L1 provides security and decentralisation; the L2 provides scalability. This is Ethereum's strategy.

Sharding: Split the blockchain into multiple parallel shards, each processing a subset of transactions. Each shard has its own validators, and the overall system achieves higher throughput because work is divided. This is complex and still under development for most chains.

Modular blockchains: Separate the blockchain into distinct layers - execution, settlement, consensus, and data availability. Celestia provides data availability; rollups provide execution; Ethereum provides settlement. No single chain needs to do everything.

MST takes a different approach: a high-throughput monolithic L1 that achieves scalability at the base layer without sacrificing decentralisation. By optimising the consensus mechanism (Parlia), minimising block time (~3 seconds), and enabling broad participation through fractional staking, MST pushes the trilemma's boundaries without requiring L2s or sharding. For most applications, 4,000+ TPS is sufficient for mass adoption, and the simplicity of a single-layer architecture avoids the fragmentation and complexity of L2 ecosystems.

Key Terms - Sub-Module 14.1

Scalability Trilemma
The principle that a blockchain can achieve at most two of three properties: security, decentralisation, and scalability. Improving one forces a trade-off in at least one other.
Security
The ability of a blockchain to resist attacks, ensure transaction finality, and prevent double-spending or ledger tampering.
Decentralisation
The distribution of control across many independent nodes, ensuring no single entity can censor, reverse, or control the network.
Scalability
The ability to process a high volume of transactions quickly and cheaply, measured in TPS (transactions per second).
TPS (Transactions Per Second)
A measure of blockchain throughput. Bitcoin: ~7 TPS. Ethereum: ~30 TPS. MST: 4,000+ TPS. Visa: ~65,000 TPS.
Parlia (PoSA)
MST's consensus mechanism combining Proof-of-Authority efficiency with stake-weighted validator elections for high throughput and decentralisation.
Fractional Staking
A model allowing users to own a fraction of a validator node, lowering barriers to participation and expanding the validator set.
Layer 2
A secondary protocol built on top of a base blockchain (L1) that processes transactions off-chain and settles them on the L1.
