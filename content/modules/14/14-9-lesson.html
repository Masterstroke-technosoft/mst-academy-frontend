02   Real-World Story

The User Who Needed a PhD to Use DeFi

📖 Story

In late 2023, a software engineer named Vikram decided to try DeFi for the first time. He had 0.5 ETH on Ethereum mainnet and wanted to earn yield on a lending protocol. Simple enough, he thought. He opened his MetaMask wallet, navigated to Aave, and tried to deposit. The gas fee was $45 for the approval transaction alone. So he looked for a cheaper alternative. He found that Aave was also available on Arbitrum, where fees were lower. But his ETH was on mainnet, so he needed to bridge it. He found the Arbitrum bridge, initiated a transfer, and was told his funds would arrive in 7 days. Seven days! He cancelled the bridge and tried a third-party bridge (Hop Protocol). That bridge charged a 0.5% fee and asked him to switch networks in MetaMask. He had to manually add the Arbitrum network RPC URL. Then he needed ETH on Arbitrum to pay for gas, but his ETH was now wrapped as WETH on Arbitrum. He couldn't use WETH for gas. He had to find a faucet or buy a small amount of ETH on Arbitrum through a centralized exchange. By the time he finally made the deposit - 45 minutes later - he had used three different applications, two bridges, and paid over $60 in total fees.


Vikram's experience is not unique. It is the standard onboarding journey for anyone entering the multi-chain world today. Users are expected to understand bridges, gas tokens, network switching, RPC URLs, and the difference between Arbitrum and Optimism. This is the user experience equivalent of asking someone to configure a modem in 1995 just to send an email. The industry knows this is broken. The solution is called chain abstraction, and it will fundamentally change how users interact with blockchains.


In a chain-abstracted world, Vikram would simply open a single interface - perhaps his wallet - and express his intent: "Deposit 0.5 ETH into the highest-yielding lending protocol." A solver (an automated agent) would find the best path, handle all bridging, swapping, and gas management, and execute the transaction. Vikram wouldn't know or care whether the funds ended up on Ethereum, Arbitrum, or MST. He would just see "Deposit successful." This is not science fiction. It is being built right now by the leading teams in the space.

03   What Is Chain Abstraction? - Hiding the Blockchain from the User

Making Blockchain Invisible - The Ultimate UX Frontier

Chain abstraction is the concept of hiding all blockchain-specific details from the user. Just as the internet abstracted away the complexity of TCP/IP, DNS, and routing - so that users simply type a URL and get a web page - chain abstraction hides gas tokens, network switching, bridging, and chain selection. The user expresses what they want; the infrastructure figures out how to make it happen.

Chain abstraction is the logical endpoint of the scaling journey we have traced throughout this module. State channels, rollups, sharding, and modular chains all solve the supply side of the equation - they increase blockchain throughput. But they create a demand-side problem: users now face an overwhelming array of choices. Chain abstraction solves the demand side by eliminating those choices.

There are several layers to chain abstraction, each building on the previous:

1. Account Abstraction (ERC-4337): The first layer. Smart contract wallets that can pay gas in any token, batch transactions, and recover lost keys. This is already live on most EVM chains.

2. Network Abstraction: The wallet automatically switches networks and manages RPC connections. The user never sees a "Switch Network" prompt.

3. Gas Abstraction: The user pays gas in any token (USDC, the app's token, or even fiat via a credit card). The infrastructure converts it to the chain's native gas token behind the scenes.

4. Bridge Abstraction: The user moves assets between chains without knowing it. The infrastructure finds the cheapest and fastest bridge and executes the transfer automatically.

5. Full Chain Abstraction: The user interacts with a single interface that represents their entire blockchain portfolio across all chains. They request outcomes, not transactions. Solvers compete to fulfil their requests.

04   Intents-Based Architecture - The Next Evolution of Transactions

From "How" to "What" - A Paradigm Shift in Blockchain Interaction

Today, when you interact with a blockchain, you submit a transaction. A transaction is a precise set of instructions: "call this contract at this address, with these parameters, spending exactly this amount of gas, at this gas price." You specify exactly how the operation should be performed. You must know the contract address, the function signature, the gas parameters, and the network you're on. This is powerful but also extremely low-level - analogous to writing assembly code instead of using a high-level language.

An intent is fundamentally different. An intent specifies what you want to achieve, not how to achieve it. For example: "I want to swap 100 USDC for as much ETH as possible, within the next 5 minutes, and I'm willing to pay up to 0.5% in fees." This intent is broadcast to a network of solvers - automated agents who compete to fulfil it. Each solver proposes a specific execution path (e.g., "I'll route this through Uniswap on Arbitrum, then bridge to Ethereum, and deliver ETH to your wallet"). The user signs the intent once, and the winning solver executes the optimal path.

Intents flip the paradigm from user-specified execution paths to user-specified outcomes, with professional solvers competing on price and speed.

Why intents are transformative:

1. No more failed transactions: In the current model, if gas prices spike or slippage changes, your transaction fails. With intents, the solver bears the execution risk. If the first attempt fails, the solver retries with adjusted parameters.

2. Better prices through competition: Solvers compete to offer the best execution. If Solver A offers 0.3% fee and Solver B offers 0.25%, the user gets Solver B's price. This is the same principle that makes Uber cheaper than traditional taxis.

3. Cross-chain by default: Solvers can route through any chain, any DEX, any bridge. The user doesn't need to know which chain the funds end up on. The solver optimises across the entire multi-chain landscape.

4. MEV protection built in: Solvers can bundle intents together, submit them through private mempools (Flashbots), and protect users from sandwich attacks. This is much harder for individual users to do on their own.

🔑 The Solver Economy

Solvers are the professional market makers of the intent-based world. They run high-performance infrastructure, maintain inventory across multiple chains, and compete on sub-millisecond latency. In exchange for providing liquidity and taking execution risk, they earn a small spread. This is fundamentally more efficient than every user running their own execution strategy. The solver economy is already emerging: CoW Swap, UniswapX, and 1inch Fusion are early examples of intent-based trading live in production.

05   Cross-Chain Protocols - ERC-7683, Across, Socket, and Li.Fi

The Infrastructure Layer Powering the Multi-Chain World

Several protocols are building the infrastructure that will enable chain abstraction and intent-based execution at scale. Each takes a different approach, but all share the same goal: making cross-chain interaction seamless.

1. ERC-7683 - The Cross-Chain Intent Standard:

ERC-7683 is a proposed Ethereum standard that defines a common format for cross-chain intents. It specifies how an intent should be structured, how it should be filled, and how settlement should occur across chains. The goal is to create a universal language that any solver, any bridge, and any wallet can understand. If ERC-7683 is widely adopted, it will do for cross-chain transactions what ERC-20 did for tokens - create a single standard that enables an entire ecosystem to interoperate. The standard is being developed by Across Protocol and Uniswap Labs, with support from major wallet and bridge teams.

2. Across Protocol - The Optimistic Cross-Chain Bridge:

Across is an intents-based bridge that uses an optimistic model: when a user submits a cross-chain transfer intent, a relayer (solver) immediately fills the order on the destination chain using their own liquidity. The relayer then waits for the source chain to confirm the lock transaction. If the lock is valid, the relayer is repaid. If not, the relayer can dispute through a bond mechanism. This model eliminates the 7-day wait of traditional optimistic bridges because the relayer provides instant liquidity. Across has become one of the most popular bridges for this reason - transfers complete in seconds, not days.

3. Socket Protocol (Bungee) - The Cross-Chain Aggregator:

Socket (formerly known as Bungee) is a cross-chain liquidity aggregator. It connects to dozens of bridges and DEXs across multiple chains and finds the optimal route for any cross-chain transfer. Instead of the user comparing bridges manually, Socket does it automatically. Socket also supports intents, allowing users to specify "I want X tokens on Chain Y" and letting Socket's routing algorithm find the cheapest and fastest path.

4. Li.Fi - The Cross-Chain Abstraction SDK:

Li.Fi provides a developer toolkit (SDK) that abstracts cross-chain complexity for application developers. Instead of building custom bridge integrations, developers integrate Li.Fi's SDK and get access to all major bridges, DEXs, and chains through a single API. Li.Fi handles routing, gas estimation, and transaction execution. It is used by wallets like MetaMask and Phantom, as well as major DeFi applications.

06   The Converging Multi-Chain Landscape

How the Ecosystem Is Moving Toward One Unified Experience

The multi-chain world is converging in several important ways that point toward the chain-abstracted future:

1. EVM Dominance and Compatibility: The vast majority of new chains are EVM-compatible. This means they share the same smart contract language (Solidity), the same token standards (ERC-20, ERC-721, ERC-1155), and the same wallet interfaces (EIP-1193). This common foundation makes cross-chain development dramatically easier than it would be if each chain had its own virtual machine.

2. Stablecoin Standardisation: USDC and USDT are the dominant stablecoins across all chains. When every chain has the same $1 token, cross-chain transfers become much simpler - there is no need for complex price oracles or liquidity pools. A dollar on one chain is a dollar on another. Circle's Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol (CCTP) enables native USDC burns and mints across chains, further unifying the stablecoin layer.

3. Wallet Convergence: Wallets like MetaMask, Rainbow, and Phantom are evolving from single-chain wallets to multi-chain portals. They handle network switching, bridging, and gas management internally. The user's portfolio is displayed as a unified balance across all chains. This is chain abstraction in practice, one wallet update at a time.

4. Solver Networks: As intents-based architecture grows, solver networks are becoming the invisible execution layer of the multi-chain world. Users express desires; solvers handle the complexity. This is the same pattern that transformed internet search (Google), ride-hailing (Uber), and e-commerce (Amazon). The underlying complexity is hidden behind a simple interface, and professional intermediaries compete to deliver the best outcome.

07   MST's Place in the Multi-Chain Future

How a High-Throughput L1 Thrives in a Chain-Abstracted World

Some critics argue that in a chain-abstracted future, individual blockchains become interchangeable commodities - and that a dedicated high-throughput L1 like MST loses its competitive advantage because users won't care which chain they're on. This criticism misunderstands how chain abstraction actually works.

Chain abstraction does not eliminate differences between chains - it automates the management of those differences. Solvers still need to choose which chain to route a transaction through, and they will always prefer chains that offer the best combination of speed, cost, and security. In a chain-abstracted world, MST's advantages become even more valuable:

1. Lowest execution cost wins the solver: Solvers compete on price. If a solver can execute a trade on MST for a fraction of a paisa in gas versus $0.05 on an Ethereum L2, the MST solver will offer a better price to the user. In a competitive solver market, the chain with the lowest fees attracts the most order flow.

2. Fastest finality wins the user: Intents are time-sensitive. A solver that can confirm a transaction in 3 seconds (MST) can offer a tighter execution guarantee than one that must wait 7 days (optimistic rollup settlement). Users want their transactions filled now, not in a week.

3. EVM compatibility ensures integration: MST's full EVM compatibility means that any cross-chain protocol that works with Ethereum works with MST with minimal changes. ERC-7683 intents, Across-style solvers, and Li.Fi SDKs can all integrate MST natively.

4. SARAL Protocol for user onboarding: Even in a chain-abstracted world, users still need a wallet. SARAL's biometric wallet creation removes the seed-phrase barrier, making MST the natural entry point for new users entering the multi-chain ecosystem.

🔑 The Chain-Abstracted Future Favours Performance

Chain abstraction does not make all chains equal - it makes chain performance more important, not less. In a world where users specify outcomes and solvers choose the execution path, the chain with the best speed-to-cost ratio will capture the majority of order flow. MST is engineered precisely for this future: fast, cheap, and compatible with every standard that powers the multi-chain ecosystem.

08   Case Study - How an Intent Flows Through the Multi-Chain Ecosystem

Tracing a Single User Request Across Chains, Solvers, and Protocols

To make the multi-chain future concrete, let's trace a single intent through the entire ecosystem. This is how a user interaction will look in 2-3 years.

Step 1: User expresses intent. Meena opens her wallet (BridgeKey, with SARAL biometric login). She types: "Swap 500 USDC for ETH and deposit into the highest-yielding lending protocol." She signs the intent with her fingerprint. That's it. Meena has no idea which chain her USDC is on, nor does she care.

Step 2: Intent broadcast to solver network. Meena's wallet formats the intent according to ERC-7683 and broadcasts it to a network of solvers. Five solvers respond within milliseconds, each proposing a different execution path and fee.

Step 3: Solver competition. Solver A proposes: "Bridge USDC from Ethereum to MST (via Across, instant), swap on MST's DEX (0.01% fee), deposit into LendFi on MST (4.2% APY). Total cost: 0.15%." Solver B proposes: "Bridge USDC to Arbitrum, swap there, deposit into Aave (3.8% APY). Total cost: 0.12%." Meena's wallet automatically selects the best combination of cost and yield - Solver B in this case.

Step 4: Solver executes. Solver B locks 500 USDC on Ethereum, provides instant liquidity on Arbitrum using their own funds, executes the swap and deposit, and waits for the Ethereum lock to confirm. The entire process takes under 10 seconds. Meena sees "Deposit complete: 500 USDC earning 3.8% APY."

Step 5: Settlement. Over the next hour, Solver B is repaid for the liquidity they provided by the Ethereum lock confirmation. If the lock fails (unlikely), Solver B can dispute through Across's bond mechanism. Meena never sees any of this complexity.

This flow is not hypothetical. It is the integration of technologies that exist today: ERC-7683 for intents, Across for instant bridging, DEX aggregators for optimal swaps, and smart contract wallets for gas abstraction. The multi-chain future is being built right now.

09   The Endgame - One Chain or Many?

Will the Future Be Monolithic, Modular, or Something in Between?

The blockchain industry is debating two competing visions of the future. Vision 1: A single, ultra-high-performance L1 (like MST, Solana, or a massively scaled Ethereum) handles everything. Users and developers never need to think about multiple chains. Vision 2: A network of specialised chains, each optimised for a specific use case, connected by seamless interoperability protocols and hidden behind chain abstraction. Users interact with a unified interface, but underneath, their transactions flow across dozens of chains.

The likely outcome is a hybrid of both visions. A small number of high-performance general-purpose L1s (MST, Solana, Ethereum with full Danksharding) will serve as the backbone, handling the majority of transactions. Specialised chains (app-chains, gaming chains, privacy chains) will handle specific use cases that benefit from customisation. Chain abstraction and intents-based protocols will make the entire ecosystem feel like a single, seamless experience to the end user.

In this hybrid future, MST's role is clear: it is one of the high-performance general-purpose L1s that power the ecosystem. Its combination of speed, low cost, EVM compatibility, and 55,000+ validator decentralisation makes it a natural home for applications that need scale without complexity. And with WASMify and ZK-based communication, MST can also serve as a settlement layer for specialised chains that want to inherit its security while customising their execution environment.
