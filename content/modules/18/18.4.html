02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Pitch That Won $50,000 in 4 Minutes 52 Seconds

📖 Story

Karthik, a developer from Chennai, had built a working prototype of "AgriEscrow" - a smart-contract platform that releases fertiliser subsidy payments to farmers only when IoT soil sensors confirm application. He had been invited to pitch to the MST scaleGrant committee. He was nervous; this was his first-ever live pitch.

He opened with a single sentence: "Last year, Indian fertiliser subsidies worth ₹1.2 lakh crore were disbursed - but 11% went to ghost farmers because verification happens on paper, three months late." The room went silent. He had stated a real, quantified problem that touched national policy.

In the next 90 seconds, he shared his screen and walked through a live demo on MST testnet. He showed a farmer's wallet (created via SARAL with just a Google login), a smart contract that escrows subsidy funds, and a WASMify module that ingests IoT sensor data and triggers automatic release. The committee could see the code was real, not a mockup.

He then flipped to a single slide showing his traction: "Pilot with 20 farmers in Tiruchirapalli district. 100% on-time subsidy release. Cooperative bank ready to scale to 2,000 farmers if we go mainnet." He ended by stating his ask clearly - $50,000 scaleGrant - broken into marketing, liquidity for a farmer token, and legal incorporation.

Total time: 4 minutes 52 seconds. The committee asked three questions, all of which he answered calmly because he had practiced them. Two weeks later, the grant was approved. Karthik later said the pitch was not the hardest part - it was the 17 practice runs he did in his bedroom, recording himself on Loom, that made it look effortless.

Karthik's story is not exceptional talent. It is exceptional preparation. In this sub-module, you will learn the exact pitch structure, the evaluation criteria, and the practice regimen that turns a nervous developer into a confident storyteller.

03   Philosophy

Why a Live Pitch - Even Recorded - Changes Everything

In Modules 18.1 - 18.3, you learned the grant tracks, the support pillars, and how to write a proposal. The pitch is where the static document becomes a human story. The mock committee is evaluating something a PDF cannot convey: your conviction, your clarity of thought under pressure, and your ability to inspire confidence that you will execute.

A well-delivered pitch can salvage a weak proposal. A poorly delivered pitch can sink a brilliant one. The committee is not expecting a TED Talk. They are expecting evidence that you understand your project deeply enough to explain it in five minutes without hiding behind slides. The pitch is also a test of whether you can be an effective ambassador for your project - and by extension, for the MST ecosystem.

🗝️ The Pitch Is Not About You

Many first-time pitchers focus on their personal journey: "I've been coding since I was 12…" The committee has limited time. They want to know: What problem are you solving? Show me it works. Who else believes in it? What do you need from me? Your personal story is relevant only if it directly explains why you are uniquely qualified to solve this specific problem. Keep it to one sentence.

05   Segment 1 - Hook & Problem (20 sec)

The Opening That Makes Them Lean Forward

You have 20 seconds to earn the committee's full attention. Do not waste them on "Hi, my name is…" (that's on the title slide). Do not thank the committee profusely. Start with the most compelling fact you have.

✅ Strong Openings
  • "Every day, 4,000 Indian gig workers complete a job and don't get paid for 30 days - because client funds sit in a corporate bank account earning interest instead."
  • "Last monsoon, 200 farmers in Vidarbha filed for crop insurance. Zero received payouts within the mandated 30 days. The insurer's backend couldn't verify satellite rainfall data in time."
  • "40% of NFT buyers have no way to prove they own the art - because the metadata lives on a centralised server that can go down."
❌ Weak Openings
  • "So, blockchain is really cool and can change the world…"
  • "Hi everyone, I'm Rahul, and today I'll tell you about my project…" (Get to the pain first)
  • "According to Gartner, blockchain will be a $3 trillion industry by 2030…" (Generic, not about your specific problem)
06   Segment 2 - Solution & Live Demo (90 sec)

Show, Don't Just Tell

This is the single most important segment of your pitch. A live demo - even of a testnet prototype - is worth more than ten slides of explanation. The committee wants to see real code interacting with the MST blockchain. A screen recording of a wallet connecting, a transaction being sent, and a confirmation appearing is irrefutable evidence that you have built something.

1
Start with the user experience
Open your dApp frontend. Show the user flow: connect wallet (BridgeKey), perform the core action, see the result. Speak while you click. "I'm logging in with SARAL - no seed phrase. Now I'm creating an escrow. Funds locked. Now the freelancer delivers the work. I approve. Funds released. That entire flow took 8 seconds and cost ₹1.20 in gas."
2
Reveal the on-chain evidence
Switch to MST Explorer. Show the transaction hash. Point to the verified contract. This proves it's not a mockup. The committee will check - so pre-load the explorer tab and have the transaction ready.
3
Explain one technical insight
Don't explain every line of code. Pick one smart decision you made and highlight it: "We used a mapping(address => Escrow) instead of an array to keep lookup gas constant even with 10,000 escrows." This signals engineering depth without overwhelming.
⚠️ Demo Survival Rules

1. Pre-record the demo as a backup. Live internet can fail. Have a screen recording ready.
2. Pre-load everything. Tabs open, wallet unlocked, contract address copied. Every second of fumbling erodes confidence.
3. If the live demo breaks, don't panic. Calmly switch to the backup recording and continue. The committee values composure.

07   Segment 3 - Market & Traction (60 sec)

Proving Someone Actually Wants This

The committee has seen hundreds of demos. They know a working prototype does not guarantee users. This segment must answer: Is there a real market, and do you have any evidence that it wants your solution?

S
SIZE
How many people have this problem?
Give a credible number, even if rough. "There are 15 million freelance workers in India." Cite a source. Avoid "everyone."
T
TRACTION
What evidence do you have of demand?
Beta sign-ups, letters of intent, pilot results, survey data (n≥30), on-chain transactions from real testers. "We have a letter from Kochi Designers Collective with 80 members willing to pilot" is stronger than "we think people will use it."
C
COMPETITION
Why are you better than existing options?
Name one or two competitors and explain your edge - without trash-talking. "Escrow.com charges 3.25% and takes 7 days. We charge 0.5% and release instantly on milestone approval. And we're on MST, so users don't need a seed phrase."
08   Segment 4 - Team & Ask (60 sec)

The People and the Money

By now, the committee is thinking: "Interesting. But can this person pull it off? And what exactly are they asking for?" Answer both questions succinctly.

👤
Introduce the team in two sentences
"I'm Ravi, full-stack dev, 5 years building fintech backends at Razorpay. Priya handles design and user research - she ran UX for a Swiggy vertical." Relevance, not biography. If solo: "I'm building this alone, and I've committed 25 hours/week for the next 12 weeks. I've already shipped the smart contracts on testnet."
💵
State the ask with crystal clarity
"We are applying for the MST buildGrant - $10,000. This will fund a professional security audit, a UI redesign, and mainnet deployment with 50 beta farmers. We expect to be mainnet-ready in 10 weeks and will then apply for scaleGrant to expand to 2,000 farmers." Do not say "we're flexible" - know what you want.
09   Segment 5 - Closing & Buffer (40 + 30 sec)

End Strong, End Early

Your final words should be forward-looking and memorable. Do not trail off with "so, yeah, that's it." End with a clear statement of the future you're building and the role MST plays in it.

Sample Closing: "In 18 months, AgriEscrow will process subsidy payments for 100,000 farmers - reducing leakage by ₹500 crore annually. MST's low fees, sub-second finality, and SARAL onboarding make this possible. No other chain offers this combination. We're ready to build. We're asking for your support. Thank you - and I'm happy to answer questions."

The 30-second buffer is not wasted time. It prevents you from rushing if a demo took longer. It also gives the committee a moment to absorb before questions. Finishing at 4:40 feels professional. Finishing at 5:02 because you were cut off feels rushed and unprepared.

10   Evaluation Criteria

What the Mock Committee Is Scoring

Criteria What "Excellent" Looks Like What "Needs Work" Looks Like
Problem Clarity Shocking stat within first 20 seconds. Specific stakeholders named. Cost of problem quantified. Vague "this is a big market" opening. No numbers.
Solution Demo Live (or recorded) walkthrough of actual testnet dApp. Transaction shown on explorer. Key technical insight shared. Slides with screenshots. No on-chain evidence. Purely conceptual.
Traction Evidence Letter of intent, pilot data, beta sign-up numbers, on-chain activity. Credible and verifiable. "We have a lot of interest." No proof.
Team Credibility Relevant experience described in one sentence per person. Solo founder addresses sustainability. Long personal story. No links to GitHub/LinkedIn. Team gaps unaddressed.
Ask Precision Exact grant track named. Amount justified with milestone plan. Clear what funds unlock. "We're looking for funding." Amount unspecified. No plan.
Delivery & Presence Calm, rehearsed, within time. Eye contact with camera. Slides minimal. Handles glitches gracefully. Reading from script. Over time. Panics if demo breaks. No eye contact.
11   Pitch Killers

The 7 Mistakes That Instantly Weaken Your Pitch

1
Reading slides verbatim
The committee can read. Your slides should have images, diagrams, and key numbers - not paragraphs. If you read, you signal that you don't know your material.
2
Over-running the 5-minute limit
A pitch that runs to 7 minutes will be cut off or scored down. Practice with a visible timer. If you consistently over-run, cut content - not speaking speed.
3
Burying or skipping the demo
A pitch without a demo is a story, not a proof of execution. Even a 30-second screen recording of a Hardhat test passing is better than no demo at all.
4
No MST-specific argument
If your pitch could be given to an Ethereum or Solana grant committee without changing a word, you haven't explained why MST. Mention SARAL, WASMify, low fees, Trika POSA - whatever you're actually using.
5
Failing to state the ask
A surprising number of pitches end without a clear ask. The committee is left thinking, "So… what do they want?" Always end with: "We're applying for [track] for [amount] to achieve [specific outcome]."
6
Overly complex technical jargon
"We use a homomorphic encryption scheme with recursive zk-SNARKs" will lose 80% of the committee. Explain technology in plain language. The goal is to be understood, not to sound smart.
7
No eye contact with the camera
In a recorded pitch, looking at the camera is the equivalent of eye contact in person. Place a sticky note with "LOOK HERE" next to your lens. It feels awkward - but the recording shows confidence.
12   Recording Tips

Technical Setup for a Professional Video Pitch

💡
Lighting: Face must be clearly visible
Sit facing a window or use a cheap ring light. Avoid backlighting (window behind you). The committee needs to see your expressions - they convey conviction.
🎙️
Audio: Use an external mic if possible
Phone earphones with a mic are better than laptop audio. Record a 10-second test and listen for echo, hum, or breathing noise. Clear audio makes you sound more credible.
🖥️
Screen share: Clean desktop, close notifications
Close all unrelated tabs, disable notifications, and set your IDE to a neutral theme. The committee should see only what's relevant. A cluttered desktop signals disorganisation.
⏱️
Visible timer: Keep a stopwatch in view
Use a phone or second screen with a large digital timer. Check it at each segment transition. If you're 15 seconds behind, cut the next anecdote, not the demo.
13   Assessment Preparation

Top Facts - Know These Before You Record

01
The Pitch Structure Is Non-Negotiable
Hook → Problem → Solution Demo → Traction → Team → Ask. You may adjust timings slightly, but skipping any section will lose marks. The demo is weighted most heavily.
02
The Demo Must Show On-Chain Evidence
A screenshot or slide is not a demo. You must show either a live interaction or a pre-recorded screen capture of a real transaction on MST testnet/mainnet with the transaction hash visible on an explorer.
03
Time Limit: 5 Minutes Strict
Videos longer than 5:30 will be penalised. Videos shorter than 3:00 may lack depth. Target 4:30 - 5:00.
04
MST Grant Track Must Be Stated
Your ask must specify which grant track (initGrant, buildGrant, scaleGrant) you are pitching for, and your project's current stage must match that track's requirements.
05
Delivery Is Graded, Not Just Content
Clarity of speech, eye contact with camera, handling of demo glitches, and adherence to time all contribute to your mark. Practice at least 3 full run-throughs before recording the submission.

14   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 18.4

Hook
The opening sentence of a pitch designed to grab attention instantly - typically a shocking statistic, a provocative question, or a vivid user story. Sets the tone for the entire presentation.
Live Demo
A real-time (or pre-recorded) demonstration of the actual dApp interacting with the blockchain, showing wallet connection, transaction execution, and on-chain verification via a block explorer.
Traction
Verifiable evidence that real users or partners are engaging with the project - beta sign-ups, pilot results, letters of intent, or on-chain transaction metrics.
Ask
The specific funding request stated clearly: the grant track (initGrant, buildGrant, scaleGrant), the exact amount, and what the funds will unlock.
Buffer
The 30-second unallocated time at the end of a 5-minute pitch, reserved for technical delays or a calm closing. Prevents the pitch from feeling rushed.
Eye Contact (Camera)
In a recorded pitch, looking directly into the camera lens rather than at the screen - the equivalent of in-person eye contact. Conveys confidence and honesty.
