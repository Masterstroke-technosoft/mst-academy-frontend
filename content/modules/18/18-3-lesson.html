02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Two Proposals - One Funded, One Forgotten

📖 Story

Two teams applied for the MST buildGrant on the same day. Both had built testnet MVPs - both had similar technical quality. Yet Team A received the $10,000 grant within three weeks, while Team B was rejected with a polite "resubmit after significant revisions."

Team A's proposal began with a single sentence that made the reviewer pause: "Indian gig workers lose an average of ₹12,400 per year to delayed or disputed payments - a problem that blockchain finality can eliminate overnight." They cited a NITI Aayog report. They named the exact smart contracts they would build, showed a Figma prototype, and attached a 3-minute demo video. Their milestone table was precise: Week 4 - escrow contract audited; Week 6 - mobile-first frontend on testnet; Week 10 - mainnet launch with 50 beta testers from a local delivery cooperative they had already spoken to. Their budget requested $8,000 for a security audit, $1,500 for UI design, and $500 for cloud hosting and testnet gas - exactly the $10,000 cap, fully justified.

Team B's proposal was titled "Blockchain-Based Freelance Platform for the World." It ran to 25 pages but never named a specific user. The problem statement read "centralised platforms exploit creators" without data. The solution section was a feature list, not an architecture. The milestone plan was a single sentence: "We will launch in 3 months." The budget simply said "$10,000 for development." The reviewer couldn't answer: who is this for? what does this actually do? how will I know if the money was well spent?

Team A's proposal answered every question before it was asked. Team B's proposal raised a hundred questions and answered none. This sub-module will teach you to be Team A - every time.

03   Psychology of a Reviewer

What the Committee Actually Thinks When Reading Your Proposal

Understanding the reviewer's mindset is the single most underrated skill in grant writing. The MST grants committee reviews hundreds of proposals. They are not looking for reasons to reject you - but they have limited time, limited budget, and a fiduciary duty to deploy treasury funds responsibly. Their mental checklist looks something like this:

1
"Can I understand the problem in 30 seconds?"
If the reviewer finishes the first paragraph and still doesn't know who has the problem and what it costs them, the proposal is already on thin ice. Clarity beats cleverness. A reviewer who understands your problem quickly becomes an advocate; one who is confused becomes indifferent.
2
"Does this person actually know how to build this?"
The reviewer scans for evidence of technical competence: a GitHub link, a testnet contract address, a mention of specific MST protocols (SARAL, WASMify), a realistic architecture. Vague language like "we will create a decentralised solution" without technical specifics signals that the applicant hasn't done the engineering work.
3
"Is the grant amount justified with a clear plan?"
The committee hates one-line budgets. They want to see how every dollar maps to a deliverable. A well-structured milestone table with cost allocations signals that the applicant will be accountable and easy to manage. An unstructured request signals future headaches.
4
"Does this project make the MST ecosystem stronger?"
The reviewer is ultimately a steward of the MST treasury. They fund projects that attract users, transactions, developers, or real-world assets to the network. A standalone app that could exist on any chain with no MST-specific advantage is less compelling than one that leverages MST's unique features.
🗝️ The Golden Rule of Grant Writing

Your proposal is not about how great your idea is. It is about how clearly you can communicate a problem, a feasible solution, and a plan that makes the reviewer confident you will execute. Confidence is the currency of grant approvals. Confidence comes from specificity, evidence, and realism - not from hype.

05   Section 1 - Executive Summary

The Half-Page That Decides Everything

The executive summary is the most important half-page of your entire proposal. Many reviewers decide whether to read further based on this section alone. It must be a self-contained pitch that covers the problem, solution, ask, and impact - all in 150 - 200 words.

💡 The Executive Summary Formula

Sentence 1: State the problem and who suffers. "Freelance graphic designers in India lose 25% of earnings to delayed or disputed client payments."
Sentence 2: State your solution and how it works. "We are building EscrowArt, a smart-contract-based escrow dApp on MST that holds client funds in a multi-sig wallet and auto-releases upon milestone approval."
Sentence 3: State what you are asking for and what it will produce. "We request $10,000 under the MST buildGrant to complete a security audit, build the mobile frontend, and launch on mainnet with 50 beta designers."
Sentence 4: State the ecosystem impact. "This will bring 500+ creative freelancers to MST in the first year and demonstrate SARAL-based onboarding for non-technical users."

06   Section 2 - Problem Statement

Making the Reviewer Feel the Pain

A weak problem statement says: "Artists don't get paid fairly." A strong problem statement says: "According to a 2023 FICCI report, Indian independent artists spend an average of 14 hours per month chasing client payments, and 18% of invoices are never paid at all. Existing escrow platforms charge 5 - 8% fees and take 7 days to release funds. No solution exists that combines instant payment release with sub-1% fees."

The difference is specificity, data, and named stakeholders. The reviewer needs to believe that a) the problem is real, b) it affects a sizeable group, c) current solutions are inadequate, and d) there is urgency to solve it now. Use numbers. Cite sources. Name the people who suffer.

W
WHO
Who exactly experiences this problem?
Be granular: "Freelance video editors in Tier-2 Indian cities" is better than "freelancers." "Smallholder tea farmers in Assam" is better than "farmers." If you can name a specific cooperative or community you've spoken to, even better.
W
WHAT
What is the measurable cost of this problem?
Quantify the pain: money lost, time wasted, opportunities missed. "₹12,000/year lost per freelancer" is concrete. "They suffer" is not. If exact data is unavailable, use reasonable estimates and state your assumptions.
W
WHY NOW
Why do existing solutions fail?
Critique the incumbents - but respectfully. "Western Union fees are 7% for India remittances" is a fact. "Banks are evil" is not. Show that you understand the landscape and that blockchain (specifically MST's low fees and fast finality) offers a genuine improvement.
07   Section 3 - Solution & Technical Approach

Proving You Can Actually Build It

This section is the heart of the proposal. It must convince a technically literate reviewer that you have a workable architecture, that you have chosen the right MST features, and that you understand the engineering challenges ahead. A diagram is mandatory. A link to an existing testnet deployment is gold.

🏗️
Architecture diagram (required)
A simple box-and-arrow diagram showing: User → Frontend (React) → BridgeKey Wallet → Smart Contracts (on MST) → Events → Indexer → Frontend display. Label which contracts handle which logic. Even a hand-drawn diagram photographed clearly is acceptable - the point is visual clarity, not artistic beauty.
📜
Smart contract specification
List each contract, its state variables, its main functions, and its access control. Example: "EscrowFactory.sol - deploys new escrow instances. Escrow.sol - holds funds, emits MilestoneApproved event, releases to freelancer. onlyClient and onlyFreelancer modifiers." This shows you've moved beyond the idea stage.
🔗
Why MST specifically?
Explicitly state which MST features you are using and why. "We use SARAL for seedless freelancer onboarding - a critical UX improvement over MetaMask." "We use MST's sub-second block times to enable instant payment release - impossible on Ethereum L1." This demonstrates ecosystem alignment.
🧪
Current status (be honest)
If you have a testnet deployment, provide the contract address and a link to the verified code on MST Explorer. If you only have a local Hardhat project, link the GitHub repo. If you only have a design, link the Figma. Overstating progress is a fast track to rejection; the committee will check.
08   Section 4 - Team & Qualifications

Why You Are the Right Person to Build This

The reviewer is betting on you, not just your idea. A brilliant idea in the hands of someone who cannot execute is a failed grant. A modest idea in the hands of a proven builder is a high-confidence bet. This section must establish your capability - or, if you have gaps, your honest plan to fill them.

✅ What to Include
  • Your name, location, and time zone (important for mentorship scheduling)
  • Link to your GitHub profile with relevant repositories
  • Link to your LinkedIn or portfolio
  • Brief description of past projects (Web2 or Web3) with outcomes
  • If solo: explicitly state "I am building this alone" and explain how you will manage the workload
  • If team: list each member with their role and relevant experience
❌ What to Avoid
  • "I am a fast learner" without evidence of past learning speed
  • "I have 10 years of experience" without a link to verify
  • Listing a co-founder who "will join later" - only include committed team members
  • Overstating blockchain experience - if you're new to Solidity, say so and mention your Web2 background + plan to upskill during the grant
⚠️ The Solo Founder Trap

Solo founders are not automatically rejected - but they must address the obvious risk: what happens if you get sick, lose motivation, or get a full-time job offer? A strong solo application includes a mitigation plan: "I have committed 20 hours/week to this project for the next 12 weeks and have savings to cover my living expenses beyond the grant." Showing you've thought about sustainability earns trust.

09   Section 5 - Milestones & Timeline

Turning Your Grant Into Verifiable Progress

This is the section where you prove you can plan. The milestone table is the contract between you and the foundation - it defines what "success" looks like at each stage and when payouts should occur. A well-structured milestone table makes the reviewer's job easy; a vague one makes it impossible.

Week Deliverable Success Metric (How We Verify) Grant Allocation
1 - 2 Contract code freeze & submit for audit GitHub commit hash shared; auditor confirms receipt $0 (audit covered separately)
3 - 4 Address audit findings; final contract All Critical/High findings resolved; auditor confirms $2,000 (Milestone 1 payout)
5 - 7 Frontend development with BridgeKey integration Staging URL live; demo video of full user flow $3,000 (Milestone 2 payout)
8 - 10 Mainnet deployment + 50 beta users onboarded Contract address on MST mainnet; 50 unique wallet interactions $3,000 (Milestone 3 payout)
11 - 12 User feedback report + buildGrant completion report Written report with user quotes, retention metrics, and scaleGrant application plan $2,000 (Final payout)
💡 Milestone Design Principles

1. Make milestones independently verifiable. "Improve code quality" cannot be verified. "Pass audit with zero Critical findings" can.
2. Front-load the hardest work. The committee wants to see early evidence of execution. Don't save the smart contract build for Week 10.
3. Align payouts with milestone completion, not calendar dates. If a milestone is delayed, the payout is delayed - this protects both sides.

10   Section 6 - Budget & Justification

Showing Exactly Where the Money Goes

The budget section is where many proposals lose credibility. A single line "Development: $10,000" tells the reviewer nothing. A line-item budget with clear justifications demonstrates professionalism and respect for the committee's time.

💰
Sample Budget for a buildGrant Proposal
Security Audit (via MST partner): $0 (covered in-kind by buildGrant)
UI/UX Designer (freelance, 40 hours × $35/hr): $1,400
Frontend Developer stipend (your own time, 12 weeks × 15 hrs/week × $20/hr): $3,600
Cloud hosting & domain (12 months): $300
User testing incentives (₹500 Amazon voucher × 20 testers): $200
Contingency (unforeseen costs): $500
Total Requested: $6,000
Remaining Grant Capacity: $4,000 (not requested - only ask for what you need)

Notice two things about this budget: first, it does not request the full $10,000 - it only requests what the project needs. This actually increases credibility. Second, the builder's own time is valued at a reasonable hourly rate, showing they understand the economics of software development. The committee is more likely to fund a budget that feels thoughtful and restrained than one that maxes out the cap with vague allocations.

11   Section 7 - Appendix & Supporting Materials

The Evidence That Seals the Deal

The appendix is optional but highly recommended. It is where you include everything that doesn't fit cleanly into the main proposal but strengthens your case. The most powerful appendix item is a demo video - a 3 - 5 minute screen recording that walks through your current prototype, explains the user flow, and shows the smart contract interactions on a block explorer.

✅ Strong Appendix Items
  • Link to a 3 - 5 minute demo video (unlisted YouTube or Loom)
  • Figma prototype link (if no code yet)
  • GitHub repository link (public or private shared with MST)
  • Letter of intent from a potential user or partner (e.g., "We, XYZ Cooperative, will beta test this dApp")
  • Market research data or survey results you conducted
  • Competitor analysis table
❌ Weak Appendix Items
  • 30-page whitepaper that nobody will read
  • Generic market size charts copied from a consulting report
  • Tokenomics models with no explanation
  • Screenshots of code without a repository link
  • Testimonials from friends with no verifiable credentials
12   Rejection Triggers

The Top 10 Reasons Proposals Get Rejected

Rejection Trigger What It Looks Like How to Fix It
1. Vague problem "Blockchain can revolutionise healthcare." Name the exact stakeholder and the quantified pain: "Rural clinics in Karnataka spend 8 hours/week reconciling patient records across three systems."
2. No MST-specific advantage A generic NFT marketplace that could run on any EVM chain. Explain why MST's low fees, SARAL onboarding, or WASMify make it uniquely suited.
3. Overpromising scope "We will build a full DeFi suite with lending, AMM, and yield farming in 8 weeks." Scope down to one core feature. A working escrow dApp beats a broken DeFi suite.
4. Zero evidence of technical ability No GitHub, no contract address, no diagram. Include whatever you have - even a local Hardhat project. Show you've started.
5. Unrealistic budget "$10,000 for marketing" on an initGrant (which doesn't cover marketing). Match budget items to what the specific grant tier covers. Read 18.2 again.
6. Copy-paste proposal Generic language that could apply to any grant program. Customise every section for MST. Mention MST features, use MST terminology, reference the MST ecosystem.
7. Missing team information No name, no LinkedIn, no GitHub. Even if solo, introduce yourself properly. The reviewer is investing in a person.
8. No verifiable milestones "We will work hard and make progress." Define milestones with objective verification criteria: "Contract deployed to MST mainnet at address 0x…"
9. Ignoring grant tier requirements Applying for buildGrant with only an idea and no testnet deployment. Read the tier requirements. If you don't meet them, apply for the tier you do qualify for, or build the prerequisite first.
10. Typos and poor formatting Spelling errors, inconsistent fonts, broken links. Proofread. Ask a friend to review. A sloppy proposal signals a sloppy builder.
13   Writing Tips

Prose, Persuasion, and Polish

✂️
Cut every unnecessary word
Read each sentence and ask: "If I delete this, does the meaning change?" If not, delete it. Grant reviewers appreciate brevity. A 5-page proposal that says everything necessary beats a 20-page proposal that buries the signal in noise.
📖
Use the active voice
"We will deploy the escrow contract" (active) is stronger than "The escrow contract will be deployed" (passive). Active voice conveys ownership and accountability.
🔢
Quantify wherever possible
"Reduce fees" is weak. "Reduce fees from 5% to 0.5%" is strong. "Onboard many users" is weak. "Onboard 50 beta users from the Kochi Designers Collective" is strong.
👁️
Get a second pair of eyes
Before submitting, ask someone who knows nothing about your project to read the proposal. If they can explain back to you what you're building and why, the proposal is clear. If they can't, revise.
14   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 18.3

Executive Summary
A half-page overview at the start of a grant proposal that summarises the problem, solution, funding ask, and expected impact. Often the only section read in full by time-constrained reviewers.
Problem Statement
A section identifying the specific pain point, the stakeholders affected, the quantified cost of the problem, and why existing solutions are inadequate. Must be data-backed and specific.
Milestone-Based Payout
A funding disbursement model where grant money is released only upon completion of pre-agreed, verifiable deliverables, reducing risk for the grant provider.
Verifiable Deliverable
A milestone output that can be objectively checked - e.g., a contract address on a block explorer, a GitHub commit hash, or a live staging URL. Vague deliverables like "improved code" are not verifiable.
Solo Founder Mitigation Plan
A statement in the Team section explaining how a single-person project will handle burnout, illness, or loss of motivation - demonstrating awareness of the key risk of solo grant recipients.
Ecosystem Alignment
The degree to which a project leverages and strengthens the MST blockchain ecosystem, rather than being a generic dApp that could exist on any chain. A key evaluation criterion for MST grants.
