02 · Real-World Introduction Story

The Deck That Cost a $50,000 Grant

📖 Story

Last year, a team from Hyderabad built a brilliant on-chain credential verification dApp on MST testnet. They called it VeriSeal. The smart contracts were elegant, the SARAL onboarding was seamless, and they had a live demo. They applied for the MST scaleGrant - up to $50,000. The committee was excited. The technology was exactly what MST needed: real-world identity, zero-knowledge proofs, WASMify-ready.


Then the pitch deck landed. Slide 1: a crowded logo grid with 27 partner logos - none of which had signed. Slide 2: a 400-word paragraph on "the future of decentralised identity." Slide 4: a tokenomics pie chart with no explanation. Slide 8: "Team" slide with just three names and no LinkedIn links. There was no traction slide, no competitive analysis, and - critically - no clear "Ask" slide. The last slide simply said "Thank You." The committee had no idea what the team wanted, how they would spend the money, or what milestones they would hit.


VeriSeal did not get the grant. A smaller project with a less sophisticated product - but a razor-sharp, 11-slide deck - got the full $50,000. That team understood a fundamental truth: in Web3, your code gets you in the room, but your deck gets you the check.

A pitch deck is not a document - it is a narrative weapon. It must do three things in under three minutes: convince the investor that the problem is massive, your solution is the only viable one, and your team is the one that can execute. This sub-module teaches you exactly how to build that weapon.

03 · Deck Anatomy

The 12 Essential Slides Every Web3 Pitch Deck Needs

Based on analysis of 200+ funded Web3 decks (including Y Combinator, a16z, and MST Grant winners), a winning deck follows a specific narrative arc. Below is the master sequence - use it as a checklist.

FIGURE 19.2-A · The Investor Pitch Deck Narrative Arc
      [1] Title       →  Who are you? (One‑liner)
      [2] Problem     →  What's broken?
      [3] Solution    →  How you fix it (with a demo screenshot)
      [4] Market      →  TAM/SAM/SOM, real numbers
      [5] Product     →  How it works (architecture, not code)
      [6] Traction    →  Users, revenue, grants, waitlist
      [7] Business    →  How you make money (token flow, fees)
      [8] Tokenomics  →  Supply, utility, vesting, sinks
      [9] Competition →  Why you're 10x better (feature table)
     [10] Team        →  Faces, roles, relevant experience
     [11] Roadmap     →  Past milestones + next 12‑18 months
     [12] Ask         →  Amount, allocation, milestones, runway
Sequence matters. Problem → Solution → Why Now → Why You. Never bury traction or the Ask.
04 · The Slides, Brick by Brick

How to Build Each Slide - With Web3-Native Examples

1
Title Slide - The 5-Second Rule
Your company name, a one-sentence value proposition, and your logo. Avoid buzzwords. Example: "KaamFi - Zero-fee freelancer marketplace on MST, powered by SARAL login." Include your name, title, and contact. If you are an MST grant applicant, put the MST logo and "initGrant Applicant" badge. This slide must answer "What do you do?" before the investor blinks.
2
Problem Slide - The Pain You Cure
Use one relatable story or data point. For a Web3 project, never say "Web3 adoption is low." Instead: "Freelancers in India lose ₹8,000/year in platform fees. 60% of gig workers have no bank account." If you’re building on MST, tie the problem to a specific MST feature gap or real-world friction. Use an image or a tweet to make it visceral. Maximum 3 bullet points. End with: "This is a $X billion problem."
3
Solution Slide - The Magic, Not the Manual
Show, don't tell. Place a clean screenshot of your dApp's main screen. A one-line description. Three key benefits (not features). Example: "KaamFi: Freelancers sign up with Google (SARAL), receive payments instantly in USDC on MST, pay zero platform fees." Mention the MST protocol you use (SARAL, WASMify) as a trust signal. This is the slide where investors decide to keep listening.
4
Market Slide - TAM/SAM/SOM With Teeth
TAM (Total Addressable Market): global gig economy - $455B. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): Indian freelance payments - $60B. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): MST ecosystem freelancers - $5M (year 1). Use credible sources (McKinsey, Statista). Include a bottom-up calculation: "If we capture 0.5% of Indian freelancers, we generate $X in fees." This slide must answer: "Is this big enough for a venture return?"
5
Product & Architecture - The "How"
A high-level architecture diagram (not code). Show: user onboards via SARAL → smart contract escrows payment → WASMify verifies delivery → funds released. Mention MST’s low gas (compare to Ethereum). If you have a live testnet demo, put a QR code link. Never paste Solidity code here. The goal is to show technical defensibility, not teach coding.
6
Traction - The Only Truth
Even pre-revenue, you can show traction: 500 testnet users, 1,200 transactions, 3 enterprise LOIs, an MST initGrant award. If you have none, show the size of your waitlist or community (Discord members, Twitter followers). Use a graph (user growth curve) or a timeline of achievements. This slide proves you can execute. Pro tip: include a quote from a pilot user or an MST mentor.
7
Business Model - How You Make Money
For a dApp, revenue often flows through token mechanics: a small transaction fee (0.1%), premium features, or a buy-and-burn model. Draw the money flow. Example: "2% fee on each escrow release → 50% to stakers, 50% to buyback & burn." Show unit economics: cost per user, lifetime value. Be honest - if you’re not monetising yet, state the plan and timeline.
8
Tokenomics - The Web3-Specific Slide
This is where many decks fail. Use a clear pie chart (allocation) + table (vesting). Explain utility in one sentence. Show the value accrual loop: user action → token sink → supply decreases → value increases. Mention the token standard (ERC-20 on MST). If there’s no token, explain why (maybe a stablecoin model). Reference MST’s low gas as an enabler for micro-transactions.
9
Competition - Your Unfair Advantage
A feature comparison table: You vs. incumbents (Upwork, Fiverr) and crypto rivals (ETH-based platforms). Use columns: "Platform Fees", "Onboarding", "Gas Cost", "Decentralisation". Highlight why MST gives you an edge: "0.001 MST fee vs $5 Ethereum gas - 5000x cheaper." Never say "no competitors." That tells investors you haven’t done your homework.
10
Team Slide - Faces, Not Bullet Points
Photos of founders (professional headshots). Name, role, one key accomplishment ("Ex-Google, built a DEX with $10M TVL"). Link to LinkedIn and GitHub. If you are a solo founder, include key advisors (an MST mentor, a legal advisor). Show that you’ve known each other for at least 6 months. The team slide is the most viewed slide - spend time on it.
11
Roadmap - Past & Future
Left column: what you’ve already done (capstone, testnet, audit). Right column: next 12-18 months with clear milestones (Q2: mainnet launch, Q3: 5,000 users, Q4: token launch). Use dates, not quarters like "Q3 2025." A roadmap proves you have a plan for the money. Include the grant application milestone if relevant.
12
Ask Slide - The Close That Gets Funded
State the exact amount you’re raising ("$25,000 under MST buildGrant"). Show allocation: 40% dev, 30% marketing, 20% legal/audit, 10% ops. List milestones this funding will achieve (mainnet launch, 10k users, audit). Mention your existing resources (testnet, team, community). End with contact details and a call to action: "We are seeking the MST buildGrant to bring zero-fee freelancing to 1 million Indian gig workers. Let’s talk."
05 · Do's & Don'ts

The Seven Deadly Sins of Web3 Pitch Decks

Pitfall ❌ What It Looks Like ✅ Fix
1. Text Overload Slide with 12 bullet points, 8-pt font. One idea per slide. Use big fonts, images, and max 3 bullets.
2. Jargon Bomb "Leveraging ZK-SNARKs and homomorphic encryption for trustless interoperability…" "We let you prove your age without showing your ID."
3. No Traction "We will launch soon." Show testnet stats, waitlist, LOIs, grant awards, pilot users.
4. Missing Ask Slide 12 says "Thank You!" Exact amount, allocation, milestones. Always.
5. Vague Tokenomics "We have a token." No utility. Clear utility, supply, vesting, sinks, and value accrual diagram.
6. Competitor Denial "We have no competitors." Feature comparison table showing your 10x advantage.
7. Generic Team Names only, no LinkedIn. Photos, roles, key accomplishments, GitHub links.
06 · MST-Specific Tips

How to Win the MST Grant Committee

💡 The MST Lens

The MST Grant Committee looks for projects that utilise unique MST features (SARAL, WASMify, low gas, EVM compatibility, Trika POSA). Mentioning these in your deck shows you’re not just copy-pasting an Ethereum project. Show how your dApp is better on MST than anywhere else. For example, if your project involves micro-payments, highlight that Ethereum gas would kill your model, while MST’s near-zero fees make it viable.

In your deck, include a small "Why MST?" callout box on the Product or Tokenomics slide. This instantly signals to the committee that you understand the ecosystem and are building natively, not just porting.

08 · Assessment Preparation

Top Facts - Know These Cold

01
The 12-Slide Master Sequence
Title → Problem → Solution → Market → Product → Traction → Business Model → Tokenomics → Competition → Team → Roadmap → Ask. This order has funded more Web3 startups than any other.
02
The 5-Second Rule
An investor must understand what you do within 5 seconds of seeing your title slide. If not, they stop reading.
03
Traction Over Everything
Investors rank the traction slide as the most important after the team slide. Even testnet data, waitlist numbers, or pilot LOIs beat zero traction.
04
The Ask Must Be Explicit
Never end a pitch without stating: how much you’re raising, what you’ll spend it on, and what milestones it will unlock. The MST Grant Committee rejects proposals without a clear Ask.
05
MST Differentiation is a MoI
Highlighting why your dApp is uniquely possible on MST (gas, SARAL, WASMify) is a competitive moat that VCs love. Always include a "Why MST?" callout.
09 · Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 19.2

Pitch Deck
A concise presentation (10-15 slides) that outlines a startup's problem, solution, market, team, and funding ask to potential investors.
TAM / SAM / SOM
Total Addressable Market, Serviceable Addressable Market, Serviceable Obtainable Market - metrics to show market size and realistic capture.
Traction
Evidence of user adoption or market validation - users, revenue, LOIs, testnet transactions, community size. The strongest signal in a deck.
Ask Slide
The final slide that states the exact funding amount, how it will be used, and what milestones it will achieve. Non-negotiable.
Value Accrual Loop
A diagram showing how user activity leads to token demand/burns, increasing token value, which then attracts more users - a flywheel.
MST Grant Committee
The team that evaluates applications for initGrant, buildGrant, and scaleGrant, looking for strong teams, clear tokenomics, and MST-native features.
Narrative Arc
The storytelling flow of a pitch deck: problem → solution → why now → why you → what you need. Keeps the investor engaged.
SARAL/WASMify Callout
A small box or highlight in a deck that explicitly names the MST protocols used, demonstrating ecosystem alignment and technical defensibility.
