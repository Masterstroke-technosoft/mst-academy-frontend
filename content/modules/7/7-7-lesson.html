02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Day ECDSA Died

📖 Story

It is January 3, 2036 - the 27th anniversary of Bitcoin's genesis block. In a research lab in Shenzhen, a team of cryptographers powers on a 10,000-qubit error-corrected quantum computer. Within hours, they run Shor's algorithm against an old Bitcoin address containing 50 BTC from the first mining era - coins that have never moved. The private key, which has guarded those coins since 2009, is mathematically derived in minutes. The coins are transferred. The blockchain is still "secure" in the sense that its consensus rules are intact, but the cryptographic foundation - elliptic curve cryptography - has been shattered.


This is not science fiction. The question is not if quantum computers will break ECDSA, but when. Conservative estimates from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and major quantum labs place a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) within the 2030 - 2040 window. Some experts believe it could happen sooner.


For a blockchain like MST, which may secure billions of dollars in CBDCs, supply-chain records, and digital identities, the risk is existential. Every transaction signed with ECDSA today - every wallet creation, every SARAL biometric key, every Trika POSA validator attestation - could be retroactively broken by a future quantum adversary. This is the "harvest now, decrypt later" threat: an attacker stores all blockchain data today, waiting for a quantum computer to extract private keys and steal funds, forge identities, or rewrite history.


MST's post-quantum security layer is designed to eliminate this risk before the quantum era arrives, by migrating the entire network to NIST-standardized post-quantum cryptography while maintaining backward compatibility.

The quantum threat is not a theoretical exercise. It's an engineering challenge with a clear timeline, and MST is building the solution into the protocol from the ground up.

03   Background & History

From Shor's Algorithm to NIST Standards - The Race Against Quantum

1994
Shor's Algorithm. Peter Shor at Bell Labs publishes a quantum algorithm that can factor large integers and compute discrete logarithms in polynomial time. This algorithm, if run on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer, would break RSA, DSA, and all elliptic curve cryptography - including the ECDSA used by Bitcoin, Ethereum, and MST.
1996
Grover's Algorithm. Lov Grover invents a quantum search algorithm that provides a quadratic speedup for unstructured search. For blockchain, this means a 256-bit hash has only 128 bits of quantum security - still strong, but hash functions need to be doubled in output size for equivalent security.
2016
NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Competition Begins. Recognizing the quantum threat, NIST launches an open competition to standardize quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms. 82 initial submissions are received from teams worldwide, covering key encapsulation mechanisms (KEMs) and digital signature schemes.
2022 - 2024
NIST Selects Winners. After multiple rounds of cryptanalysis, NIST selects the first standards: CRYSTALS-Kyber for key encapsulation (lattice-based), and CRYSTALS-Dilithium, Falcon, and SPHINCS+ for digital signatures. These algorithms are now being integrated into TLS, VPNs, and blockchain protocols.
2025 - 2030
The Migration Era. Industries begin transitioning to post-quantum cryptography. For blockchains, this means supporting both classical (ECDSA) and post-quantum (Dilithium) signatures in parallel - a "hybrid mode" - until quantum computers are proven capable of breaking ECDSA. MST's hybrid architecture is designed for this transition.
2030+
Post-Quantum Only. Once quantum threats are imminent, the classical signature schemes will be deprecated, and all transactions will require post-quantum signatures. Wallets that have not migrated will be frozen - but MST's planned migration tools ensure no user is left behind.
💡 The Cryptographic Reality

ECDSA with secp256k1 offers 128 bits of classical security but zero bits of quantum security. Shor's algorithm reduces the discrete log problem from exponential to polynomial time. Post-quantum cryptography is not an upgrade - it's a complete replacement of the cryptographic foundation.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Lock and the Quantum Pick - Understanding the Threat

🔐 The Classical Lock vs. The Quantum Skeleton Key

Imagine your blockchain wallet is a strong metal lock (ECDSA). For 30 years, the only way to open it was to try trillions of key combinations - impossible with classical computers. A quantum computer running Shor's algorithm is like a skeleton key that reads the internal pins of the lock and crafts a perfect copy in seconds. The lock isn't stronger or weaker - it's fundamentally bypassed by a new kind of tool.

Post-quantum cryptography replaces the metal lock with a lattice lock - a lock whose security is based on mathematical problems that even quantum computers cannot efficiently solve, like finding the shortest vector in a high-dimensional lattice.

W
WHY
Why does MST need a post-quantum layer?
Because every digital signature on MST - wallet transactions, SARAL biometric attestations, Trika POSA validator votes - relies on ECDSA, which quantum computers will break. If MST waits until quantum computers exist, it will be too late for all the data already on-chain.
W
WHAT
What does the post-quantum layer do?
It adds support for NIST-standardized post-quantum algorithms to MST's transaction signing, key generation, and consensus mechanisms. During a transition period, both classical and post-quantum signatures are accepted. Eventually, only post-quantum signatures will be valid.
H
HOW
How does the migration work?
MST introduces hybrid addresses that can be controlled by either a classical ECDSA key or a post-quantum Dilithium key. Users generate a new post-quantum key pair, register it on-chain, and can then move funds to a hybrid address. Old addresses remain spendable with ECDSA until a protocol upgrade requires post-quantum signatures only, with ample notice.
W
WHERE
Where are post-quantum algorithms applied?
Wallets: New key generation uses Dilithium or Falcon. Transactions: Signatures can be classical or post-quantum. Consensus: Validator attestations will migrate to post-quantum signatures. SARAL: Threshold key shares will use lattice-based KEM for share distribution. FortunaX: Randomness beacon will adopt post-quantum VRFs.
L
LIMITS
What are the challenges?
Post-quantum signatures are larger than ECDSA - Dilithium signatures are ~2.5 KB vs. 64 bytes for ECDSA. This increases transaction size and gas costs. Lattice-based key generation is slower. The algorithms are newer and have less battle-testing than ECDSA. MST mitigates these by offering hybrid mode (classical + PQ) and using aggressive optimization, and by participating in the wider cryptographic community's ongoing evaluation.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The NIST Algorithms and MST's Integration Plan

MST's post-quantum strategy is built around the NIST-selected algorithms. Understanding each one's role reveals how MST will evolve its cryptographic primitives without disrupting the network.

1
CRYSTALS-Dilithium - The Primary Post-Quantum Signature
Dilithium is a lattice-based signature scheme based on the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem. Its security reduces to the hardness of finding short vectors in high-dimensional lattices - a problem believed to be hard for both classical and quantum computers. Dilithium offers three security levels; MST will use Dilithium3 (NIST Level 3, ~128 bits quantum security). A Dilithium signature is ~2.5 KB, public key is ~1.3 KB - larger than ECDSA but manageable. MST will implement Dilithium as a precompiled contract for efficient verification, similar to ecrecover. Wallet addresses will be derived as hash(Dilithium_pubkey) using SHA-3, creating a new address format distinct from ECDSA addresses.
2
Falcon - The Compact Alternative
Falcon is another lattice-based signature scheme (NTRU lattice) that produces smaller signatures (~666 bytes) but requires floating-point arithmetic in key generation, making it harder to implement in constant-time. MST will support Falcon as an alternative for applications that need smaller transaction sizes (e.g., mobile wallets). Falcon's compactness makes it attractive for SARAL's biometric-verified key shares, where bandwidth is limited.
3
SPHINCS+ - The Stateless Hash-Based Backup
SPHINCS+ is a stateless hash-based signature scheme. Unlike lattice-based schemes, its security relies only on the collision resistance of the underlying hash function (SHA-256 or SHAKE256), not on the hardness of lattice problems. This makes it a conservative fallback: even if lattice cryptography is broken, SPHINCS+ remains secure. Its downside is very large signatures (~8-50 KB). MST will use SPHINCS+ for high-value, low-frequency operations like validator key rotation and cross-zone certification, where size is less critical.
4
CRYSTALS-Kyber - Key Encapsulation for SARAL
Kyber is a lattice-based Key Encapsulation Mechanism (KEM) - a way to securely share symmetric keys over a public channel. In SARAL, the threshold key shares between the user's device, the MST key-management node, and the OAuth backup will be distributed using Kyber-based encryption. This replaces the current ECDH (Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman) key exchange with a quantum-resistant alternative. Kyber's encapsulation is fast (~0.1 ms) and its ciphertexts are small (~768 bytes for Kyber-768, NIST Level 3).
5
Hybrid Mode - Classical + Post-Quantum in Parallel
During the migration period (estimated 2025 - 2032), MST will operate in hybrid mode. A transaction must be signed by either a valid ECDSA signature or a valid Dilithium/Falcon signature - but a new "hybrid" signature mode will require both to be valid for high-security contexts. This ensures that even if one scheme is broken, the other still protects the transaction. Hybrid addresses (derived from both an ECDSA key and a Dilithium key) allow funds to be controlled by either key, enabling gradual migration without forcing all users to move at once. Over time, the protocol will require post-quantum signatures for all transactions.
🗝️ The "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" Defense

An attacker storing all MST transaction data today could break ECDSA in 2036 and steal funds. The only defense is to move funds to post-quantum addresses before quantum computers exist. MST's hybrid mode encourages this migration by supporting both signature types, allowing users to transition at their own pace while the network remains fully functional.

💡 The Size Trade-Off

Post-quantum signatures are 10 - 100× larger than ECDSA. This directly increases transaction fees. MST's high throughput and low gas costs partially offset this, but dApp developers should be aware that PQ transactions will cost more gas. Hybrid mode allows users to choose when to pay this premium.

08   Real-World Case Studies

Why Post-Quantum Matters - Real Assets at Stake

🏦
CBDC Vault - Protecting the Digital Rupee for 50 Years
The RBI's Digital Rupee on MST is expected to circulate for decades. If a quantum computer in 2040 can derive the central bank's minting keys from on-chain signatures recorded in 2026, the entire currency supply could be counterfeited. MST's post-quantum layer ensures that by 2030, all CBDC-controlling keys will be lattice-based, making "harvest now, decrypt later" ineffective.
🏠
Land Registry - Immutable Records Must Stay Authentic
Andhra Pradesh's land registry pilot records title deeds on blockchain. These records must remain verifiable and non-repudiable for generations. If a quantum attacker forges a signature to transfer land, the immutability of blockchain works against justice - the forged record is permanent. MST's transition to PQ signatures ensures that title transfer signatures remain unforgeable indefinitely.
🔗
Cross-Chain Bridges - Signed Messages Across Zones
MST's Inter-Zone Communication (IZC) uses validator attestations to secure cross-zone transfers. If those attestations could be forged by a quantum adversary, the entire hybrid architecture collapses - an attacker could mint fake tokens in the public zone by forging a permissioned zone validator's signature. Post-quantum validator keys (Dilithium) will be required for IZC messages well before the quantum threat materializes.

10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 7.7

Shor's Algorithm
A quantum algorithm that solves integer factorization and discrete logarithms in polynomial time, breaking RSA and ECDSA.
Grover's Algorithm
A quantum search algorithm providing quadratic speedup; reduces the effective security of hash functions by half (e.g., SHA-256 → 128-bit quantum security).
CRYSTALS-Dilithium
A NIST-standardized lattice-based digital signature scheme. MST's primary post-quantum signature algorithm.
CRYSTALS-Kyber
A lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism (KEM) for securely sharing symmetric keys; used in SARAL for post-quantum key share distribution.
Falcon
A compact lattice-based signature scheme (NTRU) producing smaller signatures than Dilithium; alternative for bandwidth-sensitive applications.
SPHINCS+
A stateless hash-based signature scheme; conservative fallback that does not rely on lattice assumptions. Large signatures but well-studied security.
Hybrid Mode
MST's transition period where both classical (ECDSA) and post-quantum signatures are valid, and hybrid addresses accept either key type.
Harvest Now, Decrypt Later
An attack strategy where encrypted/signed data is stored today and broken later when quantum computers become available.
Lattice-Based Cryptography
Cryptographic primitives whose security is based on the hardness of lattice problems like Learning With Errors (LWE), believed resistant to quantum attacks.
Precompile (Post-Quantum)
A built-in smart contract on MST that efficiently verifies post-quantum signatures (Dilithium, Falcon, SPHINCS+) with low gas cost.
