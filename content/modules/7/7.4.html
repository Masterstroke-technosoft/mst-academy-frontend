02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Lost Passphrase That Changed Everything

📖 Story

Priya, a 24-year-old graphic designer from Pune, received ₹8,000 worth of Ethereum as a wedding gift. Her cousin set up a MetaMask wallet and told her to write down the 12-word seed phrase and keep it safe. "Never lose this, never share it," he said. Priya carefully wrote the words on a piece of paper and slipped it into a drawer.


Two years later, Ethereum's price surged. Priya's gift was now worth ₹2.8 lakh. She wanted to sell some to pay for a design course. She opened the drawer - the paper was gone. Perhaps it was thrown away during a cleaning spree, or maybe the ink faded. Without the seed phrase, the wallet was irretrievable. The money was still on-chain, visible on Etherscan, but Priya could never move it. She became one of the estimated 20% of crypto users who have lost access to their funds due to seed phrase mismanagement.


Now imagine a different system. Priya downloads an app, taps "Sign in with Google," scans her fingerprint, and a wallet is created instantly. No seed phrase. No paper. No "write this down." Her private key is generated and split across multiple secure enclaves - her phone's Secure Enclave, MST's distributed key-management nodes, and an encrypted backup with her Google account. To sign a transaction, she just scans her fingerprint again. Even if her phone is lost, she can recover the wallet by signing into Google on a new device and completing biometric verification.


That system is SARAL - MST's non-custodial, biometric-based key management protocol. It delivers the simplicity of Web2 login with the self-custody of Web3. Priya would have never lost her funds, and onboarding the next billion users becomes possible.

The seed phrase is the single biggest barrier to Web3 adoption. SARAL removes it entirely, while ensuring users remain in complete control of their private keys - not a centralised custodian.

03   Background & History

From BIP39 to Biometrics - The Evolution of Wallet Security

2013
BIP39 Mnemonic Phrases. Bitcoin Improvement Proposal 39 standardised a way to encode a private key into 12-24 human-readable words. This made backups possible but introduced a catastrophic single point of failure: lose the words, lose everything. No customer support, no "forgot password."
2018
Hardware Wallets. Ledger and Trezor popularised storing private keys on dedicated secure hardware, isolated from the internet. While more secure against malware, they still relied on seed phrase backups and added cost and friction for casual users.
2020
Multi-Party Computation (MPC) Wallets. Fireblocks and others split a private key into shares held by different parties, requiring collaboration to sign. This eliminated single-point key compromise but often introduced a trusted third party and was not user-friendly for retail.
2022
Passkeys & WebAuthn. Apple, Google, and Microsoft adopted FIDO2/WebAuthn standards, enabling biometric-based passwordless logins. The cryptographic keys were stored in device secure enclaves and synced across devices. This provided the UX foundation for SARAL's approach.
2024 - Today
SARAL Protocol. MST combines threshold signatures, secure enclaves, and OAuth (Google/Apple) to create a self-custodial wallet where the user's biometrics and social login enable key generation, recovery, and transaction signing - without any party ever holding the full key. Seed phrases are eliminated for good.
💡 The SARAL Philosophy

"SARAL" means "simple" in Hindi. The protocol's goal: make Web3 wallet creation as simple as signing into a website, while preserving the self-custody that makes blockchain valuable.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

The Safe Deposit Box Analogy - How SARAL Works

🏦 The Three-Key Safe

Imagine a bank safe deposit box that requires three keys to open. You hold one key (your phone's biometric), the bank holds a second key (MST's key-management node), and your lawyer holds a third key (your Google account's encrypted backup). To open the box (sign a transaction), you need any two of the three keys. If you lose your phone, you can still open the box with the bank's key and your lawyer's key - but nobody can open it alone. This is threshold cryptography, the core of SARAL.

W
WHY
Why does SARAL exist?
Because seed phrases are a UX disaster. 20% of crypto users have lost funds permanently. For Web3 to reach a billion users, wallet creation must be as simple as "Sign in with Google" - with no risk of permanent loss and no trusted custodian holding the keys.
W
WHAT
What exactly does SARAL do?
SARAL is a non-custodial key management protocol. It uses the user's biometric (fingerprint/face), a social login (Google/Apple), and a distributed network of MST key-management nodes to generate, store, and use a blockchain private key. The full key never exists in one place; signing requires a threshold of shares.
H
HOW
How does the technical flow work?
1) User authenticates with Google → an OAuth token is obtained. 2) The SDK sends a request to a SARAL key-management node. 3) The node, the user's device secure enclave, and the OAuth provider's key sync service cooperatively perform a distributed key generation (DKG) to create shares of an ECDSA key. 4) The user's public key (MST address) is registered on-chain. 5) To sign a transaction, the user re-authenticates with biometrics, the device share and the node share jointly produce a signature via threshold ECDSA. No single party ever holds the full private key.
W
WHERE
Where is it used?
SARAL powers the BridgeKey wallet - MST's flagship wallet. It can be integrated by any dApp using the SARAL SDK, enabling one-click wallet creation and transaction signing from web and mobile apps. It's especially impactful for gaming, social, and remittance dApps targeting non-technical users.
L
LIMITS
What are its limitations?
SARAL relies on the security of the user's Google/Apple account and their device's biometric sensor. If both are simultaneously compromised, the attacker could initiate a recovery. Also, the key-management nodes are a semi-trusted infrastructure; while they cannot sign alone, they could collude with an OAuth provider. MST mitigates this by decentralising the key-management nodes across multiple independent operators and requiring additional authentication factors for high-value transactions.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The Cryptography of SARAL - Threshold ECDSA & Distributed Key Generation

SARAL's magic relies on two advanced cryptographic techniques: Distributed Key Generation (DKG) and Threshold ECDSA Signing. Understanding these reveals why SARAL is non-custodial despite never showing the user a full private key.

1
Distributed Key Generation (DKG) - Creating a Key Without Creating It
In traditional key generation, a single device creates a private key and then splits it. This is insecure: one device briefly holds the full key. SARAL uses Distributed Key Generation: the user's device, the MST key-management node, and a recovery service (tied to the OAuth account) run a multi-party protocol that outputs shares of an ECDSA private key. The full private key never exists at any point in time on any device. Each party holds one share, and no single share can sign a transaction. The protocol ensures that the resulting public key (and thus the wallet address) is computed without anyone knowing the private key.
2
Threshold ECDSA - Signing with Shares
ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) is used by Ethereum and MST for transaction signing. Standard ECDSA requires a single private key. Threshold ECDSA allows a group of parties, each holding a share of the key, to collaboratively produce a valid signature without ever combining their shares. In SARAL, the user's device (using biometric-unlocked secure enclave) and the MST key-management node jointly run the threshold signing protocol. A 2-of-3 threshold is used: any two of the three shares (device, node, recovery service) can sign. This means if the user loses their phone, the node + recovery service can sign a recovery transaction to move funds to a new key.
3
Secure Enclave & Biometric Binding
On mobile devices, the key share is stored in the Secure Enclave (iOS) or StrongBox (Android) - a hardware-isolated coprocessor that requires biometric authentication to access. The share never leaves the enclave in plaintext. When signing, the enclave performs its part of the threshold computation internally and outputs only the partial signature. This prevents malware from extracting the key share even if the device is compromised.
4
OAuth-Based Recovery
The third share is encrypted and stored in the user's cloud account (Google Drive / iCloud) via the OAuth token. During recovery, the user signs into Google on a new device, the SARAL SDK retrieves the encrypted share, and together with the MST node's share, it performs threshold signing to authorise a key migration. The old device's share is not needed. This provides a familiar "forgot password" recovery flow while remaining non-custodial - Google cannot decrypt the share, and the MST node cannot act alone.
5
Key-Management Nodes - Decentralised & Audited
MST operates a network of key-management nodes run by independent validators. These nodes participate in DKG and threshold signing. They are economically incentivised to behave honestly (they stake MSTC, subject to slashing for misbehaviour). The threshold signing protocol ensures that even if a subset of nodes is compromised, user funds remain safe as long as the threshold is not met. Users can also choose to run their own recovery service for maximum self-sovereignty.
🗝️ The Non-Custodial Guarantee

At no point does any single entity - not Google, not the MST node, not the user's device - hold the full private key. Transaction signing requires cooperation of the user (via biometric) and the network. This is self-custody without seed phrases.

Q9
Descriptive (Long Answer) Hard 6 marks
Explain, with reference to specific cryptographic techniques, how SARAL achieves non-custodial self-sovereignty while eliminating seed phrases. Your answer must cover: (a) the role of Distributed Key Generation, (b) threshold ECDSA for transaction signing, and (c) how the user maintains control even though a third-party node holds one key share.
✅ Model Answer & Marks Breakdown (6 marks)

Model Answer: SARAL replaces the traditional single-private-key model with a 2-of-3 threshold scheme. (a) Distributed Key Generation (DKG): Instead of generating a key on one device, the user's phone, an MST key-management node, and a cloud backup service jointly run a multi-party computation that outputs three shares of an ECDSA private key. The full private key never exists in memory anywhere, eliminating the single point of failure. (b) Threshold ECDSA: To sign a transaction, any two of the three shares must cooperate via a threshold signing protocol. The user's device (unlocked by biometric) and the MST node jointly produce a valid signature without ever combining their shares. The signature is indistinguishable from one produced by a normal single key, so it is accepted by the MST network. (c) User control despite third-party share: The MST node holds only one share and cannot sign alone. The user's biometric authorises the local share, and together they meet the threshold. Even if the node is compromised, it cannot move funds without the user's biometric or the cloud backup share. The user never surrenders custody because no single party ever holds enough shares to act independently. This achieves self-sovereignty without a seed phrase.

Marks breakdown (6 marks):
• 2 marks: Accurately explains DKG and its role in never creating a full key.
• 2 marks: Describes threshold ECDSA and how two shares jointly sign without revealing the key.

Descriptive (Short Answer) Medium 2 marks
In 100 - 150 words, describe how SARAL enables wallet recovery without a seed phrase when a user loses their phone. Mention the cryptographic roles of the OAuth cloud backup and the MST key-management node.
✅ Model Answer & Marks Breakdown

10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 7.4

SARAL Protocol
MST's non-custodial key management system using biometrics, OAuth, and threshold cryptography to eliminate seed phrases.
Threshold ECDSA
A multi-party protocol allowing a group to jointly produce an ECDSA signature without any single party holding the full private key.
Distributed Key Generation (DKG)
A cryptographic protocol where multiple parties cooperatively generate shares of a key; the full key never exists at any point.
Secure Enclave
Hardware-isolated coprocessor on mobile devices that stores cryptographic keys and performs operations only after biometric authentication.
OAuth
An open standard for access delegation, used by SARAL for "Sign in with Google/Apple" to authenticate the user and store encrypted recovery share.
Key-Management Node
An MST network node that holds one share of the user's key and participates in threshold signing. Operated by independent validators.
2-of-3 Threshold
Any two out of three shares are sufficient to sign a transaction. Enables recovery (node + cloud share) without the original device.
SARAL SDK
The developer toolkit for integrating SARAL into dApps. Provides methods like createWallet and signTransaction.
Biometric Prompt
A system-level authentication dialog (fingerprint or face scan) required to unlock the secure enclave for key operations.
Recovery Share
The encrypted key share stored in the user's cloud account, enabling wallet recovery on a new device without the original device.
