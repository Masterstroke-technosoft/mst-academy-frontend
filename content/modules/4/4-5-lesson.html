02 - Real World Introduction Story

The Rigged Loot Box and the Cheating Card Dealer

📖 Story Time - Two Problems, One Solution

Story 1 - The Loot Box Scandal: It is 2021. Arjun has spent ₹8,000 on PUBG loot boxes chasing a rare skin with a claimed 1% drop rate. He opens 400 boxes - not a single rare item. He posts his experience on Reddit and discovers hundreds of others with the same experience. The developer's server generates "random" numbers server-side. Nobody can verify them. The "1% rate" is just a number on a marketing page. There is no proof the loot box RNG is honest. It cannot be audited.

Story 2 - The Card Dealer: Sanya loves online Rummy on a popular app. She notices she consistently gets terrible hands. She suspects the server shuffles the deck after seeing which players are in the round - giving good cards to players the algorithm wants to retain. But she can never prove it. The shuffle happens in a black box on the company's servers.

Both problems share the same root cause: centralized, unverifiable randomness. The server decides the outcome - and the user must simply trust it. In gaming worth thousands of crores, "trust me" is not enough.

Cryptographic commitments and VRFs are the solution. On MST Blockchain, FortunaX and SatvaShuffle make random number generation and card shuffling publicly verifiable, tamper-proof, and provably fair - before the player even presses play.

💡
The Core Problem in One Line

If the entity generating random numbers can see the result before committing to it - they can cheat. Commitments and VRFs cryptographically remove this power.

03 - Background & History

From Coin Flips to Cryptographic Commitments

The Coin Flip Problem

Alice and Bob want to flip a fair coin over the phone to decide who pays for dinner. If Alice announces "heads" first, Bob can simply say "tails" - he always wins. If Bob flips and announces the result, Alice has to trust him. There is no fair way to do this without a trusted third party - or a commitment scheme.

Commitment schemes were formalised by Manuel Blum in 1983, precisely to solve this problem. They allow one party to commit to a value (locking it in, invisible to the other) and reveal it later - with proof that the revealed value is the same one that was committed.

VRFs - A More Recent Innovation

Verifiable Random Functions were introduced by Micali, Rabin, and Vadhan in 1999. They solve a different but related problem: not "how to commit to a value" but "how to generate randomness that is unpredictable, unique, and publicly verifiable." Algorand uses VRFs for its consensus lottery. Chainlink VRF brings verifiable randomness to Ethereum smart contracts. MST's FortunaX implements VRF-based randomness natively on the blockchain.

04 - Beginner-Friendly Deep Explanation

Part A: Cryptographic Commitment Schemes

The Sealed Envelope Analogy

Imagine two players are betting on the IPL match winner. Rohan thinks Mumbai Indians will win. He writes "Mumbai Indians" on a piece of paper, seals it in an envelope, and hands it to a neutral referee - before the match begins. After the match, he opens the envelope in front of everyone. If the paper says "Mumbai Indians" and MI won, his bet is proven.

The envelope is a commitment. It has two properties:

🙈

Hiding

The referee (and everyone else) cannot open the envelope prematurely. The committed value is completely hidden until the reveal phase. The verifier learns nothing about the value before reveal.

🔒

Binding

Rohan cannot change his prediction after the match starts. Once committed, he is bound to that value. The prover cannot open to a different value than what was committed.

🎯

Together

Hiding prevents the verifier from seeing early. Binding prevents the prover from cheating late. Both are required simultaneously.

🗝️
Hiding AND Binding - Both Are Non-Negotiable

A commitment without Hiding: the verifier reads the envelope immediately - they see your prediction before the match, defeating the purpose. A commitment without Binding: Rohan writes "Mumbai Indians," but if they lose, he somehow produces a paper saying "Chennai Super Kings" from the same envelope - he can lie. You need both properties simultaneously for a commitment to be useful.

The Commit-Reveal Pattern - Step by Step

On blockchain, commitment schemes follow a precise two-phase protocol called Commit-Reveal:

1

COMMIT PHASE - Lock the value on-chain

The player chooses their secret value v (e.g., the outcome they're betting on, or their chosen move in a game). They pick a random salt r. They compute the commitment: C = keccak256(v ++ r) and submit C to the smart contract. The contract stores C. The player's actual choice is hidden - but locked in.

2

WAITING PERIOD - The event happens

The match plays out, the dice rolls, the cards are dealt. The committed value cannot be changed - it is already recorded on-chain as a hash. Even the player cannot alter it without breaking the hash.

3

REVEAL PHASE - Open the commitment

The player submits the original values: v and r. The smart contract recomputes keccak256(v ++ r) and checks: does this equal the stored commitment C? If yes - the commitment is valid. If no - the player is trying to cheat by revealing a different value.

⚠️
Why You MUST Include a Random Salt

Without a salt, a commitment to "Mumbai Indians" is always keccak256("Mumbai Indians") - the same hash every time. An adversary can precompute the hash of every possible value and compare to your commitment - immediately knowing your secret before the reveal. The salt r must be a cryptographically random 32-byte value, making the input space effectively infinite and precomputation impossible. This was covered in Sub-Module 4.3 (hash commit-reveal attacks).

Pedersen Commitments - The Mathematically Elegant Version

Hash-based commitments (keccak256(v ++ r)) are simple but have a limitation: you cannot do arithmetic on committed values. If Alice commits to "100" and Bob commits to "50," you cannot prove their sum is "150" without revealing both values.

Pedersen Commitments solve this - they are additively homomorphic: you can add commitments together and the result is a commitment to the sum of the original values, all without ever revealing the individual values.

Pedersen Commitment Formula:

C = v·G + r·H

Where: v = the secret value being committed to, r = a random blinding factor, G and H are two generator points on an elliptic curve (public, fixed parameters), C = the commitment point on the curve.

Homomorphic property: If Alice commits to value a (commitment C_a) and Bob commits to value b (commitment C_b), then C_a + C_b is a commitment to a + b - without revealing a or b. This is how confidential transactions in Monero and Bulletproofs work: the blockchain can verify amounts sum to zero (no inflation) without seeing any individual amount.

🌍
Real-World Use - Monero Confidential Transactions

Monero uses Pedersen Commitments for every transaction amount. The sender commits to the amount being sent. The receiver commits to the amount received. The network verifies: sum of input commitments = sum of output commitments + fee commitment. This proves no coins were created from thin air - without anyone seeing a single number. The amounts remain completely hidden.

04B - Beginner-Friendly Deep Explanation

Part B: Verifiable Random Functions (VRFs)

Why Blockchain Randomness Is Hard

A smart contract is a deterministic machine - given the same inputs, it always produces the same outputs. True randomness is non-deterministic. These two facts are fundamentally at odds. How do you introduce genuine randomness into a deterministic system - in a way that nobody can predict or manipulate?

The Wrong Ways - Exploitable Randomness Sources

❌ Block Hash as RNG

Exploitable by Miners/Validators

blockhash(block.number) seems random - but validators choose which transactions to include and can see the block hash before finalising. They can selectively include/exclude transactions to get a favourable hash. A validator running a lottery contract can discard blocks where they don't win.

❌ block.timestamp as RNG

Manipulable Within ~15 Seconds

Validators can manipulate block.timestamp within about 15 seconds without other validators noticing. For any game where the random outcome depends on the timestamp, the validator producing that block can bias the result in their favour.

"Never use block.timestamp or blockhash as your only source of randomness in a smart contract. Any miner or validator can influence both."

The Right Way - Verifiable Random Functions (VRFs)

A VRF is a cryptographic function that takes a private key and a public input (seed) and produces:

  1. A pseudorandom output - looks completely random
  2. A proof - anyone can verify the output was computed correctly from the seed using the corresponding public key

Three critical properties make VRFs special:

🎲

Pseudorandomness

Without knowing the private key, the VRF output is indistinguishable from true randomness. Nobody can predict the output before it is revealed.

✅

Provability

The private key holder provides a proof alongside the output. Anyone with the public key can verify: "Yes, this output was correctly computed from this seed." Cannot be faked.

🔑

Uniqueness

For any given private key and seed, there is exactly ONE valid output. The private key holder cannot generate multiple different outputs for the same seed and pick the one they like - the output is deterministic.

The VRF Coin Flip - Alice and Bob, Again

Alice wants to flip a provably fair coin for a bet with Bob on MST Blockchain. Using a VRF:

1

Setup: Alice has a VRF key pair

Alice has VRF_privateKey and publishes VRF_publicKey on-chain. The smart contract stores her public key as the designated randomness provider.

2

Bob submits the seed (input)

Bob sends a transaction with a publicly known seed (e.g., the current block hash + his address + a nonce): seed = keccak256(blockhash ++ Bob_addr ++ nonce). This seed is public and Bob cannot know Alice's output for it in advance.

3

Alice computes VRF output off-chain

(output, proof) = VRF_prove(VRF_privateKey, seed). The output is a pseudorandom 32-byte value. Alice submits both to the smart contract.

4

Smart contract verifies and uses the output

The contract calls VRF_verify(VRF_publicKey, seed, output, proof). If valid, it uses output % 2 as the coin flip result (0 = heads, 1 = tails). Result is fair, verifiable, and unmanipulable.

🔮
Why Alice Cannot Cheat

Alice generated the randomness - couldn't she pick a favourable output? No. Uniqueness: there is exactly ONE valid VRF output for a given private key and seed. She cannot generate multiple outputs and pick the best one. If she submits a different value, the proof verification will fail - the contract will reject it. She is mathematically forced to submit the honest output or nothing at all.

💡
Why Bob Cannot Cheat

Bob submitted the seed - couldn't he choose a seed that he knows will give a good outcome? No. Pseudorandomness: without knowing Alice's private key, Bob cannot predict what output any seed will produce. The VRF output is computationally indistinguishable from true randomness to anyone who doesn't hold the private key. Bob's seed choice is meaningless to the outcome.

05 - Technical Deep Dive

How VRFs Work Internally - ECVRF

The most widely used VRF construction in blockchain is ECVRF - the Elliptic Curve VRF, standardised in IETF RFC 9381. Here is the intuition:

VRF_Prove (private key side):

  1. Hash the input seed to a curve point: H = hash_to_curve(seed)
  2. Compute the "gamma" point: Γ = privKey × H (elliptic curve scalar multiplication)
  3. The VRF output (pseudorandom value) is: β = keccak256(Γ)
  4. Generate a Schnorr-like proof π = (c, s) that proves you know the privKey used to compute Γ from H - without revealing privKey

VRF_Verify (public key side):

  1. Recompute H from the seed: H = hash_to_curve(seed)
  2. Use the proof (c, s) and public key to reconstruct Γ
  3. Verify the reconstructed Γ matches the submitted Γ
  4. Recompute β = keccak256(Γ) and confirm it matches the submitted output
🧮
The Security Intuition

Computing Γ = privKey × H is easy (just elliptic curve multiplication). Reversing it - finding privKey given only Γ and H - is the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP), which is computationally infeasible. This means nobody can forge the proof or predict the output without the private key. And since privKey × H has exactly one result for any H, the output is unique and cannot be cherry-picked.

07 - Step-by-Step Workflow

The Five "W" Questions Answered

❓ WHY Can't Blockchain Smart Contracts Generate Their Own Randomness?

A smart contract is deterministic - every node running it must produce the same result to reach consensus. True randomness is, by definition, different each time. If a smart contract used a local random number generator, every node would get a different result and consensus would be impossible. All "randomness" that appears in a smart contract must come from an external source that all nodes can agree on - either a committed value (commit-reveal) or a VRF output verified on-chain.

❓ WHAT Makes a VRF Different From Just Hashing the Block Hash?

Hashing the block hash: keccak256(blockhash) - the validator who produces the block sees the block hash before it is finalised. They can try different nonces until they find a block hash that produces a favourable keccak256(blockhash) outcome. This is called a grinding attack. A VRF cannot be ground - Uniqueness ensures there is exactly ONE output per seed, and the private key holder cannot generate alternatives to try. The output is deterministic once the seed is committed.

❓ HOW Does MST's FortunaX Combine Both Commitments and VRFs?

FortunaX uses a two-layer approach for maximum fairness:

  • Layer 1 - VRF Randomness: The FortunaX oracle generates a VRF output from a public seed (recent block hash + request ID). This output is pseudorandom and verifiable.
  • Layer 2 - Commit-Reveal: The player also contributes a committed random value (their own hash commitment). The final randomness is keccak256(VRF_output ++ player_reveal). This ensures that even if the FortunaX oracle were somehow compromised, the player's commitment prevents the oracle from knowing the final outcome in advance.

This is called a dual-randomness model - neither party alone controls the outcome.

❓ WHERE Else Are Commitment Schemes Used in Blockchain?

🏷️

Sealed-Bid Auctions

ENS (Ethereum Name Service) domain auctions use commit-reveal: bidders commit their bid amount before the reveal phase. No one can see competitor bids and overbid by just ₹1.

🗳️

On-Chain Voting

Voters commit to their vote (hidden) in round 1. All votes are revealed in round 2. This prevents last-minute vote changing based on watching others - and enables private voting.

⚡

State Channel Games

Rock-Paper-Scissors in a state channel: both players commit moves simultaneously. Reveal happens atomically. Neither player can change their move after seeing the opponent's.

🔐

ZKP Systems

Polynomial commitment schemes (KZG, FRI) are the mathematical foundation of zk-SNARKs and zk-STARKs. The "commit" in "commitment" is the same hiding + binding concept, applied to polynomials.

❓ WHAT Are the Limitations?

  • ⚠️ Commit-Reveal requires two transactions: The commit and reveal are separate on-chain transactions. This costs double the gas and introduces latency. If a player commits and then refuses to reveal (because the result went against them), the game must handle this "griefing" - usually via a timeout that forfeits the non-revealer's stake.
  • ⚠️ VRF oracle is a trust dependency: The VRF private key holder must be trustworthy and available. If the oracle goes offline, no randomness is produced. Solutions: decentralised VRF (multiple oracles), threshold VRF (needs k-of-n oracles), or Chainlink VRF on Ethereum.
  • ⚠️ Pedersen Commitment binding is computational: It relies on the Discrete Logarithm Problem. A quantum computer could break it (though this requires a quantum computer vastly more powerful than what exists today).
08 - Code Examples

Implementing a Commitment Scheme in Solidity

Here is a minimal but complete commit-reveal guessing game implemented in Solidity - the type of contract you will complete in the assessment. Study each line carefully.

CommitReveal.sol - Full Commit-Reveal Game
// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT pragma solidity ^0.8.20; contract CommitReveal { // ── STATE VARIABLES ────────────────────────────────────────── // Stores each player's commitment hash mapping(address => bytes32) public commitments; // Stores which block the commitment was made (to prevent reveal-same-block) mapping(address => uint256) public commitBlock; // Tracks whether a player has already revealed (prevents double-reveal) mapping(address => bool) public revealed; // ── EVENTS ──────────────────────────────────────────────────── event Committed(address indexed player, bytes32 commitment); event Revealed(address indexed player, string choice, bool valid); // ── PHASE 1: COMMIT ─────────────────────────────────────────── // Player submits hash of their choice + salt. // They do NOT submit the choice itself - only the hash. function commit(bytes32 _commitment) external { // A player can only have one active commitment at a time require(commitments[msg.sender] == bytes32(0), "Already committed"); // Store the commitment hash on-chain commitments[msg.sender] = _commitment; // Record which block the commitment was made // (used in reveal to prevent same-block front-running) commitBlock[msg.sender] = block.number; emit Committed(msg.sender, _commitment); } // ── PHASE 2: REVEAL ─────────────────────────────────────────── // Player reveals original choice + salt. // Contract verifies hash matches the stored commitment. function reveal(string calldata _choice, bytes32 _salt) external { // Must have a stored commitment to reveal require(commitments[msg.sender] != bytes32(0), "No commitment found"); // Cannot reveal in the same block as commit (prevents front-running) require(block.number > commitBlock[msg.sender], "Wait for next block"); // Cannot reveal twice require(!revealed[msg.sender], "Already revealed"); // ── THE CORE VERIFICATION ──────────────────────────────────── // Recompute the hash from the revealed values bytes32 computedHash = keccak256( abi.encodePacked(_choice, _salt) ); // Check: does the recomputed hash match the stored commitment? // If yes → the player is revealing their genuine committed value. // If no → the player is trying to reveal a different value. Reject. bool isValid = (computedHash == commitments[msg.sender]); // Mark as revealed to prevent replay revealed[msg.sender] = true; // Clear the commitment (reset for future games) commitments[msg.sender] = bytes32(0); emit Revealed(msg.sender, _choice, isValid); // In a real game: if isValid, process game logic here // e.g., compare _choice with game result and distribute winnings } // ── HELPER: Generate commitment off-chain (view function) ────── // Players can call this to compute their commitment before submitting. // In practice, this is done entirely client-side - never send your // secret choice and salt to the blockchain before committing! function generateCommitment( string calldata _choice, bytes32 _salt ) external pure returns (bytes32) { return keccak256(abi.encodePacked(_choice, _salt)); } }

Line-by-Line Key Points:

  • commitments[msg.sender] = bytes32(0) - the zero bytes32 is used as a sentinel "no commitment" value. Always check this before committing and clear it after reveal.
  • require(block.number > commitBlock[msg.sender]) - this prevents a front-runner from submitting a commit and reveal in the same transaction or same block, bypassing the hiding phase.
  • keccak256(abi.encodePacked(_choice, _salt)) - abi.encodePacked concatenates the values into bytes before hashing. Never use abi.encode for this - it pads values and may produce different results from off-chain computation.
  • revealed[msg.sender] = true - without this, a player could call reveal multiple times (e.g., trying different choice+salt combinations until one matches). Always protect against replay.
⚠️
abi.encodePacked Collision Risk

Be careful with abi.encodePacked when hashing multiple dynamic types (strings, bytes). encodePacked("AB", "C") and encodePacked("A", "BC") produce the same bytes - a potential collision. For production commit-reveal with multiple inputs, prefer keccak256(abi.encode(_choice, _salt)) which properly separates values. For the simple string+bytes32 pattern above, this is not an issue since bytes32 is fixed-length.

10 - Real World Case Studies

Commitments and VRFs in Production

Case Study 1: ENS Sealed-Bid Domain Auction

The Ethereum Name Service (ENS) uses a commit-reveal auction for domain registration. When you want to claim riya.eth:

  1. Commit phase (5 days): You submit keccak256(name, owner, value, salt). Your bid amount is hidden. Other bidders cannot see how much you bid and overbid by a tiny margin.
  2. Reveal phase (48 hours): You reveal your actual bid. ENS verifies your hash matches. The highest valid reveal wins.
  3. Settlement: Winner pays their bid. Losers get refunds.

Without the commit-reveal scheme, the last bidder could always win by bidding just above the current highest - making fair auctions impossible on a public blockchain where all transactions are visible in the mempool.

Case Study 2: Chainlink VRF on Ethereum

Chainlink VRF is the most widely used verifiable randomness solution on Ethereum. It uses ECVRF:

  • Chainlink nodes maintain ECVRF key pairs (published on-chain)
  • A requesting contract submits a seed and pays LINK tokens
  • The assigned Chainlink node computes (output, proof) = ECVRF_prove(privKey, seed)
  • The output and proof are submitted to the contract, which calls ECVRF_verify
  • If valid, the random number is delivered to the requesting contract

Used by Axie Infinity, Sandbox, PoolTogether lottery, and dozens of NFT minting contracts. MST's FortunaX implements the same pattern natively on the MST chain.

Case Study 3: Klethesia - Transparent Slot RTP on MST

Klethesia is MST's protocol for slot game developers. Return-to-Player (RTP) percentages in slots are notoriously hard to verify - a slot claiming 96% RTP might actually pay 80% with nobody noticing. Klethesia solves this:

  • The slot's payout table and RTP calculation are published as a smart contract (immutable, auditable)
  • Each spin uses FortunaX VRF for provably fair randomness
  • Every spin's result, randomness proof, and payout are recorded on-chain
  • Anyone can aggregate 10,000 spins from MSTScan and compute the actual RTP - provably matching the claimed 96%

This is the difference between "trust our website" and "verify it yourself on the blockchain." Klethesia makes the latter possible for the first time in online gaming history.

11 - Assessment Preparation

Top Must-Know Facts Before the Test

  • A commitment scheme has two properties: Hiding (verifier cannot learn the value before reveal) and Binding (prover cannot change the value after committing).
  • The commit-reveal pattern has two phases: Commit (C = keccak256(v ++ salt) posted on-chain) and Reveal (submit v and salt; contract recomputes and verifies).
  • A random salt must be included in hash commitments - without it, an adversary can precompute hashes for all possible values and break hiding.
  • Pedersen Commitments are additively homomorphic: Commit(a) + Commit(b) = Commit(a+b) without revealing a or b. Used in Monero confidential transactions.
  • A VRF (Verifiable Random Function) produces a pseudorandom output and a proof that the output was correctly generated from a given seed using a specific private key.
  • Three VRF properties: Pseudorandomness (output looks random without the private key), Provability (proof verifiable with public key), Uniqueness (one output per seed - cannot cherry-pick).
  • block.hash and block.timestamp are not safe randomness sources - validators/miners can influence both through grinding attacks or timestamp manipulation.
  • Grinding attack: A validator tries many different nonces to find a block hash that produces a favourable RNG outcome. VRF Uniqueness prevents this.
  • ECVRF (Elliptic Curve VRF) is based on ECDLP: compute Γ = privKey × H (easy), but recover privKey from Γ and H (impossible). The output is keccak256(Γ).
  • MST's FortunaX uses VRF for provably fair random number generation in loot boxes and lotteries. SatvaShuffle uses commit-reveal + VRF for card shuffles in Poker/Rummy. Klethesia makes slot RTP transparent and auditable.
  • The dual-randomness model in FortunaX: final randomness = keccak256(VRF_output ++ player_commit_reveal). Neither the oracle nor the player alone controls the outcome.
  • In Solidity: always check require(!revealed[msg.sender]) in reveal to prevent replay attacks. Always clear the commitment after reveal.
⚠️
Exam Trap - Hiding vs Binding

Students frequently swap these: "Binding means I can't see the value" (wrong - that's Hiding). "Hiding means I can't change the value" (wrong - that's Binding). Remember: Hiding = hidden from verifier. Binding = binds the prover.

12 - Security Notes

Critical Security Principles

🔴
Never Commit Without a Salt - Small Input Spaces Are Broken

If you commit to a value from a small set (e.g., "HEADS" or "TAILS" - only 2 possibilities), any observer can compute keccak256("HEADS") and keccak256("TAILS") and compare to your commitment - instantly revealing your choice. The salt must be a cryptographically random 32-byte value, generated fresh per commitment. Never reuse salts.

🔴
Commit-Reveal Griefing - Handle Non-Revealers

A player may commit, see the game outcome going against them, and refuse to reveal. This "griefing" attack prevents game resolution. Always implement a timeout: if a player does not reveal within N blocks after the event, their committed stake is forfeited and distributed to honest players. The timeout window must be long enough for honest players to reveal but short enough to prevent indefinite griefing.

🔴
VRF Oracle Key Security Is Critical

The VRF private key is the root of trust for all randomness the oracle generates. If this key is stolen, an attacker can predict all future VRF outputs (since they can compute privKey × H for any seed). The VRF private key must be stored in an HSM (Hardware Security Module) - never in a hot wallet or regular server memory. MST's FortunaX oracle uses HSM-backed key storage for exactly this reason.

🛡️
Front-Running Protection in Reveal Phase

When a player submits their reveal transaction to the mempool, a watching bot can see the (choice, salt) before it is mined. If the game contract pays out based on the reveal alone, the bot can front-run the transaction - submitting a higher-gas transaction with the same reveal and stealing the payout. Prevent this by only paying to msg.sender of the original commit, and checking that the revealer is the original committer.

Polynomial Commitment Schemes - The Foundation of ZKPs

Everything in Sub-Module 4.4 (ZKPs) relies on a more advanced form of commitment: polynomial commitments (KZG commitments in SNARKs, FRI in STARKs). These allow a prover to commit to an entire polynomial and later prove the evaluation at any point - without revealing the polynomial. This is how a ZK circuit's witness is committed to and verified. Understanding the simpler hash commitment and Pedersen commitment in this sub-module builds the intuition for polynomial commitments in advanced ZK work.

Threshold VRF - The Future of Decentralised Randomness

A single-oracle VRF has one point of failure: the oracle's private key. The future is threshold VRF (tVRF) - where a group of k-of-n nodes must cooperate to produce a VRF output. No single node learns the full private key. Even if (n-k) nodes are compromised, the randomness remains unpredictable. This is the model used by Algorand's consensus (where the VRF is distributed across thousands of validators) and is on MST's roadmap for FortunaX v2.

14 - Glossary

Key Terms You Must Know

Commitment Scheme
A cryptographic protocol that binds a prover to a value (Binding) while keeping it hidden from the verifier (Hiding) until an explicit reveal phase.
Hiding
Commitment property: the verifier cannot learn the committed value before the prover explicitly reveals it. Analogous to the sealed envelope.
Binding
Commitment property: the prover cannot change the committed value after committing. They are bound to the original value - cannot open to a different one.
Commit-Reveal
A two-phase on-chain protocol: Phase 1 - post commitment hash; Phase 2 - reveal original value + salt. Contract verifies hash matches. Used in games, auctions, and voting.
Salt
A cryptographically random value added to a commitment input before hashing. Prevents dictionary/precomputation attacks when the committed value comes from a small input space.
Pedersen Commitment
An elliptic curve commitment scheme: C = v·G + r·H. Additively homomorphic - commitments to values can be added to produce a commitment to the sum. Used in Monero and Bulletproofs.
Homomorphic
A property where mathematical operations on commitments correspond to operations on the underlying values. Pedersen Commitments are additively homomorphic: Commit(a) + Commit(b) = Commit(a+b).
VRF
Verifiable Random Function. Takes a private key and public seed; produces a pseudorandom output and a cryptographic proof of correctness verifiable with the public key.
Pseudorandomness
VRF property: without knowing the private key, the output is computationally indistinguishable from true randomness. Nobody can predict the output before it is revealed.
Uniqueness (VRF)
VRF property: for any given private key and seed, exactly one valid output exists. The key holder cannot generate multiple outputs and pick a favourable one.
Grinding Attack
An attack where a block producer tries many different nonces to find a block hash that produces a favourable random outcome. Prevented by VRF Uniqueness - only one output per seed exists.
ECVRF
Elliptic Curve VRF (RFC 9381). VRF construction where Γ = privKey × H (curve multiplication). Output = keccak256(Γ). Security relies on ECDLP hardness.
FortunaX
MST's on-chain VRF-based random number generator protocol for gaming and lotteries. Produces provably fair, verifiable random outcomes. Combines VRF oracle output with player commit-reveal for dual-randomness.
SatvaShuffle
MST's protocol for card game developers. Uses commit-reveal + VRF seed to produce provably fair deck shuffles for Poker and Rummy. Deck commitment is posted on-chain before any player joins.
Klethesia
MST's transparency protocol for slot games. Publishes payout tables and RTP calculations as immutable smart contracts, uses FortunaX for randomness, enabling public audit of actual vs claimed RTP.

1 mark
In a commitment scheme, which property ensures the prover cannot change their committed value after the commit phase?
  • A) Hiding - the value is encrypted so it cannot be changed
  • B) Zero-Knowledge - the prover reveals nothing about the value
  • C) Binding - the prover is mathematically bound to the committed value and cannot open to a different one
  • D) Completeness - an honest prover always succeeds in committing
✅ Correct Answer: C

Binding is the property that prevents the prover from changing their committed value. Once C = keccak256(v ++ salt) is posted on-chain, the prover cannot reveal a different value v' - because keccak256(v' ++ salt) ≠ C (collision resistance of the hash function enforces binding). If the prover tries to open with a different value, the contract's recomputed hash will not match the stored commitment, and the reveal is rejected. Hiding (Option A) is the separate property that prevents the verifier from learning the value before reveal - not about changing values.

MCQ
● Medium
1 mark
A game developer uses uint256 result = uint256(blockhash(block.number - 1)) % 6 + 1; as a dice roll in their Solidity smart contract. What is the specific vulnerability in this approach?
  • A) The result is always the same because blockhash is deterministic
  • B) The computation is too slow for real-time gaming
  • C) A malicious validator can withhold their block if it produces an unfavourable result and try again with the next block - a grinding attack - biasing the dice outcome
  • D) The modulo operation introduces statistical bias making 1 slightly more likely than 6
✅ Correct Answer: C

This is the classic block hash manipulation / grinding attack. The block hash is determined by the block producer (validator/miner). If a validator is also a player in this dice game (or is colluding with a player), they can compute what the dice result would be before finalising their block. If the result is unfavourable, they can simply discard the block and try a different nonce - changing the block hash - until they get a favourable dice result. This is trivially easy with only 6 possible outcomes. Option D is not the issue here - the modulo bias (result could be 1 more often than 6 since 2²⁵⁶ is not divisible by 6) is a minor and separate statistical issue. The grinding attack is the primary critical security vulnerability.

3
MCQ
● Hard
1 mark
Pedersen Commitments satisfy an important algebraic property. If Alice commits to value 300 (producing C_A) and Bob commits to value 150 (producing C_B), which statement is mathematically TRUE about Pedersen Commitments?
  • A) C_A × C_B is a commitment to 300 × 150 = 45,000 (multiplicative homomorphism)
  • B) C_A + C_B is a valid commitment to 450, without revealing 300 or 150 individually (additive homomorphism)
  • C) C_A - C_B is a commitment to 150, but only if the blinding factors cancel out exactly
  • D) Pedersen Commitments are not homomorphic - you cannot perform operations on commitments
✅ Correct Answer: B

Pedersen Commitments are additively homomorphic. If C_A = 300·G + r_A·H and C_B = 150·G + r_B·H, then C_A + C_B = (300+150)·G + (r_A+r_B)·H = 450·G + r_AB·H - which is a valid Pedersen Commitment to 450 with blinding factor r_AB = r_A + r_B. Nobody learned 300 or 150 - only that their sum is 450 (which the verifier can confirm). This is the mathematical foundation of Monero's confidential transactions: input commitments + output commitments + fee commitment sum to zero, proving no coins were created, without revealing any amount. Option A is multiplicative - Pedersen Commitments are not multiplicatively homomorphic (that would require more complex structures like Paillier encryption).

// FIX 1 — Prevent overwriting an existing commitment
require(commitments[msg.sender] == bytes32(0), "Already committed");
// Without this, a player can commit "HEADS", see the result go bad,
// then immediately overwrite with a new commitment for "TAILS" and reveal.
// The mapping starts at bytes32(0) — use this as sentinel for "no commit".

// FIX 2 — Prevent same-block commit + reveal (front-running)
require(block.number > commitBlock[msg.sender], "Wait for next block");
// A player could submit commit() and reveal() in the same transaction bundle
// or same block. The event result must be on-chain BETWEEN commit and reveal.
// Requiring a later block number ensures the commitment was made first.

// FIX 3 — Prevent multiple reveals (replay attack)
require(!revealed[msg.sender], "Already revealed");
// Without this, a player could call reveal() multiple times with different
// (choice, salt) pairs until one happens to match — a brute-force attack.
// This check must come BEFORE setting revealed = true (checks-effects-interactions).

// FIX 4 — Include salt in the hash
bytes32 computedHash = keccak256(
    abi.encodePacked(_choice, _salt)  // salt MUST be included
);
// Without the salt, the commitment hash is just keccak256(choice).
// An attacker can precompute keccak256("HEADS") and keccak256("TAILS") 
// and compare to any commitment — instantly revealing the hidden choice.
// The salt makes the input space effectively 2^256, defeating precomputation.

// FIX 5 — Set revealed = true BEFORE any external calls
revealed[msg.sender] = true;
// Must be set before the transfer() to prevent reentrancy.
// Follows the Checks-Effects-Interactions pattern:
// Checks: require statements (done above)
// Effects: state changes (this line + Fix 6)
// Interactions: external calls (transfer below)

// FIX 6 — Clear the commitment after reveal
commitments[msg.sender] = bytes32(0);
// Without clearing, the commitment persists. If revealed is not checked
// or reset elsewhere, the same commitment could be used again in future games.
// Clearing to bytes32(0) resets the player for the next game round.

// FIX 7 — Add receive() function AND require(isValid) before transfer
require(isValid, "Invalid reveal");
// The transfer call needs ETH in the contract. Add a receive() function:
receive() external payable {}
// And fund the contract on deployment. Also, the if(isValid) pattern is
// fragile — better to require(isValid) and revert on failure, ensuring
// state changes (Fix 5 & 6) are reverted too. OR keep the if() but 
// accept that state is updated even on invalid reveals (valid design choice
// to prevent griefing — state cleared regardless of outcome).
Each fix addresses a specific attack vector: Fix 1 prevents overwrite cheating, Fix 2 prevents front-running, Fix 3 prevents replay, Fix 4 provides hiding, Fix 5+6 follow checks-effects-interactions pattern, Fix 7 ensures the contract can actually pay winners.
