02 - Real World Introduction Story

The Bank That Knows Nothing But Trusts Everything

📖 Story Time

Kavya is a 28-year-old software engineer in Hyderabad. She applies for a home loan at a bank. The loan officer asks for her salary slip, bank statements, tax returns, and her exact current savings balance.

Kavya is uncomfortable. She earns well - but she doesn't want the bank officer to know her exact salary, which investments she holds, or how she spends her money. She just needs to prove: "My monthly income is above ₹75,000 and my savings are above ₹5 lakh." That's all the bank actually needs to know.

With traditional systems, she must hand over everything - all private financial data - just to prove two simple facts. She reveals 100% of her private information to prove 2% of what's actually needed.

Now imagine a system where Kavya produces a cryptographic proof - a single 288-byte piece of data - that mathematically guarantees: "Income > ₹75,000 AND Savings > ₹5 lakh." The bank verifies this proof in milliseconds. They learn nothing else. Not her exact income. Not her exact savings. Not her employer. Not her spending patterns. Nothing.

This is not science fiction. It exists right now. It is called a Zero-Knowledge Proof. And it is the most important privacy technology of our generation.

💡
ZKPs Are Already Around You

Every time you send a private transaction on Zcash, use a zkSync rollup, or interact with an Aztec protocol application, you are using zero-knowledge proofs. Ethereum's transition to ZK-Rollups is one of the biggest shifts in blockchain history - and it is powered entirely by this concept.

03 - Background & History

The 1985 Paper That Changed Privacy Forever

Zero-Knowledge Proofs were formally introduced in 1985 by three MIT researchers: Shafi Goldwasser, Silvio Micali, and Charles Rackoff - in a landmark paper titled "The Knowledge Complexity of Interactive Proof Systems." Goldwasser and Micali later won the Turing Award (the Nobel Prize of computing) partly for this work.

Their insight was startlingly elegant: it is possible for one party (the Prover) to convince another party (the Verifier) that they know a secret - without revealing the secret itself, or any information that could help deduce it.

"A zero-knowledge proof is a method by which one party can prove to another party that they know a value, without conveying any information apart from the fact that they know the value."

For decades, ZKPs were a beautiful theoretical result with no practical application - the computations were far too slow. Then, between 2012 and 2020, a series of breakthroughs - Groth16, PLONK, STARKs, Bulletproofs - made ZKPs fast enough to run in real systems. The blockchain world immediately understood the implications and adopted them for two killer use-cases: privacy and scalability.

04 - Beginner-Friendly Deep Explanation

Understanding ZKPs - Four Analogies, Zero Maths

Analogy 1: The Ali Baba Cave - The Classic Explanation

This is the most famous analogy in all of cryptography. Imagine a circular cave with one entrance and a magic door in the middle that can only be opened with a secret password.

ENTRANCE │ ▼ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ CAVE │ │ │ │ ← PATH A PATH B → │ │ │ │ ╔═══════╗ │ │ ║ MAGIC ║ │ │ ║ DOOR ║ │ │ ╚═══════╝ │ │ (needs password) │ │ │ └─────────────────────────────┘ ALICE claims she knows the password. BOB (the Verifier) stands at the entrance and cannot see the door. ROUND 1: → Alice enters and randomly goes down Path A or B (Bob cannot see which) → Bob shouts: "Come out from Path B!" → Alice emerges from Path B ✅ (If she knew the password, she opened the door and crossed over. If she didn't know it, she had a 50% chance of guessing right.) ROUND 2: → Alice enters and randomly goes down a path → Bob shouts: "Come out from Path A!" → Alice emerges from Path A ✅ ROUND 3, 4, 5... 30 ROUNDS: → Alice emerges correctly every single time. After 30 rounds: probability of cheating = (1/2)^30 ≈ 1 in 1,000,000,000 Bob is now CONVINCED Alice knows the password. But Bob learned NOTHING about the password itself. ✅ THIS IS ZERO-KNOWLEDGE.
🗝️
The Critical Insight

Bob is convinced because the only explanation for Alice emerging from the correct path every single time is that she can open the magic door. But he never saw the password. He never saw her use it. He gained zero knowledge about the secret itself - only the fact that she knows it.

Analogy 2: The Colourblind Friend - Proving Without Revealing

You have two balls - one red, one blue. Your friend is colourblind and cannot tell them apart. You want to prove to them that the balls are indeed different colours - without revealing which one is red and which is blue.

1

Your friend holds one ball in each hand behind their back

They may or may not secretly swap the balls between hands.

2

They bring their hands forward

You (the Prover) state whether they swapped or not.

3

Repeat many times

If the balls were identical (same colour), you'd have a 50% chance of guessing whether they swapped. After 30 rounds of correct answers, the probability you're cheating is 1 in a billion. Your friend is convinced the balls are different colours - without knowing which is which.

Analogy 3: The Aadhaar Age Verification - A Real ZKP Design

Swiggy wants to sell alcohol to users who are 18 or older. Currently, you have to upload your Aadhaar card - revealing your full name, date of birth, address, and photo - just to prove you're old enough.

❌ Today's Approach (Privacy Nightmare)

Upload Entire Aadhaar

Swiggy sees: Full name, exact DOB, exact address, Aadhaar number, father's name, photo. You reveal 100% of your identity to prove ONE FACT: age ≥ 18.

✅ ZKP Approach (Privacy Preserved)

Zero-Knowledge Age Proof

A ZK circuit takes your DOB (from Aadhaar) and today's date as private inputs. It outputs a cryptographic proof that "DOB is at least 18 years before today." Swiggy verifies the proof. They learn: age ≥ 18. Nothing else. Not your name. Not your exact age. Not your address.

🧠
The ZKP Mindset Shift

Traditional systems ask: "Share your data so I can verify it." ZKP systems ask: "Prove a specific fact about your data, share nothing else." The computation moves from the verifier (who would need all your data) to a ZK circuit (which processes your private data locally and produces only a proof). The verifier checks the proof - not the data.

The Three Formal Properties of Any ZKP

Formally, for a proof system to be called Zero-Knowledge, it must satisfy all three of these properties simultaneously:

✅

Completeness

If the statement is true AND the prover is honest, the verifier will always accept the proof. An honest prover can always convince an honest verifier.

🔒

Soundness

If the statement is false, a cheating prover cannot convince the verifier to accept - except with negligible probability. A dishonest prover cannot fake a valid proof.

🫥

Zero-Knowledge

If the statement is true, the verifier learns absolutely nothing about the witness (the secret) beyond the single fact that the statement is true.

📝
The Witness

In ZKP terminology, the witness is the secret information that the prover knows and uses to construct the proof. In our age example: the witness is your exact date of birth. The public statement is "age ≥ 18." The ZKP proves the statement is true while keeping the witness completely private.

05 - Technical Deep Dive

zk-SNARKs, zk-STARKs, and Bulletproofs

From Interactive to Non-Interactive: Why It Matters for Blockchain

The Ali Baba cave analogy is an Interactive ZKP - Alice and Bob must go back and forth many times. This works between two people in real time, but it is useless for a blockchain smart contract - there is no "Bob" to send challenges in real time.

The breakthrough that made ZKPs practical for blockchain was making them Non-Interactive (NIZKs) - the prover generates a single, self-contained proof that any verifier can check instantly, without any back-and-forth. The key technique is the Fiat-Shamir heuristic - replacing the verifier's random challenges with a cryptographic hash of the proof so far, making the proof its own challenge source.

zk-SNARKs - Succinct Non-Interactive Arguments of Knowledge

The most widely deployed ZKP system in blockchain today. Let's break the name down:

  • Succinct - The proof is tiny (typically 128 - 288 bytes) regardless of the complexity of the computation being proved. The verifier's work is minimal.
  • Non-Interactive - The prover generates the proof once. No back-and-forth needed.
  • Arguments - Computationally sound (secure against polynomial-time adversaries).
  • of Knowledge - Not just "the statement is true" but "the prover actually knows the witness."

The Trusted Setup - zk-SNARKs' Critical Weakness

Most zk-SNARK systems (like Groth16, used in Zcash and early Tornado Cash) require a Trusted Setup Ceremony: a one-time computation that generates public parameters for the proof system. If the randomness used in this ceremony is kept secret by anyone who participated, they could potentially forge proofs. This is called the "toxic waste" problem.

☢️
The Toxic Waste Problem

In a trusted setup ceremony, participants generate random numbers and then destroy them. If even one participant destroys their secret, the system is secure - an attacker would need to obtain ALL participants' secrets. Zcash's trusted setup involved thousands of participants across multiple continents. The risk is real but manageable at scale. Newer SNARK systems like PLONK use a "universal" trusted setup - one ceremony covers all circuits. zk-STARKs and Bulletproofs require NO trusted setup.

zk-STARKs - Scalable Transparent Arguments of Knowledge

Invented by Eli Ben-Sasson (StarkWare) in 2018. STARKs solve SNARKs' two biggest weaknesses:

  • 🔑 Transparent (no trusted setup): STARKs use public randomness (hash functions) instead of a toxic-waste ceremony. Anyone can verify the setup parameters.
  • 🔮 Quantum-resistant: STARKs rely on hash function security (collision resistance) - not elliptic curve discrete logs. Quantum computers (Shor's Algorithm) cannot break them.
  • 📏 Larger proofs: The trade-off - STARK proofs are larger (40 - 200 KB) compared to SNARK proofs (~288 bytes). But verification is extremely fast and parallelisable.
  • ⚡ Better scalability: Proving time scales quasi-linearly with computation, making STARKs better for very large computations.

Used in: StarkNet, StarkEx (dYdX, Immutable X), and the future Ethereum validity proofs.

Bulletproofs - No Trusted Setup, Compact Range Proofs

Bulletproofs (Bünz et al., 2018) are a ZKP system specifically optimised for range proofs - proving that a hidden value lies within a specific range (e.g., "my account balance is between 0 and 2⁶⁴ - meaning it is non-negative - without revealing the balance"). They require no trusted setup and produce compact proofs. Used by Monero for confidential transactions, and in Grin/MimbleWimble.

How a ZK Circuit Works - The Computation Blueprint

To create a ZKP for a computation, you must first convert that computation into an arithmetic circuit - a series of addition and multiplication gates over a finite field. This circuit is then compiled into a mathematical representation (R1CS - Rank 1 Constraint System, or AIR - Algebraic Intermediate Representation for STARKs). The prover evaluates the circuit with private inputs (the witness) and uses polynomial commitments to generate a proof that they know inputs satisfying all the constraints - without revealing the inputs.

Age Verification Circuit (Simplified)

Private input (witness): birthdate (e.g., 15 March 1999)

Public input: current_date (e.g., 1 January 2025)

Public output: true or false

Circuit constraint: (current_date - birthdate) ≥ 18 years

The circuit also verifies: birthdate is signed by UIDAI's key (proving it came from a genuine Aadhaar record)

Result: A 288-byte proof that Swiggy verifies in <1 millisecond. They learn: age ≥ 18. Nothing else.

07 - Step-by-Step Workflow

The Five "W" Questions Answered

❓ WHY Do ZKPs Exist - What Was Impossible Before Them?

Before ZKPs, the only way to prove you knew something was to reveal it. If you wanted to prove you knew a password, you typed it. If you wanted to prove your income, you showed your salary slips. If you wanted to prove a computation was done correctly, you showed all the intermediate steps. ZKPs break this fundamental constraint - you can prove knowledge or computation without revealing the underlying data or steps.

❓ WHAT Are the Two Main Blockchain Use-Cases?

Use-Case 1: Privacy

Confidential Transactions

Prove a transaction is valid (sender has enough balance, amounts are non-negative, signatures are correct) without revealing sender, receiver, or amount. Used in Zcash, Aztec, Tornado Cash, and MST's future privacy layer.

Use-Case 2: Scalability

ZK-Rollups

Prove that 10,000 transactions were executed correctly off-chain with a single 288-byte proof on-chain. The Layer 1 contract verifies the proof instead of re-executing every transaction. Used in zkSync, StarkNet, Polygon zkEVM.

❓ HOW Is a ZK Circuit Actually Written?

ZK circuits are written in domain-specific languages:

  • Circom: The most popular ZK circuit language. Used with SnarkJS. Circuit looks like a constrained program with inputs (private + public) and outputs. Used by Tornado Cash and many DeFi protocols.
  • Noir (Aztec): A Rust-like ZK language that compiles to PLONK proofs. Aimed at developer usability.
  • Cairo (StarkWare): A Turing-complete language that compiles to STARK-provable computations. Used for StarkNet smart contracts.
  • Halo2 (Zcash, Scroll): A PLONK-based proving system with recursive proofs (proofs of proofs).

❓ WHERE Are ZKPs Used in the Real World Right Now?

💰

Zcash - Shielded Transactions

Every "shielded" Zcash transaction uses a Groth16 zk-SNARK to prove the transaction is valid (no double-spend, amounts balance) without revealing sender, receiver, or amount.

⚡

zkSync / StarkNet - ZK-Rollups

Process thousands of transactions per second off-chain. A SNARK or STARK proof is posted to Ethereum proving correctness. Full Ethereum security at 100× the throughput.

🪪

Polygon ID - ZK Identity

Issue verifiable credentials (KYC status, age, accreditation) as ZK proofs. Prove you passed KYC without revealing which documents you submitted or to whom.

🏛️

MACI - ZK Voting

Minimum Anti-Collusion Infrastructure. Uses ZKPs to make voting anonymous while proving the tally is correct. Used in Gitcoin grants and Ethereum governance.

🔗

MST WASMify - ZK Data Bridge

Off-chain computation results (GPS data, IoT sensors, API responses) are proved correct using ZKPs before being used by on-chain smart contracts. Trustless data bridges.

🃏

MST FortunaX / SatvaShuffle

ZK proofs prove that random numbers (for lotteries) and card shuffles (for Poker/Rummy) were generated fairly - without revealing the shuffle algorithm's internal state.

❓ WHAT Are the Limitations of ZKPs Right Now?

  • ⚠️ Proving time is slow: Generating a ZK proof for a complex computation takes seconds to minutes - much slower than simply running the computation. This is improving rapidly with hardware acceleration (ZK-ASICs, GPUs).
  • ⚠️ Trusted setup risk: zk-SNARKs with per-circuit trusted setups require ceremony integrity. PLONK and STARKs reduce this concern.
  • ⚠️ Developer complexity: Writing ZK circuits is hard - it requires thinking in terms of arithmetic constraints, not regular programming. Newer languages (Noir, Cairo) are making this easier.
  • ⚠️ Proof size vs verification trade-off: STARKs are quantum-safe but produce large proofs. SNARKs produce tiny proofs but need a trusted setup and are not quantum-safe.

Case Study 2: Tornado Cash - ZK Privacy on Ethereum

Tornado Cash was a privacy protocol on Ethereum that used Groth16 zk-SNARKs to break the link between deposit and withdrawal addresses. Here's how it worked:

1

Alice generates a random secret and deposits 1 ETH

Alice generates a random 31-byte secret r, commits keccak256(r) to the smart contract, and deposits 1 ETH. The commitment is stored on-chain. Alice's address is publicly linked to this commitment.

2

Later, Bob generates a ZK proof to withdraw

Bob (who may be Alice using a fresh wallet) proves via a SNARK: "I know a secret r such that keccak256(r) is in the commitment set, and this specific nullifier has not been used before." The proof reveals the nullifier (preventing double-withdrawal) but NOT which commitment it corresponds to.

3

Smart contract verifies and sends 1 ETH to Bob's address

The contract verifies the SNARK proof, marks the nullifier as used, and sends 1 ETH to Bob's fresh address. No link between Alice's deposit address and Bob's withdrawal address exists on-chain.

Case Study 3: MST WASMify - ZK Proofs for Real-World Data

MST's WASMify protocol allows off-chain computations to produce ZK-verifiable results. Example: a Swiggy delivery payment contract needs to verify a delivery was completed (GPS data shows rider reached destination) before releasing payment. Without ZKPs, the GPS data must be put on-chain - expensive and privacy-invasive. With WASMify:

  1. The delivery rider's GPS coordinates are processed off-chain in a WASMify module
  2. The module computes: "Did GPS path reach within 100m of destination?" → TRUE
  3. A ZK proof is generated proving this computation was done correctly on genuine GPS data
  4. The MST smart contract verifies the 288-byte proof and releases payment to the rider
  5. No GPS coordinates ever go on-chain. Privacy preserved. Computation verified. ✅
11 - Assessment Preparation

Top Must-Know Facts Before the Test

  • A Zero-Knowledge Proof lets a Prover convince a Verifier that a statement is true without revealing anything about the witness (the secret used to prove it).
  • Three formal properties: Completeness (honest prover always convinces), Soundness (dishonest prover cannot fake), Zero-Knowledge (verifier learns nothing extra).
  • The witness is the private secret input that the prover knows - e.g., the exact birthdate in an age proof, or the private key in a signature proof.
  • Interactive ZKPs require back-and-forth communication (Ali Baba cave). Non-Interactive ZKPs (NIZKs) produce a single self-contained proof - required for blockchains.
  • zk-SNARKs: Tiny proof (~200 bytes), very fast verification, requires trusted setup, NOT quantum-safe. Used by Zcash, zkSync, Polygon zkEVM.
  • zk-STARKs: Large proof (~100 KB), no trusted setup, quantum-safe (hash-based). Used by StarkNet, StarkEx, dYdX.
  • Bulletproofs: No trusted setup, compact (~1 KB), slow verification (linear). Best for range proofs. Used by Monero.
  • The Trusted Setup (toxic waste) in SNARKs: a one-time ceremony that must be done securely. If all participants destroy their secret, the system is safe. PLONK uses a universal setup. STARKs and Bulletproofs require none.
  • ZK-Rollups: Execute thousands of transactions off-chain, post a single ZK proof on-chain. Ethereum verifies the proof, not each transaction. Instant finality (no fraud window unlike Optimistic Rollups).
  • The Fiat-Shamir heuristic transforms an interactive ZKP into a non-interactive one by replacing the verifier's random challenge with a hash of the prover's commitment - making the proof self-contained.
  • ZK circuit languages: Circom (JavaScript-like, with SnarkJS), Noir (Rust-like, Aztec), Cairo (StarkWare), Halo2 (Zcash). Each compiles to a proof-system-specific format.
  • MST uses ZKPs in: WASMify (off-chain data verification), FortunaX (provably fair RNG), SatvaShuffle (provably fair card shuffles), and future privacy transaction layers.
⚠️
Common Exam Trap - ZKP ≠ Encryption

A ZKP does not encrypt your data. It generates a proof that you know something. The data stays entirely off-chain with you - it is never transmitted, even in encrypted form. A ZKP is not a secure channel; it is a mathematical proof of knowledge. The Verifier never receives your secret in any form.

⚠️
Common Exam Trap - ZK-Rollup vs Optimistic Rollup Finality

ZK-Rollups have instant finality - once the proof is verified on-chain, the state is final. Optimistic Rollups have a 7-day challenge window - funds are locked for a week waiting for potential fraud proofs. For questions about finality time, ZK-Rollups win decisively.

12 - Security Notes

Critical Security Principles

🔴
The Trusted Setup Is a Real Attack Surface

If a trusted setup ceremony is compromised - specifically, if even one participant retains their toxic waste secret - they can create fake proofs that appear mathematically valid. This would allow minting tokens from thin air or bypassing all verification. The Zcash Sapling ceremony involved dozens of independent participants across the world precisely to minimise this risk. For production systems, prefer PLONK (universal setup) or STARKs (no setup) where possible.

🔴
Soundness Is Only Computational - Not Information-Theoretic

ZKP soundness guarantees that a cheating prover cannot fool the verifier "except with negligible probability." This relies on the hardness of underlying mathematical problems (discrete log, hash collisions). If these problems become easy (e.g., via quantum computers for SNARK-based systems), the soundness guarantee breaks. This is why SNARKs are not quantum-safe - Shor's Algorithm could break ECC pairings.

🛡️
Recursive ZKPs - Proof of Proofs

Advanced ZKP systems like Halo2 and Nova support recursive proof composition - a ZKP that proves another ZKP is valid. This enables "proof aggregation" - compressing millions of individual proofs into one. Ethereum's "Proof of Ethereum" vision uses this to prove the correctness of the entire Ethereum execution history in a single proof. MST's post-quantum roadmap includes recursive STARK proofs for the same purpose.

14 - Glossary

Key Terms You Must Know

Zero-Knowledge Proof
A cryptographic proof that convinces a verifier that a statement is true without revealing any information about the witness (the secret used to prove it).
Prover
The party who knows the secret (witness) and generates the ZKP. Goal: convince the verifier without revealing the secret.
Verifier
The party who checks the proof. Goal: determine if the statement is true without learning the secret. In blockchain: often a smart contract.
Witness
The private secret input to a ZK circuit - the information the prover knows and uses to generate the proof but never reveals. E.g., exact birthdate, private key, exact balance.
Completeness
ZKP property: if the statement is true and the prover is honest, the verifier always accepts. An honest prover can always succeed.
Soundness
ZKP property: if the statement is false, a dishonest prover cannot produce an accepted proof (except with negligible probability). Cheating is computationally infeasible.
zk-SNARK
Succinct Non-Interactive Argument of Knowledge. Tiny proof (~200 bytes), fast verification, requires trusted setup, not quantum-safe. Used in Zcash, zkSync, Polygon zkEVM.
zk-STARK
Scalable Transparent Argument of Knowledge. Large proof (~100 KB), no trusted setup, quantum-safe (hash-based). Used in StarkNet, StarkEx, dYdX.
Bulletproofs
ZKP system optimised for range proofs. No trusted setup, proof ~1 KB, verification is slow (linear). Used in Monero for confidential transaction amounts.
Trusted Setup
A one-time ceremony required by most zk-SNARK systems to generate public parameters. Participants must destroy secret randomness ("toxic waste"). Compromise allows forging proofs.
ZK-Rollup
A Layer 2 scaling solution that executes transactions off-chain and posts a single ZK proof on-chain. The L1 verifies the proof, not each transaction - enabling 100-1000× throughput.
Fiat-Shamir Heuristic
A technique that converts an interactive ZKP into a non-interactive one by replacing the verifier's random challenge with a hash of the prover's commitment. Makes blockchain-compatible proofs.
ZK Circuit
The arithmetic representation of a computation that a ZK proof system can prove. Written in languages like Circom, Noir, or Cairo. Defines constraints that the witness must satisfy.
Recursive ZKP
A ZKP that proves the validity of another ZKP - "proof of a proof." Enables aggregating millions of proofs into one. Used in Halo2, Nova, and Ethereum's future proof-of-everything vision.

What is the "Trusted Setup" in zk-SNARKs, and why is it considered a risk?

The Trusted Setup generates the public proving and verification keys for a zk-SNARK system using secret randomness. Each participant contributes randomness and then destroys their share. (a) Actors (1 mark): Four actors: (1) User (Priya) - holds an Aadhaar-issued verifiable credential containing her birthdate; (2) UIDAI - issued and cryptographically signed the birthdate credential with UIDAI's private key; (3) ZK Proof Generator - runs locally on Priya's phone, takes private inputs and generates the proof; (4) Swiggy's Smart Contract on MST - the verifier that checks the proof and gates the purchase.

Statement: "A ZK-Rollup is less secure than the base Layer 1 blockchain it posts proofs to, because transactions are executed off-chain and an attacker could submit fraudulent state transitions."

This statement is FALSE.

This describes Optimistic Rollups (which rely on a fraud window), not ZK-Rollups. A ZK-Rollup is actually as secure as the base Layer 1 - not less secure. Here is why:

When a ZK-Rollup batch is submitted to the Layer 1 smart contract, it includes a cryptographic ZK proof (e.g., a SNARK or STARK) that mathematically proves every transaction in the batch was executed correctly - valid signatures, no double-spends, correct balance updates. An attacker cannot submit fraudulent state transitions because they cannot generate a valid proof for a false state.
