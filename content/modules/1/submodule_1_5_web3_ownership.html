3.2 - The Key Turning Points That Led to Web 3.0

  1. 1
    2008 - Bitcoin: "What if money needed no bank?"
    Satoshi Nakamoto published the Bitcoin whitepaper. For the first time in history, two strangers on opposite sides of the planet could exchange value without any bank, government, or intermediary. A smart contract replaced the bank. The ledger was public. Nobody owned it. Everyone trusted it.
  2. 2
    2013 - Ethereum: "What if contracts needed no lawyer?"
    Vitalik Buterin proposed Ethereum - a blockchain that could run arbitrary code (smart contracts). Now not just money, but any agreement could be automated: "If the product arrives, automatically pay the seller." If the conditions were met, the code executed. No lawyer. No judge. No delay.
  3. 3
    2017 - ICO Boom & NFT Concepts: "What if content had verifiable ownership?"
    The Initial Coin Offering (ICO) era introduced the concept of tokenised ownership. ERC-721 (the NFT standard) was proposed - any digital asset could now have a unique, verifiable, on-chain owner. A PUBG skin, a music album, a piece of land in a game - all could be provably owned by one wallet address.
  4. 4
    2021 - 2024 - DeFi, DAOs, and the Ownership Economy Matures
    Decentralised Finance (DeFi) protocols replaced banks for lending, borrowing, and earning yield. Decentralised Autonomous Organisations (DAOs) replaced corporate governance. Users voted on protocol decisions with tokens. The full vision crystallised: the internet can be owned and governed by its users, not by corporations.
  5. 5
    2024 - MST Blockchain: "What if Web 3.0 was as easy as Swiggy?"
    MST Blockchain launched with the SARAL Protocol - allowing users to access Web 3.0 applications with Google login, no seed phrases, no browser extensions. For the first time, the full power of blockchain ownership was accessible to a regular person in Mumbai who has never heard of MetaMask.

4.1 - The Three Pillars of Web 3.0

🌐
1. Decentralisation
No single company, server, or person controls the network. Thousands of computers (nodes/validators) worldwide collectively run the system. No one can shut it down, censor it, or change the rules unilaterally.
🤝
2. Trustlessness
You don't need to trust a company. You trust the code. Smart contracts execute automatically and identically every time. No human can interfere mid-execution. "Don't trust, verify" - anyone can read the contract's code.
🔑
3. User Ownership
You own your data via cryptographic keys (wallet). You own digital assets as on-chain tokens. You own your reputation via on-chain history. No platform can revoke this ownership. You control what you share and what you don't.
🔓
4. Permissionlessness
Anyone can deploy a smart contract. Anyone can build on an open blockchain protocol. No approval from Google, Apple, or any authority required. Like the early internet - truly open for any developer worldwide.
👁
5. Transparency
All transactions are publicly verifiable on a blockchain explorer. The smart contract code is public. Anyone can audit the system. There are no hidden rules, no opaque algorithms, no secret commission structures.
🔒
6. Censorship Resistance
No one can remove your content, freeze your funds, or block your transactions (if the network is decentralised enough). Unlike YouTube deleting videos or banks freezing accounts, a blockchain transaction is final and irreversible.

4.2 - What Does "Trustless" Actually Mean?

"Trustless" is the most misunderstood word in blockchain. It does NOT mean "you can't trust anyone." It means "you don't NEED to trust any individual or company - you trust the math instead."

🔢 Analogy: The ATM

When you use an ATM to withdraw ₹5,000, you don't need to trust the bank teller. You don't need to know if the bank manager is honest today. You trust the machine's code: "If PIN correct and balance ≥ ₹5,000, dispense cash." The code executes the same way every time for everyone. A blockchain smart contract is an ATM for any agreement - not just cash withdrawal. It executes automatically when conditions are met, no human intermediary required, no possibility of corruption at the point of execution.

🔐 Trust Comparison: Web 2.0 vs Web 3.0
  WEB 2.0 — TRUST-BASED:
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  You buy on Amazon. You trust:
  ├─ That Amazon won't delete your purchase history ──→ POSSIBLE (has happened)
  ├─ That Amazon won't change return policy ──────────→ HAPPENS regularly
  ├─ That Amazon won't discriminate your visibility ──→ DOCUMENTED CASES
  ├─ That Amazon won't share your data ───────────────→ THEY DO (advertisers)
  └─ That Amazon will still exist in 10 years ────────→ UNCERTAIN

  You trust Amazon's PROMISES. Promises can be broken.

  WEB 3.0 — TRUSTLESS (VERIFY, DON'T TRUST):
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  You buy on a blockchain marketplace. You trust:
  ├─ The smart contract code ─────────────────────────→ PUBLIC, AUDITABLE
  ├─ That funds go to seller when delivery confirmed ─→ CODED INTO CONTRACT
  ├─ That your history is on-chain ───────────────────→ PERMANENT, IMMUTABLE
  ├─ That rules won't change without governance vote ─→ ENFORCED BY PROTOCOL
  └─ That platform will exist ────────────────────────→ BLOCKCHAIN HAS NO OFF SWITCH

  You trust CRYPTOGRAPHIC PROOF. Proof cannot be broken.

Security Notes

9.1 - The Private Key Problem

In Web 3.0, your private key IS you. If you lose it, you lose everything in that wallet - permanently. No customer care, no password reset, no government authority can recover it. This is both the greatest strength (no one can steal your money if they can't get your key) and the greatest risk (carelessness can cost everything).

⚠️ The $300M Mistake

In 2017, a developer accidentally locked $300 million USD worth of Ethereum by triggering a bug in a wallet smart contract - forever. Nobody could access it. No court order, no hack, no negotiation could recover it. The code executed as written. This is why smart contract auditing is one of the highest-paid roles in blockchain, and why MST's grant program includes a mandatory security audit before scaling.

9.2 - Immutability: Feature or Bug?

Smart contracts cannot be changed after deployment. This is their greatest strength (trustlessness) and their most dangerous property. A bug in a deployed contract is permanent. This is why MST recommends the Upgradeable Proxy pattern (Module 10) and requires audits before mainnet deployment. MST's grant program provides a free security audit for qualifying projects.

Glossary / Key Terms

Web 3.0

The third generation of the web, built on blockchain technology. Adds user ownership and decentralisation to Web 2.0's interactivity. Core principles: decentralisation, trustlessness, user sovereignty.

dApp (Decentralised Application)

An application whose backend runs on a blockchain (smart contract) rather than a centralised server. Frontend may be hosted on IPFS. No single company controls it.

Smart Contract

Self-executing code stored on a blockchain. Runs automatically when conditions are met. Cannot be modified after deployment. Replaces middlemen like brokers, escrow agents, and payment processors.

Trustless

Does not require trust in any person or institution. Trust is placed in cryptographic proof and public code instead. "Don't trust, verify" - anyone can audit the system.

Wallet (Crypto)

Software that stores cryptographic key pairs. Your public key = your address. Your private key = your signature/proof of ownership. Controls all on-chain assets.

Private Key

A secret 256-bit number that proves ownership of a wallet. Signs transactions. Must NEVER be shared. If lost, wallet contents are permanently inaccessible.

Public Key / Address

Derived from private key. Shareable - like a bank account number. Anyone can send tokens to it. Cannot be used to access the wallet or sign transactions.

Decentralised Storage (IPFS)

InterPlanetary File System. Files stored across a network of computers, addressed by their content hash. No single server to take down. Used for NFT metadata and dApp frontends.

Permissionlessness

Anyone can participate without needing approval from a gatekeeper. Deploy smart contracts, use the network, build applications - no App Store approval, no company registration needed.

DID (Decentralised Identifier)

A W3C standard for self-sovereign identity. A cryptographic identifier that you control, not dependent on any company's server. Your Web 3.0 "Aadhaar" - but controlled only by you.

SARAL Protocol (MST)

MST Blockchain's user onboarding system. Allows users to create non-custodial wallets using Google, Facebook, or mobile number login - no seed phrases or browser extensions required.

Censorship Resistance

Property of a decentralised network where no single entity can block transactions, remove content, or freeze accounts. No government or company has an "off switch" for a sufficiently decentralised network.

Question 4MCQ1 MarkMedium
What is the primary function of IPFS (InterPlanetary File System) in a Web 3.0 application?
  • A
    To execute smart contracts faster than a standard blockchain
  • B
    To provide a decentralised alternative to DNS (domain name system)
  • C
    To store files in a decentralised manner using content-based addressing so no single company controls the data
  • D
    To replace crypto wallets with simpler password-based authentication
✅ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: IPFS is a peer-to-peer file storage protocol. Unlike traditional web servers (where a file lives at amazon.com/file.jpg and Amazon can delete it), IPFS stores files across thousands of nodes and addresses them by their content hash. If the content exists anywhere on the IPFS network, it's accessible - no single server to take down. This is crucial for Web 3.0: if your NFT's image is stored on IPFS, the NFT truly cannot be deleted even if the company that created it goes bankrupt. IPFS does not execute smart contracts (that's the blockchain's job), does not replace DNS (that's separate), and does not affect wallet authentication.
