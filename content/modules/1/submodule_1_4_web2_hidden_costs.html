Background Knowledge - How Platforms Learned to Extract

3.1 - The Original Promise: Cut Out the Middleman

When the internet first went commercial (mid-1990s), the big idea was disintermediation - removing middlemen. A farmer in Pune could sell vegetables directly to a buyer in Delhi. A musician could sell songs directly to fans. No middlemen, lower costs, higher profits for creators.

📦 The Old Supply Chain (Pre-Internet)

Manufacturer → Distributor → Wholesaler → Retailer → Customer
Each step adds 15 - 40% margin. By the time a ₹100 product reaches you, it costs ₹300 - 400. The internet was supposed to eliminate the middle steps and make it: Manufacturer → Customer. Lower prices. More profit for makers.

What actually happened? New middlemen emerged. And they were far more powerful than the old ones - because they had data, algorithms, and network effects locking everyone in.

Old MiddlemanTheir CutNew Platform MiddlemanTheir Cut

⚠️ The Brutal Truth

The internet did not eliminate middlemen. It replaced slow, local, physical middlemen with fast, global, digital monopoly middlemen who extract the same or higher percentages - but at global scale, across billions of transactions, with no accountability.

3.2 - The App Store Tax: Apple & Google's 30% Toll Booth

In 2008, Apple launched the App Store with a simple rule: Apple takes 30% of every sale made through an iOS app. Google followed with the Play Store at the same rate. This became known as "The App Store Tax."

For 15 years, every developer who wanted to reach iPhone or Android users had to accept this. No negotiation. No alternatives. No exceptions (well, almost - recent antitrust rulings forced small reductions).

💥 Real Impact - Spotify's Lawsuit Against Apple

Spotify charges ₹149/month for premium. When you subscribe through the iPhone app, Apple takes ₹44.70 (30%). Spotify's actual cost to run the service is roughly ₹100 - 110 per user per month. After Apple's cut, Spotify made ZERO profit on iPhone subscriptions for years. This is why Spotify filed an antitrust case against Apple in Europe (2019) - and won a landmark ruling in 2024. The EU forced Apple to allow alternative payment systems.

Nothing is truly free - the cost is just hidden or redistributed.

Step-by-Step: Calculating Hidden Costs Like a Pro

Before accepting any platform deal, a smart entrepreneur (or blockchain developer building a dApp) should do this calculation. Here is the framework:

  1. 1
    Identify ALL fees charged by the platform
    Platform commission, payment processing, logistics fee, listing fee, advertising spend (required to be visible), return/refund policy costs. Many platforms don't put all of these in one place - you need to dig through their pricing pages and merchant agreements.
  2. 2
    Calculate fees as percentage of your REVENUE (not profit)
    Platforms always quote fees as a percentage of selling price. But the impact should be measured against your gross margin. A 28% commission on ₹500 revenue removes 28% of revenue - but if your margin was 40%, that commission actually consumes 70% of your profit margin.
  3. 3
    Add government taxes that interact with platform fees
    GST on services (18%) is charged on the platform's commission, not on the total transaction. But you still have to charge and collect GST on your sale price. These interact in complex ways - always calculate net GST impact separately.
  4. 4
    Add hidden operational costs required by the platform
    Swiggy mandates specific packaging. Amazon requires specific barcodes and labelling. App stores require specific frameworks, APIs, and review processes that cost developer time. These "compliance costs" are real costs imposed by the platform that rarely appear in quoted commission rates.
  5. 5
    Compare against direct-to-customer alternative
    Calculate what you would earn selling the same product/service directly (own website, own app, blockchain marketplace). The difference is the true "platform tax" - the real cost of access to their network. This number often shocks business owners into rethinking their strategy.
  6. 6
    Assess the switching cost / lock-in risk
    If 60%+ of your revenue comes from one platform, your business is existentially dependent on that platform's decisions. This is a massive risk that rarely appears in standard cost calculations. A blockchain-based alternative reduces this risk to near zero because your reputation and customer relationships are on-chain and portable.
The real cost of a platform is not just the commission rate. It is the commission + payment fees + compliance costs + lock-in risk + loss of the right to negotiate.

Security Notes - Hidden Risks Beyond Hidden Costs

9.1 - Payment Data Breaches

Every time you store your card on a platform (Swiggy, Amazon, Flipkart), your card details live in their database. Platforms are high-value targets - a breach exposes millions of card numbers. India saw multiple major data breaches: MobiKwik (2021, 3.5M users), BigBasket (2020, 20M users), Domino's India (2021, 18M orders exposed). The cost of these breaches falls on users and banks, not platforms.

🗝️ Blockchain Solution

Blockchain wallets never share your private key with any platform. When you pay from a crypto wallet, the platform sees only a transaction hash - not your wallet details, not your card number, not your identity. Nothing to steal. MST's SARAL Protocol provides this non-custodial security with a user experience as simple as Google login.

9.2 - Algorithmic Manipulation Risk

Platforms can and do manipulate their own algorithms to favour their own products over third-party sellers. Amazon India has faced allegations of using seller data to create competing products. Swiggy has been alleged to promote its own cloud kitchen brands (The Bowl Company, etc.) over independent restaurants in search results. There is no transparency, no audit, and no recourse for affected sellers.

10.1 - ONDC: India's Attempt at a Decentralised Commerce Protocol

The Government of India launched ONDC (Open Network for Digital Commerce) in 2022 - an open protocol (not a platform) that allows any buyer app to transact with any seller app. Like UPI for e-commerce. Paytm, PhonePe, and hundreds of local apps can plug in as buyer/seller nodes. Commission structure is open and capped. Early results (2023 - 2024) show 60 - 70% lower commission for restaurant owners compared to Swiggy/Zomato. ONDC processed 7 million orders/month by late 2023.

ONDC is essentially applying blockchain's philosophy (open protocol, no central platform owner) without blockchain's technology. Combining ONDC's open protocol with MST Blockchain's smart contracts, immutable records, and tokenised incentives could be transformative for Indian commerce.

10.2 - The 30% Number Appears Everywhere - Is It Coincidence?

Apple: 30%. Google: 30%. Amazon: ~30% average. Steam (games): 30%. Epic Games Store explicitly undercut Steam by charging 12% to attract developers - and successfully won market share. The 30% figure is not economically determined - it is the monopoly price. It is what you charge when users cannot leave. Blockchain marketplaces that charge 2 - 3% will, over time, attract volume-sensitive sellers who can no longer justify the 30% tax - particularly at Indian transaction sizes where margins are already thin.

Glossary / Key Terms

Platform Commission

Percentage of transaction value charged by a marketplace platform for connecting buyer and seller. Swiggy: 22 - 35%. Amazon: 5 - 45% by category.

App Store Tax

30% fee charged by Apple App Store and Google Play Store on all in-app purchases and subscriptions. Developers must accept to reach iOS/Android users.

Payment Processing Fee

Fee charged by payment gateways (Razorpay, Stripe, Visa) for processing digital transactions. Typically 0 - 3.5% depending on payment method.

Disintermediation

Removing middlemen from a transaction chain to enable direct buyer-seller interaction. The internet promised this; blockchain actually delivers it.

Network Effect

Platform becomes more valuable as more users join. More sellers attract more buyers, which attracts more sellers. Creates a self-reinforcing monopoly.

Take Rate

Industry term for the percentage a marketplace keeps from each transaction. Uber's take rate: ~25%. Swiggy's take rate: 22 - 35%.

Lock-in

Condition where switching platforms is too costly (loss of customers, ratings, history) even if the platform raises prices. Creates permanent dependency.

Gas Fee

Small fee paid to blockchain validators for processing a transaction. On MST Blockchain: ₹0.01 - 1 per transaction. Replaces platform commission as the cost of transacting.

Smart Contract Escrow

Funds locked in a smart contract until predefined conditions are met (e.g., delivery confirmed). Eliminates need for a platform to manage trust between strangers.

On-chain Reputation

Buyer/seller ratings stored on blockchain rather than a platform's database. Portable - moves with the user across platforms. Eliminates reputation lock-in.

ONDC

Open Network for Digital Commerce. India's government-backed open protocol for e-commerce. Caps commissions, allows any app to plug in as buyer or seller node.

CPM (Cost Per Mille)

Advertising metric: cost per 1,000 impressions/views. India's YouTube CPM: ₹80 - 150. Determines how much ad revenue a creator earns per view.

A restaurant lists a ₹300 dish on Swiggy. Swiggy charges a 28% commission. How much commission does Swiggy deduct from this single order?
  • A
    ₹28
  • B
    ₹56
  • C
    ₹84
  • D
    ₹96
✅ Correct Answer: C - ₹84

Calculation: Commission = Selling Price × Commission Rate = ₹300 × 0.28 = ₹84. The restaurant receives ₹300 − ₹84 = ₹216 before any other deductions (payment processing, packaging, GST). This is the most fundamental calculation in the sub-module - you must be able to do it instantly. ₹28 = 28 rupees flat (not a percentage calculation). ₹56 = 28% of ₹200 (wrong base). ₹96 = 32% of ₹300 (wrong rate).

Question 2MCQ1 MarkEasy
What percentage of every in-app purchase does Apple's App Store charge as commission from developers?
  • A
    15%
  • B
    20%
  • C
    30%
  • D
    45%
✅ Correct Answer: C - 30%

Explanation: Apple's standard App Store commission has been 30% since 2008 - the same as Google Play Store. This is called the "App Store Tax." 15% is the reduced rate introduced in 2020 for developers earning less than $1M/year (the "Small Business Program"). 45% is YouTube's commission on ad revenue (higher than Apple's). 20% is not a standard platform rate. Apple's 30% rate became the subject of Epic Games' antitrust lawsuit (2020) and EU regulatory action (2024).

Question 3MCQ1 MarkMedium
An Uber driver completes a ₹500 ride. Uber takes 25% commission. The driver spends ₹90 on fuel. What is the driver's net earning from this ride?
  • A
    ₹285
  • B
    ₹285
  • C
    ₹375
  • D
    ₹260
✅ Correct Answer: B - ₹285

Step-by-step calculation:
Step 1: Uber's commission = ₹500 × 25% = ₹125
Step 2: Driver receives = ₹500 − ₹125 = ₹375
Step 3: Subtract fuel cost = ₹375 − ₹90 = ₹285
The driver earns ₹285 net - just 57% of the ₹500 the rider paid. Uber earns ₹125 without owning a car, buying fuel, or taking any physical risk. This illustrates the platform's value extraction model: own the network, tax the labour.

Question 4MCQ1 MarkMedium
Which of the following correctly describes WHY restaurant owners cannot easily leave Swiggy even after commission increases?
  • A
    Restaurants have signed long-term legal contracts that prevent leaving
  • B
    Swiggy owns the recipes and food content of partner restaurants
  • C
    Network effects mean a large percentage of their customer base now exclusively orders via the platform - leaving would eliminate that revenue instantly
  • D
    Government regulations in India prohibit restaurants from operating without a delivery platform license
✅ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: This is the classic network effect lock-in. Swiggy built a large customer base first. Now customers default to Swiggy when ordering food. A restaurant that leaves loses access to those customers - potentially 40 - 60% of their orders - immediately. The commission increase is painful, but losing 50% of orders is even more painful. Swiggy does not own contracts (A), does not own recipes (B), and no such government regulation exists (D). The trap is entirely economic, not legal - which makes it more insidious because there's nothing to challenge in court.

Question 5MCQ1 MarkMedium
Why is UPI peer-to-peer transfer considered "free" for the end user, while it still has a real cost to someone?
  • A
    NPCI (government-backed) subsidises the infrastructure cost, which is ultimately borne by taxpayers and banks' other revenues
  • B
    UPI uses no infrastructure at all, so there is literally no cost to anyone
  • C
    Banks voluntarily cover the entire cost as a social service with no expectation of benefit
  • D
    The sender pays the fee, but it is automatically waived by the government
✅ Correct Answer: A

Explanation: Nothing is truly free. UPI P2P transfers have real infrastructure costs (servers, security, fraud management). NPCI, which is owned by a consortium of banks and backed by RBI/Government of India, absorbs these costs. Banks subsidise UPI to get users hooked on digital payments (from which they earn on other products - loans, FDs, insurance). The ultimate cost is distributed across taxpayers and bank customers through other fees. Option B is factually wrong (there is infrastructure). Option C is incorrect (banks do expect returns - data, cross-sell). Option D describes a waiver mechanism that doesn't exist.

Question 6MCQ1 MarkHard
An Indian musician releases a song. It gets 5 million streams on Spotify India at ₹0.007 per stream. After paying 10% to their distributor (DistroKid), how much does the artist actually receive?
  • A
    ₹35,000
  • B
    ₹31,500
  • C
    ₹28,000
  • D
    ₹3,500
✅ Correct Answer: B - ₹31,500

Step-by-step:
Step 1: Total Spotify payout = 5,000,000 × ₹0.007 = ₹35,000
Step 2: Distributor fee (10%) = ₹35,000 × 0.10 = ₹3,500
Step 3: Artist receives = ₹35,000 − ₹3,500 = ₹31,500
Five million streams. The artist earns ₹31,500. For context: if even 315 of those fans had bought the album directly at ₹100, the artist would have earned more. This calculation makes the streaming economy's exploitation viscerally clear.

Question 7MCQ1 MarkHard
A blockchain-based marketplace charges a 0.1% gas fee per transaction instead of a 28% platform commission. For a ₹10,000 order, what is the exact rupee saving compared to Swiggy's 28% commission?
  • A
    ₹2,700
  • B
    ₹2,790
  • C
    ₹2,800
  • D
    ₹2,000
✅ Correct Answer: B - ₹2,790

Step-by-step:
Swiggy commission = ₹10,000 × 28% = ₹2,800
Blockchain gas fee = ₹10,000 × 0.1% = ₹10
Saving = ₹2,800 − ₹10 = ₹2,790
The blockchain option costs ₹10 instead of ₹2,800 - a 99.6% reduction in middleman cost. At scale (1 crore orders/year at ₹500 average), this represents ₹1,390 crore saved annually by restaurants/sellers - returned to the actual producers of value instead of extracted by a platform.

Question 9True / False3 MarksHard
Statement: "Blockchain eliminates all costs from a transaction. A restaurant selling biryani via a blockchain marketplace would have zero fees of any kind."
Careful: Consider what blockchain actually replaces vs what remains. Are gas fees zero? What about production costs, taxes, delivery costs?
✅ Verdict: FALSE

Model Justification (Full 3 Marks):

Blockchain does not eliminate all transaction costs - it dramatically reduces the platform middleman cost (from 28 - 35% to 0.1% gas fee), but several other costs remain unchanged. Government taxes such as GST (5% on restaurant food delivery) apply regardless of whether the sale happens via Swiggy or a blockchain marketplace, because tax law applies to the economic activity, not the technology used to facilitate it. Physical production costs - ingredients, electricity, cooking - are obviously unchanged. Logistics and delivery costs (driver's fuel, time) also remain, because blockchain governs payments and trust, not physical movement of goods. What blockchain eliminates is specifically the platform's rent-seeking commission - the fee Swiggy charges for being the matchmaker between buyer and seller. A blockchain smart contract can perform this matchmaking function at cost of ₹0.10 - 1 in gas instead of ₹75 - 140 in commission. So the correct statement is: "Blockchain dramatically reduces platform middleman costs while government taxes and physical costs remain." This distinction matters enormously for building realistic financial models for Web3 businesses.

1 Mark: Correctly states FALSE
1 Mark: Identifies what blockchain DOES reduce (platform commission → gas fee)
1 Mark: Identifies what remains (GST, production costs, logistics) with correct reasoning
