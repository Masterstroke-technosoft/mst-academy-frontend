02   Real-World Introduction Story

The Ration Card That Travelled 200 km Without You

📖 Story

It is 2019. A migrant worker from Darbhanga, Bihar, travels to Delhi for construction work. At the Public Distribution System (PDS) shop in Delhi, he needs subsidised grain. But his ration card - a physical paper - is still registered in Darbhanga. The Delhi shop cannot verify his entitlement; the Bihar database is not linked. He is told, "Go back and get a transfer."


Meanwhile, his cousin in Darbhanga uses a photocopy of the same ration card to collect grain twice - once using the original card in Darbhanga, and once using the photocopy at a different shop. By the time the government realises, 600 kg of rice has vanished from the system. The culprit: a centralised, paper-based identity system that cannot be verified across borders in real time, and can be duplicated.


Now imagine the same worker with a digital identity wallet on his phone. Inside the wallet is a Verifiable Credential - a digitally signed proof issued by the Bihar Food & Civil Supplies Department - stating: "This person is entitled to 5 kg of rice per month under NFSA, Aadhaar-linked." The Delhi PDS shop scans a QR code. Instantly, the shop's system verifies the cryptographic signature from the Bihar government - without needing to call a central Bihar server. The grain is dispensed, and the transaction is recorded on the state blockchain. The credential cannot be duplicated, because the verification requires the holder's private key, and the digital signature from the Issuer (Bihar government) is unforgeable.

This is not a utopian fantasy. It is the core of W3C Decentralised Identifiers (DIDs) and Verifiable Credentials (VCs) - the global standard for self-sovereign identity. In India, this is already being piloted with DigiLocker, the National Digital Health Mission (ABHA), and blockchain-based academic certificates from IIT Madras. The technology replaces the paper ration card with a cryptographic proof that you control, and that anyone can verify offline or online.

03   Background & History

From Centralised Identity to Self-Sovereign Identity

Identity systems have evolved through four phases. Understanding this progression is essential to appreciate why DIDs and VCs represent a paradigm shift, not just a tech upgrade.

1
Centralised Identity (Pre-2000)
A single authority issues and controls identities. Example: A ration card issued by the Food Department - if the department's ledger is lost, your identity vanishes. You cannot reuse the same identity across services. Each service (bank, telco, PDS) creates its own silo.
2
Federated Identity (2000-2015)
Multiple services rely on a central identity provider. Example: "Login with Google" or "Login with Facebook." Google acts as the central gatekeeper - if Google suspends your account, you lose access to all linked services. Aadhaar authentication works similarly: UIDAI is the central verifier.
3
User-Centric Identity (2015-2020)
Users consent to share specific attributes, but the identity provider still controls the underlying credentials. Example: DigiLocker - you see your documents, but the issuing authority stores them. You cannot independently prove a document's authenticity without the issuer's server being online.
4
Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI) - 2020 onwards
You hold cryptographic proofs directly. No central server required for verification. You can prove your age without revealing your birthdate, or prove you are a college graduate without sharing your full mark sheet. DIDs and VCs are the W3C standards that make this possible. MST Blockchain's identity layer is built on SSI principles, integrating SARAL for non-custodial key management.
💡 The W3C Connection

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) published the DID Core Recommendation in July 2022 and the Verifiable Credentials Data Model v1.1 in 2022. These are open, royalty-free standards - the same W3C that gave us HTML and CSS. Any blockchain, including MST, can implement these standards, making identities interoperable across systems, borders, and chains.

04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

DIDs and VCs - Like Aadhaar, But You Hold the Proof

Let's start with a simple analogy. Your physical Aadhaar card has a 12-digit number (identifier), your name, date of birth, and a QR code that a shopkeeper can scan to verify your identity - but the verification requires UIDAI's server to respond. If UIDAI's server is down, the shopkeeper cannot verify your Aadhaar.

Now imagine a new kind of Aadhaar - let's call it "Self-Aadhaar". Instead of a 12-digit number assigned by UIDAI, you generate your own identifier - a Decentralised Identifier (DID) - that looks like did:mst:0xabc123...def. You store a document (the DID Document) on the MST Blockchain that contains your public key and service endpoints. No central authority issues this identifier; you create it yourself. Then, when a government agency needs to attest that you are over 18, they issue a Verifiable Credential - a digitally signed JSON file - directly to your wallet. The credential says: "The holder of DID:mst:0xabc... is over 18 years old" and is signed by the government's private key. Now, when a wine shop scans your QR code, their app verifies the digital signature using the government's public key (published on-chain) - all offline, no UIDAI server needed.

🔑 The Three-Party Trust Triangle

Every VC ecosystem involves exactly three roles: Issuer (e.g., a university issuing a degree), Holder (you, storing the credential in your wallet), and Verifier (an employer checking your degree). The Issuer signs the credential; the Holder presents it; the Verifier checks the signature against the Issuer's DID Document on-chain. Trust is derived from cryptography, not from a phone call to the university.

W
WHY
Why do we need DIDs instead of Aadhaar numbers?
Aadhaar numbers are centrally issued and can be revoked or correlated across services. DIDs are self-generated and can be pairwise unique - you can have a different DID for your bank, your hospital, and your grocery store, preventing cross-service tracking. Moreover, DIDs eliminate the central honey-pot: there is no UIDAI database to breach.
W
WHAT
What exactly is a Verifiable Credential?
A Verifiable Credential is a tamper-evident, cryptographically signed set of claims about a subject (the DID holder). It follows the W3C data model: @context, type, issuer, issuanceDate, credentialSubject, and a proof object (the digital signature). It can be stored in a mobile wallet and presented as a QR code or JSON-LD file. The credential itself does NOT contain sensitive data - it can use selective disclosure (e.g., prove age > 18 without revealing birthdate) via zero-knowledge proofs.
H
HOW
How does verification work without a central server?
1. The Issuer's DID and public key are recorded on the MST Blockchain. 2. The Issuer signs the VC with its private key. 3. The Holder presents the VC to the Verifier. 4. The Verifier resolves the Issuer's DID from the blockchain, retrieves the public key, and cryptographically verifies the signature - all without contacting the Issuer's server. This is called off-line verification.
W
WHERE
Where is this already used in India?
• DigiLocker: issues digitally signed documents, moving towards DID-based architecture. • ABHA (Ayushman Bharat Health Account): uses a 14-digit health ID linked to your Aadhaar; the next phase plans SSI for health records. • IIT Madras: issued blockchain-based degree certificates (2022) that can be verified without contacting the institute. • Co-WIN vaccination certificates: are digitally signed and can be verified offline - a precursor to full VCs.
L
LIMITS
What are the current limitations?
DID methods vary widely (some use blockchains, some don't). Interoperability is still maturing - not all wallets support all methods. Key recovery remains a challenge: if you lose your private key, you lose your identity, unless you have a recovery mechanism (like social recovery or SARAL). Also, legal acceptance in India for VC-based proofs vs. physical documents is still evolving.
05   Technical Deep Dive

The W3C DID Core & Verifiable Credentials Data Model

Now we go under the hood. You will encounter DIDs and VCs in almost every modern blockchain application, including MST’s identity layer. Let's dissect the specification.

5.1 Decentralised Identifiers (DID)

A DID is a globally unique identifier that does not require a centralised registration authority. It is a URI conforming to the generic format:

did:example:123456789abcdefghi
│   │       │
│   │       └── method‑specific identifier
│   └── DID method name (identifies the DID method, e.g., "mst", "ethr", "key", "web")
└── scheme (always "did")

The DID method specifies how the DID is created, resolved, updated, and deactivated. For example, did:mst:0x... uses the MST Blockchain as the verifiable data registry. did:key:z6Mk... is a self-contained method where the identifier is derived directly from the public key (no blockchain needed).

Every DID resolves to a DID Document - a JSON-LD document containing:

  • id - the DID itself
  • verificationMethod - public keys and their types (e.g., Ed25519VerificationKey2020)
  • authentication - which keys can authenticate as the DID subject
  • service - endpoints for interacting with the DID subject (e.g., a credential-exchange endpoint)
  • assertionMethod - keys authorised to issue Verifiable Credentials
  • keyAgreement - keys for encrypted communication

On the MST Blockchain, a DID Document can be stored on-chain or referenced via a smart contract registry. SARAL Protocol uses a DID method that ties a biometric-derived key pair to a did:mst: identifier, enabling non-custodial key recovery.

5.2 Verifiable Credentials (VC) Data Model

A Verifiable Credential is structured as follows (simplified W3C JSON-LD):

FIGURE 16.1-A · Structure of a Verifiable Credential
{
  "@context": [
    "https://www.w3.org/2018/credentials/v1",
    "https://www.w3.org/2018/credentials/examples/v1"
  ],
  "id": "http://university.edu/credentials/1872",
  "type": ["VerifiableCredential", "UniversityDegreeCredential"],
  "issuer": "did:mst:0xabc123...university",
  "issuanceDate": "2025-08-15T00:00:00Z",
  "credentialSubject": {
    "id": "did:mst:0xdef456...student",
    "degree": {
      "type": "Bachelor of Technology",
      "name": "Computer Science"
    }
  },
  "proof": {
    "type": "Ed25519Signature2020",
    "created": "2025-08-15T10:00:00Z",
    "verificationMethod": "did:mst:0xabc123...university#keys-1",
    "proofPurpose": "assertionMethod",
    "proofValue": "z5hTJ...signaturebase64url"
  }
}
The `proof` object contains the digital signature. The Verifier can validate it using the public key from the Issuer's DID Document. The credential is self-contained: no need to phone the university.

Selective Disclosure and Zero-Knowledge Proofs: A full VC can be presented as a whole. But advanced implementations use BBS+ signatures or ZK-SNARKs to allow the Holder to derive a subset proof. For example, from a degree credential, the Holder can prove "I graduated in 2025" without revealing the exact degree name or marks. This is how you prove you are above 18 without revealing your birthdate.

The Issuer's DID Document is the trust anchor. The Verifier never contacts the Issuer. This is off-line, cryptographically secure identity verification.

MST’s SARAL Protocol implements a did:mst method where the DID Document is stored on-chain and updated only via a threshold signature scheme derived from biometric authentication. This ensures that even if a user loses their device, they can recover their DID without a seed phrase - by proving their identity using SARAL’s secure enclave-backed recovery flow.

06   Real-World Case Studies

Indian Implementations & The MST Vision

🇮🇳
DigiLocker - The World's Largest Digital Document Wallet
DigiLocker has over 270 million users and stores 7.5 billion documents. Currently, documents are digitally signed by issuers (e.g., CBSE marksheet), but verification often requires checking with the issuer's server. The next architecture, "DigiLocker 2.0", is exploring W3C VC standards - making documents self-verifiable. Once a VC is in your DigiLocker wallet, any third party can verify it offline. MST's identity layer can provide the underlying DID registry for such a system, with SARAL enabling biometric-based wallet access.
🩺
ABHA (Health ID) - Verifiable Health Records
The Ayushman Bharat Health Account assigns a 14-digit unique health ID. In the current implementation, health records are linked via consent managers, but the records themselves are stored in centralised Health Information Exchanges. With SSI, a patient could hold a VC stating "vaccinated against COVID-19 on 15/08/2025" without revealing their full health history. The doctor verifies the VC's signature - all without accessing a central health database. MST's ZK-based privacy features can enable selective disclosure: prove you are vaccinated without revealing which vaccine or where.
🎓
IIT Madras - Blockchain Degree Certificates
In 2022, IIT Madras issued blockchain-verified degree certificates to its graduating batch. The certificate is essentially a Verifiable Credential stored on Ethereum (and could be bridged to MST). Any employer can verify the certificate by checking the digital signature against IIT Madras's DID on-chain. No phone calls, no email to the registrar. This eliminates fake degree rackets. MST’s WASMify can further enrich this: a Verifier can run an on-chain check that the credential hasn't been revoked, using a revocation registry smart contract.
🏢
MST’s SARAL + DID Integration - Bank Account Opening
Imagine opening a bank account entirely online. Instead of uploading your Aadhaar PDF and waiting for KYC verification, you present a Verifiable Credential from UIDAI (or a regulated issuer) stating "this DID holder's name is X, and their Aadhaar number is linked". The bank verifies the signature using UIDAI's public DID. The entire process takes seconds. SARAL ensures the user never sees a seed phrase - they just authenticate with fingerprint, and the wallet generates the DID and signs the presentation. This is the MST vision for India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) compliant identity layer.
07   Applied Design Exercise

Designing an MST-Powered Academic Certificate System

Let's walk through a practical scenario. Problem: Fake academic certificates cost the Indian economy ₹40,000 crore annually. A university wants to issue tamper-proof, instantly verifiable degree certificates using DIDs and VCs on the MST Blockchain. You are the blockchain architect. What do you build?

1
Step 1: DID Registry on MST
Deploy a smart contract that acts as a DID Registry. The university creates a did:mst:univ123 and writes its DID Document (public keys, service endpoints) onto the registry. Each graduating student also generates a DID (using SARAL wallet) - no personal data on-chain, only the DID and public key.
2
Step 2: Credential Issuance
The university's system creates a VC following the W3C schema: issuer = university DID, subject = student DID, degree details, and signs it with the university's private key. The VC is sent to the student's wallet. The university also records a hash of the credential on-chain (for revocation status).
3
Step 3: Verification by Employer
The student shares the VC (as a QR code or file). The employer's app resolves the university DID from MST, retrieves the public key, and verifies the signature. It also checks the on-chain revocation registry: is this credential still valid? The entire verification takes under 2 seconds, works offline (after DID resolution), and doesn't require the university's server.
4
Step 4: Privacy & Selective Disclosure
Optionally, use ZK-proofs (via WASMify) so the student can prove "I have a B.Tech degree from an AICTE-approved institution" without revealing their exact CGPA or even their name. This is the gold standard of privacy-preserving identity.

This exact architecture is what you would describe in the assessment's descriptive design question.

09   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 16.1

Decentralised Identifier (DID)
A W3C standard, self-sovereign, globally unique URI that resolves to a DID Document containing public keys and service endpoints.
DID Document
A JSON-LD file containing cryptographic material (verification methods), authentication, and services associated with a DID.
Verifiable Credential (VC)
A tamper-evident, cryptographically signed set of claims about a subject, following the W3C VC Data Model.
Issuer
The entity that creates and signs a VC, asserting claims about a subject. Its public key is recorded in its DID Document.
Holder
The entity that receives and stores VCs, typically in a digital wallet, and can present them to verifiers.
Verifier
The party that checks the authenticity of a VC by verifying the Issuer's signature against the Issuer's DID Document.
Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI)
An identity model where individuals control their own identifiers and credentials without relying on a central authority.
DigiLocker
India's national digital document wallet, evolving towards W3C VC standards for offline verifiable documents.
SARAL Protocol
MST's non-custodial key management protocol that uses biometrics and secure enclaves to generate and recover DIDs without seed phrases.
Revocation Registry
A smart contract or list that records revoked VC IDs, enabling verifiers to check if a credential is still valid.
