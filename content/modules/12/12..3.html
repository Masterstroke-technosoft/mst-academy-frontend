02   Real-World Story

The CryptoPunk That Bought a House

📖 Story

In 2022, an early CryptoPunks collector named Rahul owned Punk #5822 - a rare alien punk worth over ₹2 crore. He needed ₹40 lakh to pay for his daughter's wedding and a down payment on a new house. Selling the punk would mean losing a piece of digital history he believed would continue to appreciate, and he would incur a huge capital gains tax. So instead of selling, he did something that wasn't possible a year earlier: he borrowed ₹40 lakh against his punk using an NFT lending protocol. The loan had a 50% loan-to-value ratio (LTV), meaning the punk served as collateral for a stablecoin loan. Rahul retained ownership and could reclaim the punk by repaying the loan within 90 days. The lender earned a handsome 12% annualised interest rate. Both won.


Rahul's punk was later fractionalised - split into 10,000 ERC-20 tokens - allowing hundreds of smaller investors to own a fraction of the alien. This created liquidity, a market price for the shares, and allowed the punk to be traded on decentralised exchanges without selling the whole asset. Fractionalisation turned a single high-value NFT into a tradable, composable DeFi asset.


This sub-module will teach you exactly how these two revolutionary concepts - NFT lending and fractionalisation - work, how to build them on the MST blockchain, and why they are the bridge between NFTs and the trillion-dollar DeFi economy.

03   What Is NFTFi? - The Marriage of NFTs and DeFi

Turning Illiquid Collectibles into Financial Instruments

NFTFi (NFT Finance) is the ecosystem of financial products built on top of non-fungible tokens. Just as traditional finance allows you to borrow against your house or car, NFTFi allows you to borrow against your digital assets. And just as you can buy shares of a company, fractionalisation lets you buy shares of an NFT.

The core problem that NFTFi solves is illiquidity. A ₹2 crore CryptoPunk is valuable, but it is not easily sold. There may not be a buyer at any given moment, and selling the entire asset means giving up future appreciation. NFT lending provides liquidity without requiring a sale. Fractionalisation provides shared ownership and a market price for partial exposure.

Together, these tools transform NFTs from static collectibles into dynamic financial assets that can earn yield, serve as collateral, and be traded on decentralised exchanges. This is the natural evolution of the NFT market - from "just JPEGs" to productive, composable capital.

04   NFT Lending - Borrowing and Lending Against NFTs

Collateralised Loans Without a Bank

NFT lending works similarly to a secured loan in traditional finance, but with the entire process handled by smart contracts. There are three main types of NFT lending platforms:

1. Peer-to-Peer (P2P): A borrower lists their NFT as collateral and requests a specific loan amount and duration. Lenders browse these listings and fund loans they find attractive. When the loan is repaid, the NFT is returned. If not repaid, the lender receives the NFT. Platforms like NFTfi pioneered this model.

2. Peer-to-Pool: A collective pool of funds (usually provided by liquidity providers earning interest) is available for instant loans. Borrowers can borrow against any accepted NFT without waiting for a specific lender. BendDAO uses this model, allowing instant borrowing against blue-chip NFTs with an over-collateralisation requirement.

3. Automated Market Maker (AMM): NFTs are deposited into a pool that provides instant liquidity at algorithmically determined prices. This is still experimental but promises the most seamless experience.

Let's look at a simple P2P lending contract to understand the mechanics.

// Simple NFT‑backed loan contract (P2P)
contract NFTLoan {
    struct Loan {
        address borrower;
        address lender;
        IERC721 nftContract;
        uint256 tokenId;
        uint256 loanAmount;
        uint256 interest;        // total interest to be paid
        uint256 deadline;
        bool funded;
        bool repaid;
        bool defaulted;
    }

    mapping(uint256 => Loan) public loans;
    uint256 public loanCounter;

    event LoanRequested(uint256 indexed loanId, address borrower, address nft, uint256 tokenId, uint256 amount);
    event LoanFunded(uint256 indexed loanId, address lender);
    event LoanRepaid(uint256 indexed loanId);
    event LoanDefaulted(uint256 indexed loanId, address lender);

    // Borrower: deposit NFT and request a loan
    function requestLoan(address nftContract, uint256 tokenId, uint256 amount, uint256 interest, uint256 durationDays) public returns (uint256 loanId) {
        IERC721(nftContract).transferFrom(msg.sender, address(this), tokenId);
        loanId = loanCounter++;
        loans[loanId] = Loan({
            borrower: msg.sender,
            lender: address(0),
            nftContract: IERC721(nftContract),
            tokenId: tokenId,
            loanAmount: amount,
            interest: interest,
            deadline: block.timestamp + (durationDays * 1 days),
            funded: false,
            repaid: false,
            defaulted: false
        });
        emit LoanRequested(loanId, msg.sender, nftContract, tokenId, amount);
    }

    // Lender: fund the loan by sending the requested amount
    function fundLoan(uint256 loanId) public payable {
        Loan storage loan = loans[loanId];
        require(!loan.funded, "Already funded");
        require(msg.value == loan.loanAmount, "Incorrect amount");
        loan.lender = msg.sender;
        loan.funded = true;
        payable(loan.borrower).transfer(msg.value);
        emit LoanFunded(loanId, msg.sender);
    }

    // Borrower: repay loan (principal + interest)
    function repayLoan(uint256 loanId) public payable {
        Loan storage loan = loans[loanId];
        require(loan.funded && !loan.repaid && !loan.defaulted, "Invalid state");
        require(msg.sender == loan.borrower, "Only borrower");
        uint256 totalDue = loan.loanAmount + loan.interest;
        require(msg.value == totalDue, "Incorrect repayment amount");
        loan.repaid = true;
        // Return NFT to borrower
        loan.nftContract.transferFrom(address(this), loan.borrower, loan.tokenId);
        // Transfer repayment to lender
        payable(loan.lender).transfer(totalDue);
        emit LoanRepaid(loanId);
    }

    // Lender (or anyone): claim NFT if loan not repaid by deadline
    function claimDefault(uint256 loanId) public {
        Loan storage loan = loans[loanId];
        require(loan.funded && !loan.repaid && !loan.defaulted, "Invalid state");
        require(block.timestamp > loan.deadline, "Not yet overdue");
        loan.defaulted = true;
        loan.nftContract.transferFrom(address(this), loan.lender, loan.tokenId);
        emit LoanDefaulted(loanId, loan.lender);
    }
}

Key parameters in NFT lending:

Loan-to-Value (LTV): The ratio of the loan amount to the NFT's value. If a lender values a punk at 10 ETH, they might offer a loan of 5 ETH (50% LTV). This protects the lender from price fluctuations.

Duration: How long the borrower has to repay. Typical durations are 7, 30, or 90 days.

Interest rate (APR): The cost of borrowing, usually annualised. Rates range from 5-30% depending on the NFT's volatility and liquidity.

Liquidation: If the borrower fails to repay by the deadline, the lender can claim the NFT. There is no auction - the smart contract automatically transfers the NFT to the lender.

05   Fractionalisation - Splitting an NFT into Shares

How a Single NFT Becomes Thousands of ERC-20 Tokens

Fractionalisation is the process of locking an NFT in a smart contract and issuing a corresponding supply of fungible ERC-20 tokens, each representing a fraction of the ownership of that NFT. These ERC-20 tokens can be traded on decentralised exchanges, held in wallets, and used in DeFi protocols - just like any other token.

Fractionalisation solves several problems simultaneously:

1. Price discovery: A market of many buyers and sellers determines the implied price of the NFT through the token price.

2. Liquidity: Instead of selling the entire NFT, an owner can sell a portion of the tokens, unlocking partial value.

3. Accessibility: Small investors can gain exposure to blue-chip NFTs without needing millions of rupees.

4. Composability: Fractionalised tokens can be used as collateral in other DeFi protocols, staked for yield, or bundled into index funds.

Fractionalisation transforms an NFT into a liquid, divisible asset that can be traded 24/7.

Below is a complete fractionalisation vault contract built with OpenZeppelin.

// SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT
pragma solidity ^0.8.20;

import "@openzeppelin/contracts/token/ERC20/ERC20.sol";
import "@openzeppelin/contracts/token/ERC721/IERC721Receiver.sol";

contract Fractionalise is ERC20, IERC721Receiver {
    address public immutable nftContract;
    uint256 public immutable tokenId;
    uint256 public immutable totalShares;

    address public buyoutInitiator;
    uint256 public buyoutPrice;
    uint256 public buyoutDeadline;

    event BuyoutInitiated(address indexed initiator, uint256 price, uint256 deadline);
    event BuyoutCompleted(address indexed newOwner, uint256 tokenId);

    constructor(address _nftContract, uint256 _tokenId, uint256 _totalShares, string memory name, string memory symbol) ERC20(name, symbol) {
        nftContract = _nftContract;
        tokenId = _tokenId;
        totalShares = _totalShares;
        _mint(msg.sender, _totalShares);
    }

    // Enable the contract to receive the NFT
    function onERC721Received(address, address, uint256, bytes calldata) external pure override returns (bytes4) {
        return IERC721Receiver.onERC721Received.selector;
    }

    // --- Buyout logic ---

    function initiateBuyout(uint256 price) external {
        require(buyoutInitiator == address(0), "Buyout already in progress");
        require(price > 0, "Price must be positive");
        buyoutInitiator = msg.sender;
        buyoutPrice = price;
        buyoutDeadline = block.timestamp + 7 days;
        emit BuyoutInitiated(msg.sender, price, buyoutDeadline);
    }

    function acceptBuyout() external {
        require(buyoutInitiator != address(0), "No buyout in progress");
        require(block.timestamp <= buyoutDeadline, "Buyout expired");
        uint256 shareValue = buyoutPrice / totalShares;
        uint256 holderShares = balanceOf(msg.sender);
        require(holderShares > 0, "No shares");
        uint256 payout = holderShares * shareValue;
        _burn(msg.sender, holderShares);
        payable(msg.sender).transfer(payout);
    }

    function finaliseBuyout() external {
        require(buyoutInitiator == msg.sender, "Only initiator");
        require(block.timestamp > buyoutDeadline, "Deadline not reached");
        require(totalSupply() == 0, "Not all shares surrendered");
        IERC721(nftContract).transferFrom(address(this), buyoutInitiator, tokenId);
        emit BuyoutCompleted(buyoutInitiator, tokenId);
    }
}

How the buyout works: A holder of a significant portion of tokens can initiate a buyout by offering a price. Other holders have 7 days to accept and burn their shares in exchange for ETH. If all shares are surrendered, the initiator can claim the NFT. This mechanism ensures that the NFT can be "re-unified" when someone accumulates enough shares.

06   NFT Index Tokens - A Basket of NFTs

Investing in a Collection Without Picking Winners

Beyond fractionalising a single NFT, the same concept can be applied to a basket of NFTs. A protocol can lock multiple NFTs - for example, 10 CryptoPunks, 5 Bored Apes, and 3 Art Blocks - and issue a single index token that represents a share of the entire collection. This is similar to an ETF in traditional finance.

Index tokens provide diversification. An investor who believes in the long-term value of a category (e.g., "blue-chip NFTs") can buy the index token without needing to research individual pieces. The token price reflects the aggregate floor price and demand for the entire basket. Platforms like NFTX and Floor DAO have popularised this model.

On MST, the low gas fees make rebalancing these baskets feasible. A smart contract can periodically accept new NFTs into the vault and mint additional tokens, or allow redemption of a specific NFT by burning a sufficient amount of the index token.

07   NFTFi on MST Blockchain - The Low-Fee Advantage

Why MST Is the Perfect Home for NFT Finance

NFT lending and fractionalisation are transaction-heavy. A single loan involves: NFT deposit, loan funding, interest accrual, repayment, and possibly default claim. Fractionalisation involves: NFT deposit, token minting, token transfers, buyout initiation, share surrender, and NFT withdrawal. On Ethereum, each of these steps could cost ₹500-₹5,000 in gas, making small loans or fractional shares uneconomical.

On MST, these same steps cost a fraction of a paisa. This opens up entirely new use cases:

Micro-loans: Borrow ₹5,000 against a gaming NFT, with interest of ₹50. The gas fees on Ethereum would exceed the interest; on MST, they are negligible.

Mass fractionalisation: Split a ₹50,000 NFT into 100,000 tokens worth ₹0.50 each. Users can buy a single token without losing value to gas.

Frequent rebalancing: An index fund can add or remove NFTs daily without worrying about gas costs.

MST's EVM compatibility ensures that the battle-tested smart contracts from NFTfi, BendDAO, and NFTX can be deployed on MST with minimal modifications, immediately benefiting from the lower fee environment.

08   Risks and Considerations

What Can Go Wrong and How to Mitigate It

1. Oracle risk: NFT lending relies on accurate valuation. If the oracle providing the price feed is manipulated, a lender might issue a loan far above the true value and end up with a worthless NFT. Use multiple oracles and time-weighted average prices (TWAP).

2. Liquidity risk: In a fractionalisation vault, if nobody wants to buy the tokens, the NFT is essentially locked forever. This is mitigated by the buyout mechanism that allows a single large holder to claim the whole NFT.

3. Smart contract risk: The vault contract holds the NFT. If the contract has a bug, the NFT could be stolen. Always use audited code (OpenZeppelin) and consider professional audits.

4. Regulatory risk: Fractionalised tokens could be considered securities in some jurisdictions. Consult legal counsel before launching a public offering.

10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 12.3

NFTFi
The ecosystem of decentralised finance applications built around NFTs, including lending, borrowing, fractionalisation, and derivatives.
LTV (Loan-to-Value)
The ratio of the loan amount to the appraised value of the collateral. A lower LTV protects lenders from price volatility.
Fractionalisation
The process of locking an NFT in a vault and issuing ERC-20 tokens representing proportional ownership shares.
Buyout
A mechanism that allows a shareholder to purchase the underlying NFT by paying off all other token holders.
NFT Index Token
A single ERC-20 token that represents a share of a basket of multiple NFTs, similar to an ETF.
Default
In NFT lending, the failure of a borrower to repay the loan by the deadline, resulting in the lender claiming the collateral.
Oracle
A service that provides external data (such as NFT prices) to smart contracts. Reliable oracles are critical for NFT lending.
P2P Lending
Peer-to-peer lending where individual lenders and borrowers are matched directly, as opposed to pool-based lending.
