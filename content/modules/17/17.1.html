The Day India's UPI Forgot Who It Trusted

📖 Story

It is a Tuesday morning in 2032. Riya opens her phone to pay the electricity bill - ₹2,340 - via Google Pay. Her UPI app signs the transaction with a private key that was generated years ago, secured by elliptic curve cryptography (ECC). Until now, ECC was considered ironclad, trusted by every bank, every payment app, every Aadhaar authentication.


But overnight, a commercially viable 10,000-qubit quantum computer - built in a lab no one saw coming - begins to run Shor's algorithm against the secp256k1 curve that secures billions of UPI handles. By 10 a.m., the first fraudulent transaction succeeds: ₹10,000,000 is moved from a dormant account, signed with a forged digital signature that passes every verification check. The quantum machine didn't need the original key; it reconstructed the private key from the public key in under 15 seconds.


By noon, the entire UPI infrastructure grinds to a halt. Every transaction is suspect because any address's private key can now be derived. The Reserve Bank of India freezes all digital payments. Aadhaar e-sign, which also relies on ECDSA, becomes untrustworthy overnight. Property records, court orders, university degrees - all the cryptographic proofs we built society on - suddenly hold the weight of wet tissue paper.


This is not science fiction. It is a timeline that every cybersecurity agency on Earth has mapped. The question isn't if a quantum computer capable of breaking ECDSA will exist - it's when. And when it does, any blockchain, any payment system, any identity network that hasn't migrated to post-quantum cryptography will be instantly, irrevocably compromised.

03   Background & History

From Feynman's Idea to NIST's Finalists - The Race Against the Quantum Clock

1982
Richard Feynman proposes a quantum computer. He argues that simulating quantum physics requires a machine that itself operates on quantum principles. The seed is planted.
1994
Peter Shor publishes his algorithm. He proves that a sufficiently large quantum computer can factor large integers and compute discrete logarithms in polynomial time - the exact mathematical problems that secure RSA, ECDSA, and virtually all of today's digital signatures. Overnight, every public-key cryptosystem in use becomes theoretically doomed.
1996
Lov Grover introduces Grover's algorithm. It provides a quadratic speedup for searching unsorted databases. For cryptography, this means a 256-bit hash provides only 128 bits of security against a quantum adversary. Symmetric cryptography survives - but needs longer keys.
2016
NIST launches the Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardisation Process. Recognizing the threat, the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology calls for public-key algorithms that resist both classical and quantum attacks. 82 candidates are submitted from 25 countries, including submissions from Indian cryptographers.
2022 - 2024
NIST selects the first group of quantum-resistant algorithms. After years of intense cryptanalysis, NIST announces four algorithms: CRYSTALS-Kyber (key encapsulation), CRYSTALS-Dilithium, FALCON, and SPHINCS+ (digital signatures). These become the global blueprint for post-quantum security.
Now
MST Blockchain integrates NIST PQC into its core. The network's quantum-ready pillar is designed to transition consensus-critical signatures to Dilithium/Falcon while maintaining backward compatibility through hybrid signatures during the migration window.
04   Beginner-Friendly Explanation

Why Would a Quantum Computer Break Your Wallet?

Imagine your private key is a needle hidden in a haystack the size of the entire universe. A classical computer searches for that needle by picking up one straw at a time - it will never finish. A quantum computer, running Shor's algorithm, is like having a magnet that only attracts the needle. It doesn't search; it steers the quantum state toward the answer, collapsing the haystack instantly into the solution.

🧲 The Magnet Analogy

Classical cryptography assumes certain math problems (factoring, discrete log) are so hard that even the world's fastest supercomputer would take billions of years. Quantum computing changes the rules of the game entirely - the same problems become trivially easy, like pulling a metal needle from a haystack with a giant magnet. That's why we need new "non-magnetic" needles - post-quantum algorithms based on problems that even quantum magnets can't pull out.

W
WHY
Why is quantum computing a threat to blockchain?
All major blockchains today use ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) for transaction signatures. ECDSA's security rests on the hardness of the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP). Shor's algorithm solves ECDLP in polynomial time, meaning a quantum computer can derive a private key from a public key - and therefore forge any transaction - once it reaches sufficient scale.
W
WHAT
What exactly does Shor's algorithm break?
Any public-key cryptosystem based on integer factorisation (RSA) or discrete logarithms over finite fields or elliptic curves. That includes RSA, DSA, ECDSA, EdDSA, Diffie-Hellman - essentially all digital signatures and key exchanges used on the internet today. Symmetric ciphers (AES) and hash functions (SHA) are affected by Grover's algorithm but remain secure with sufficiently large key sizes.
H
HOW
How does NIST's post-quantum cryptography work?
Instead of relying on factoring or discrete logs, PQC algorithms are built on mathematical problems that are believed to be hard even for quantum computers: lattice-based problems (Learning With Errors), hash-based signatures, code-based cryptography, and multivariate equations. NIST selected lattice-based schemes (Kyber, Dilithium, Falcon) as primary standards because of their balance of security, key size, and performance.
W
WHERE
Where is this threat most urgent in India?
UPI (1.3 billion transactions/day) uses ECDSA for device binding and merchant authentication. Aadhaar e-sign uses ECDSA. Blockchain-based land records (Andhra Pradesh pilot) use ECDSA. All could be retroactively compromised if quantum arrives before migration. The National Cyber Security Coordinator has warned that India must adopt PQC by 2027.
L
LIMITS
What are the current limitations of quantum computers?
Today's quantum machines are noisy, error-prone, and have fewer than 1,000 logical qubits. To break ECDSA-256 you'd need about 1,500 - 2,000 logical qubits with low error rates - still years away but advancing rapidly. However, data recorded today can be stored and decrypted later ("harvest now, decrypt later"), so the threat is immediate for long-lived secrets.
05   Technical Deep Dive

Shor's Algorithm, Grover's Algorithm & the NIST PQC Suite

To fully grasp the quantum threat, you need to understand the exact mathematical attack that quantum computers enable, and the exact mathematical shields that NIST has certified to stop them.

1
Shor's Algorithm - The Private Key Derivation Weapon
Problem solved: integer factorisation and discrete logarithms (including ECDLP). Complexity: O((log N)³) - polynomial, versus exponential for classical. Impact on blockchain: Given any public key (which is a point on an elliptic curve), Shor's algorithm can compute the private key (the scalar). This means any unspent output whose public key is visible on-chain can be stolen. Bitcoin and Ethereum addresses that have never been spent from are partially shielded (hash of public key), but once a transaction is signed, the public key is revealed. MST's quantum-ready pillar addresses this by allowing users to transition to post-quantum addresses before a quantum attack becomes viable.
2
Grover's Algorithm - Halving the Security of Hashes
Effect: a quantum computer can search an unsorted set of size N in O(√N) time. For a hash function with n-bit output, classical brute-force takes 2ⁿ operations; Grover's takes 2^(n/2). Therefore, SHA-256 provides only 128-bit security against a quantum adversary - still strong, but it means that if a protocol relies on 128-bit security, it must move to SHA-512 or a post-quantum hash (like SHA-3 with larger output). MST's consensus, which uses Keccak-256 for block hashing, remains secure at 128-bit post-quantum level, but MST has the option to double the hash length in a future soft fork.
3
CRYSTALS-Kyber - Post-Quantum Key Encapsulation
Kyber is a lattice-based IND-CCA2-secure KEM. Its security relies on the Module Learning With Errors (Module-LWE) problem. NIST selected Kyber as the standard for key establishment. In MST's ecosystem, Kyber can be used to securely share session keys between a user's SARAL wallet and the network, even in the presence of a quantum eavesdropper.
4
CRYSTALS-Dilithium & Falcon - Post-Quantum Digital Signatures
Both are lattice-based signature schemes. Dilithium offers large public keys (≈2.5 KB) and moderate signatures (≈2.5 KB) with fast verification - ideal for validators. Falcon has smaller signatures (≈0.7 KB) but requires more complex signing - suitable for lightweight wallets. MST's transition plan allows a hybrid mode where a transaction carries both an ECDSA signature and a Dilithium/Falcon signature during the migration window, after which ECDSA is phased out.
5
SPHINCS+ - Hash-Based Stateless Signature
SPHINCS+ is a stateless hash-based signature scheme, built entirely on the security of hash functions. It produces large signatures (≈17 KB) but has the advantage of relying on minimal security assumptions - no lattice hardness, just the pre-image resistance of SHA-3. MST may use SPHINCS+ as a fallback for specific high-security identity contracts.
🗝️ Critical Insight: "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later"

Even if a cryptographically relevant quantum computer is a decade away, any encrypted or signed data captured today can be stored and decrypted or forged once the quantum machine arrives. For blockchain, this means that all on-chain ECDSA signatures ever recorded are already being collected by state-level adversaries. MST's solution is to enable users to voluntarily migrate their assets to post-quantum addresses now, using a built-in migration contract, so that by the time the quantum machine boots up, the funds are already in lattice-secured addresses.

08   MST's Quantum-Ready Pillar

How MST Was Designed to Survive the Y2Q Era

MST's Quantum-Ready Pillar is not a future plan - it is an architectural framework already embedded in the protocol's design. It ensures that when the quantum break becomes real, MST can transition without network downtime, without forcing users to manually migrate funds, and without a chaotic hard fork.
1
Hybrid Signature Scheme - Dual Validity Period
From a predefined block height (set by governance), all transactions must carry both an ECDSA and a NIST PQC signature. Validators enforce both. This allows users who have not yet migrated to post-quantum keys to continue transacting while the network's security gradually shifts. After a migration window of ~6 months, the ECDSA requirement is dropped entirely. This mechanism is implemented as a soft fork - no new chain, no disruption.
2
SARAL-Linked Post-Quantum Identity
When a user creates a wallet via SARAL, the secure enclave generates a Dilithium keypair in addition to the ECDSA keypair. This post-quantum key is registered on-chain through a low-cost identity contract. The user never sees the key material. In the event of an emergency quantum break, the identity contract can authorise a one-time asset migration from the ECDSA address to a new fully post-quantum address, all signed by the already-secure Dilithium key - no human intervention needed.
3
Consensus-Layer PQC Upgrade (Trika POSA)
Validators currently sign block proposals and votes with ECDSA. MST's upgrade path replaces these with Dilithium or Falcon signatures. Because validator sets rotate, the transition can be coordinated through the governance DAO: validators submit new PQC public keys during a registration window, and once the transition height is reached, only PQC-signed blocks are accepted. The Trika POSA finality gadget remains unchanged; only the signature algorithm evolves.
4
WASMify & ZK-Proofs - Post-Quantum Friendly
MST's WASMify infrastructure already supports zero-knowledge proofs based on STARKs, which are inherently quantum-resistant (they rely on collision-resistant hash functions, not number-theoretic assumptions). As the ZK landscape matures, all on-chain privacy and scaling solutions will default to post-quantum STARKs, ensuring that private transactions remain confidential against quantum adversaries.
💡 Why Lattice-Based Cryptography?

MST chose to align with NIST's lattice-based standards (Dilithium/Falcon) because lattices provide strong security reductions to worst-case lattice problems that have remained hard for decades, and because they support relatively compact keys and signatures. The Learning With Errors (LWE) problem is conjectured to be hard even for quantum computers, making it a conservative choice for a blockchain that may host multi-billion-dollar asset pools.

Key Terms - Sub-Module 17.1

Shor's Algorithm
A quantum algorithm that solves integer factorisation and discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time, breaking RSA and ECDSA.
Grover's Algorithm
A quantum search algorithm that provides a quadratic speedup, reducing the effective security of a hash function from n bits to n/2 bits.
NIST PQC
The U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology's project to standardise post-quantum cryptographic algorithms (Kyber, Dilithium, Falcon, SPHINCS+).
Lattice-Based Cryptography
Cryptographic schemes whose security is based on the hardness of lattice problems like Learning With Errors (LWE), believed to resist quantum attacks.
CRYSTALS-Kyber
A lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism (KEM) selected by NIST for post-quantum key establishment.
CRYSTALS-Dilithium
A lattice-based digital signature scheme selected by NIST, offering fast verification and moderate key sizes.
Falcon
A lattice-based signature scheme with compact signatures, selected by NIST for applications where bandwidth is critical.
SPHINCS+
A stateless hash-based signature scheme standardised by NIST, relying solely on hash function security.
Hybrid Signature
A transaction that carries both a classical (ECDSA) and a post-quantum signature, enforced during a migration window to ensure security continuity.
Harvest Now, Decrypt Later
A threat model where encrypted or signed data is stored today for future decryption or forgery once quantum computers become capable.
STARK
A zero-knowledge proof system based on hash functions, inherently quantum-resistant unlike SNARKs that rely on elliptic curve pairings.
SARAL Dual-Key Architecture
MST's wallet design that generates an ECDSA and a Dilithium keypair at onboarding, enabling seamless future migration.
