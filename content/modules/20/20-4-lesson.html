02   Real-World Introduction Story

How a Discord “Helper” Became a Core Developer at a DeFi Protocol

📖 Story

In early 2023, Aditya - a computer science student from Nashik with no prior blockchain work experience - joined the MST Developer Discord. He didn't know anyone. He didn't have a job. He just wanted to learn.


Instead of lurking, Aditya started answering basic questions in the #solidity-help channel. “How do I fix this Hardhat compilation error?” He'd post the solution. “What's the difference between `memory` and `storage`?” He'd write a short explanation. He wasn't the smartest person in the server - but he was the most consistent. Within 3 months, he had answered over 200 questions. The community moderators noticed and gave him a “Community Helper” badge.


That badge caught the attention of a DeFi protocol building on MST. Their DevRel lead DMed Aditya: “We've seen your answers - you clearly understand the protocol. Are you open to a part-time developer advocate role?” Aditya interviewed, demonstrated his knowledge (which he had built entirely by helping others), and got the job. Six months later, he was promoted to a full-time Smart Contract Engineer. He never applied. The job came to him.

This is the power of community-first networking in Web3. Unlike traditional industries where networking means awkward LinkedIn messages and cold emails, Web3 networking is about public proof of competence. Every helpful answer, every open-source pull request, every insightful tweet becomes a piece of your resume that the right people will see.

03   Background & History

How Web3 Communities Replaced the Resume

2008 - 2013
The cypherpunk mailing list era. Early Bitcoin developers communicated via email lists and IRC channels. Reputation was built through technical contributions to Bitcoin Core. Satoshi himself was just an email address. Jobs didn't exist - you were either a contributor or you weren't.
2015 - 2017
Ethereum's Reddit and Gitter genesis. The Ethereum community coalesced on r/ethereum and Gitter. Vitalik Buterin was active in discussions. Early Solidity devs built their reputation by answering questions and sharing code snippets. The first “DevRel” hires were simply the most helpful community members.
2018 - 2020
Discord becomes the blockchain town square. Every protocol launched a Discord server. The role of “Community Manager” was born. Hackathons moved online (ETHGlobal, Gitcoin). Twitter/X became the public forum for thought leadership. Getting a job often started with a DM after a hackathon win.
2021 - Present
The fully on-chain portfolio network. Today, a developer's “network” includes their GitHub contribution graph, their Lens profile, their Dune dashboard views, and their Discord role badges. Hiring managers scan these signals before ever seeing a CV. The community is the first-round interview.

🗝️ The Golden Rule of Web3 Networking

Don't ask for a job. Become the person that jobs ask for. When you consistently provide value - answering questions, writing documentation, reporting bugs - opportunities naturally flow toward you. The moment you ask “any jobs?” in a Discord server, you become just another job seeker. The moment you answer someone's technical question, you become an expert.

05   Technical Deep Dive

The 5 Platforms That Matter - And How to Use Each One

Your networking strategy must span five distinct platforms. Each serves a different purpose, and neglecting any one weakens your overall presence.

1
Discord - The Real-Time Reputation Engine
Why it matters: Most blockchain protocols (including MST) use Discord as their primary community hub. Hiring managers and core developers are active in these servers.
Best practices: Join the MST Developer Discord. Complete the onboarding. Set your nickname to “YourName | Solidity Dev”. Find the #help or #solidity channels. Answer 2-3 questions per day - even simple ones. Over time, request a “Helper” or “Contributor” role.
Pitfall: Never DM people you don't know asking for a job. Always contribute publicly first. Let the DMs come to you.
MST-specific: The MST Discord has dedicated channels for #dev-general, #solidity, #wasmify, and #jobs. The #jobs channel is where MST ecosystem projects post openings - but you need at least a “Community Member” role to access it.
2
Twitter/X - The Public Portfolio
Why it matters: Twitter is where Web3 founders, VCs, and senior engineers share ideas and hire people. A single thread can go viral and reach 100,000+ people.
Best practices: Optimise your bio: “Solidity Dev | Building on MST | Prev: deployed staking pool on testnet.” Tweet 2-3 times per week: share a code snippet, a lesson learned, a review of a new EIP. Write one technical thread per month. Engage with MST's official account and key ecosystem figures.
Content ideas: “Today I gas-optimised a loop from 120k to 40k - here's how.” “Just deployed my first ERC-721 on MST Testnet - what I wish I knew.” “Why SARAL is the biggest unlock for mobile Web3.”
Pitfall: Don't only tweet “gm” and price predictions. Provide technical value. Recruiters scroll through your timeline - they want to see your engineering mind, not your market opinions.
3
GitHub - The Proof of Work
Why it matters: Your GitHub profile is the most visited page after your CV. It's where your code lives, and where recruiters verify your skills.
Best practices (extending 20.2): Contribute to open-source MST ecosystem projects: fix a typo, add a test, improve documentation. Submit PRs. Review others' PRs. Star repos you admire. Follow key MST developers - their activity appears in your feed, keeping you visible.
Networking hack: When you submit a PR to an MST ecosystem project, the maintainer will review it. That review interaction is a networking touchpoint. If your PR is high quality, the maintainer will remember your name. Later, when their protocol is hiring, your GitHub username will appear in their “recruit from contributors” search.
4
Developer Forums - The Searchable Knowledge Base
Why it matters: Forums like the MST Dev Forum, Ethereum StackExchange, and Reddit's r/ethdev are indexed by Google. A well-written answer on a forum can be read by thousands of developers over years.
Best practices: Register on the MST Dev Forum (forum.mstblockchain.com). Set up your profile with your GitHub and Twitter links. Answer questions in the “Smart Contracts” and “Tooling” categories. Write at least one detailed “How-to” guide per quarter.
Long-term value: Forum answers compound. A single post from 2023 can still be sending traffic to your profile in 2026. Recruiters searching for “MST staking contract example” will find your tutorial, see your name, and click through to your GitHub.
5
Hackathons & Events - The Accelerated Relationship Builder
Why it matters: A 48-hour hackathon builds relationships faster than 6 months of online interaction. You work side-by-side with other developers, sponsors meet you face-to-face (or screen-to-screen), and winning a prize adds a powerful credential.
Best practices: Participate in MST-sponsored hackathons (check MST Dev Forum or Discord for announcements). Join ETHIndia (the largest Indian Web3 hackathon). Even if you don't win, submit a project - you'll get feedback from judges who are often hiring managers. Network in the Discord channels during the event. Follow up with a thank-you tweet tagging the organisers.
In-person events: Attend local Web3 meetups in Bangalore, Delhi, Mumbai, or your nearest city. Meetups are smaller, less intimidating, and excellent for 1-on-1 conversations. Bring a demo of your project on your phone - showing code is infinitely better than handing out business cards.
W
WHAT
What is the single most effective networking activity for a junior developer?
Consistently answering questions in a protocol's Discord server. It builds trust with the exact people who later hire for that ecosystem. It's public, permanent, and demonstrates both technical skill and communication ability.
W
WHY
Why is cold-applying to jobs less effective in Web3?
Because Web3 hiring managers are overwhelmed with applications. They filter by signals: “Has this person contributed to our ecosystem? Have I seen their name before?” A referred candidate (or a recognised community member) skips the resume pile entirely.
H
HOW
How do I start networking if I know nobody?
Pick one protocol (MST). Join its Discord. Read the last 50 messages in #help. Find a question you can answer. Write a helpful reply. Do this 3 times. You are now a contributing member. Repeat for 30 days. You now have a reputation.
L
LIMITS
What should I avoid doing?
Never send unsolicited DMs asking for jobs or mentorship. Never spam your project link in every channel. Never engage in toxic arguments. Reputation takes months to build and seconds to destroy.

09   Assessment Preparation

Top Principles - Know These for the MCQ

1
The 80/20 Rule of Web3 Hiring
80% of jobs are filled through referrals and community reputation, not public job postings. Networking is not optional - it's the primary job search channel.
2
Contribution Before Ask
Never ask for a job, mentorship, or favour in a public channel before you have contributed value. Your first 50 messages should all be answers, tutorials, or code - not requests.
3
Discord Helper → Referral
The most reliable path to a Web3 job without experience: join a protocol Discord, answer questions consistently for 3-6 months, earn a role, then get referred by the DevRel team.
4
Twitter is Your Public Diary
Tweet your learning journey, not just successes. Recruiters value authenticity and growth trajectory. A developer who tweets “Today I learned about reentrancy” is more hireable than one who only tweets “I'm an expert.”
5
Hackathons Are Accelerated Networking
One hackathon weekend can build relationships equivalent to 3 months of online presence. Always submit a project, even if incomplete. The judges' feedback is a direct line to industry professionals.
6
MST Grant Programme Is a Career Accelerator
Winning an MST grant (initGrant, buildGrant, scaleGrant) gives you funding, mentorship, and instant ecosystem credibility. Grant recipients are heavily recruited by MST ecosystem projects.
7
Consistency Over Virality
One viral tweet is less valuable than 100 consistent, helpful interactions. Reputation is built on reliability, not spikes. The developer who answers one question every day for a year is far more trusted than the one who had a single tweet blow up.
10   Glossary

Key Terms - Sub-Module 20.4

Community Reputation
The trust and recognition a developer earns through consistent, helpful participation in public forums, Discord servers, and open-source projects.
DevRel (Developer Relations)
A team within a protocol that bridges the gap between core developers and the broader ecosystem. They often identify and refer active community members for jobs.
80/20 Rule of Web3 Hiring
The observation that approximately 80% of Web3 jobs are filled through referrals and community networks, not through public job boards.
PR (Pull Request)
A proposed change to a codebase on GitHub. Submitting quality PRs is a primary way to build technical reputation in open-source ecosystems.
Hackathon
A time-bound event (typically 24-72 hours) where developers build projects. In Web3, hackathons are primary networking and hiring venues.
MST Grant Program
A funding initiative (initGrant, buildGrant, scaleGrant) that provides capital, mentorship, and ecosystem integration to builders on MST. Grant recipients gain significant career visibility.
